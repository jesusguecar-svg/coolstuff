# **Texas Insurance Research Report: Policy Riders, Provisions, Options, and Exclusions**

## **SECTION 1: DOMAIN SNAPSHOT**

The mechanics of a life insurance contract are governed by the interplay of riders, provisions, and options, collectively representing one of the most intellectually rigorous portions of the Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent examination. This domain, identified as Domain II, carries a 15% weight and serves as the operational heart of the policy.1 While other domains address the broader legal theories of insurance or the specific types of policies available, Domain II focuses on the "moving parts" of the contract—the clauses that protect the insurer from adverse selection, the riders that allow a policyholder to customize their coverage, and the statutory provisions mandated by the Texas Department of Insurance to ensure consumer protection.2  
From an analyst's perspective, this domain is heavily tested because it directly relates to the agent's duty to explain a policy's limitations and benefits during the delivery process. Understanding how a policy owner exercises their rights—such as choosing a settlement option or assigning a policy as collateral for a loan—is central to the professional competency of a Texas agent.1 The subtopics carrying the highest yield include beneficiary designations, specifically the 120-hour survival rule mandated by the Texas Estates Code, and the intricate differences between nonforfeiture and settlement options.5  
Exam day confusion frequently arises from the semantic similarities between concepts. Candidates often struggle to distinguish between the waiver of premium and the waiver of monthly deduction, particularly regarding how they apply to different policy types like Whole Life versus Universal Life.7 Furthermore, the distinction between dividend options and nonforfeiture options remains a consistent hurdle, as both involve the application of "extra" value within a permanent policy but originate from entirely different sources: company surplus versus policy equity.9 This report provides the deep technical research required to navigate these nuances and avoid the common traps set by test writers.

## **SECTION 2: DEEP EXPLANATION BY SUBTOPIC**

## **A. Policy Riders**

#### **1\. Waiver of Premium and Waiver of Monthly Deduction**

The waiver of premium rider is a foundational disability benefit that ensures a life insurance policy remains in force if the insured suffers a total disability. In plain English, the insurance company agrees to pay the policy’s premiums for the insured during the period of disability.11 This rider exists to prevent policy lapse at a time when the insured’s earning capacity is most compromised. In a traditional life policy, once the insured has been totally disabled for a specific waiting period—typically six continuous months in Texas—the insurer waives all future premiums and refunds those paid during the waiting period.12 The benefit continues until the insured is no longer disabled or reaches a specific age, usually 60 or 65\.  
A distinct variation exists for flexible premium policies like Universal Life: the waiver of monthly deduction. Unlike the waiver of premium, which covers the entire planned premium, the waiver of monthly deduction only covers the actual costs of insurance (mortality charges) and administrative fees required to keep the policy from lapsing.7 This is a critical distinction for the exam; it means the policy stays active, but the cash value may not grow at the rate it would have if the full premium were being paid.8 The policyowner benefits from continued death benefit protection, but the "savings" element is less protected than with a standard waiver of premium. Tax treatment for these benefits is generally favorable, as the "income" represented by the waived premium is not considered taxable income to the policyowner by the IRS.9

#### **2\. Guaranteed Insurability**

The guaranteed insurability rider (GIR) is a strategic addition that grants the policyowner the right to purchase additional amounts of life insurance at specific future dates or following major life events without proving insurability.14 This rider exists to protect against the risk that the insured might become uninsurable due to health changes before they have fulfilled their long-term coverage needs. In a life policy, the owner is typically given options at ages such as 25, 28, 31, 34, 37, and 40, or upon marriage and the birth of a child.14  
The primary beneficiary of this rider is a young insured whose income and family responsibilities are expected to grow. When an option date arrives, the insured can buy more coverage at their attained age rate without a medical exam. If they miss an option date, that specific opportunity is lost, but future dates remain available. The insurer is limited because they cannot deny coverage based on health, even if the insured has developed a terminal condition. A common exam trap involves the premium; while the *right* to buy is guaranteed, the *premium* for the new coverage will be higher because it is based on the older age of the insured at the time of the increase.14

#### **3\. Payor Benefit**

The payor benefit rider is a specialized form of premium waiver found almost exclusively in juvenile insurance policies. It functions to protect the child’s coverage if the adult payor (usually a parent or guardian) dies or becomes totally disabled before the child reaches a certain age, such as 21 or 25\.15 This ensures the policy remains in force during the child's minority. The benefit is the waiver of premiums until the specified age is reached, at which point the child assumes responsibility for the payments. It creates a temporary right for the policy to remain active without funding, benefiting the minor insured who would otherwise lose coverage due to the death of the provider.

#### **4\. Accidental Death and Accidental Death and Dismemberment**

The accidental death rider, often marketed as "double indemnity," pays an additional benefit—usually equal to the face amount—if the insured's death is the direct result of an accident.17 For the benefit to trigger, death must occur within a specific timeframe after the accident, typically 90 days, to ensure the accident was the proximate cause. Accidental death and dismemberment (AD\&D) expands this by paying a "principal sum" for accidental death or the loss of two limbs/eyesight, and a "capital sum" (usually 50%) for the loss of one limb or sight in one eye.16  
These riders do not cover deaths resulting from illness, self-inflicted injury, or war.18 They exist to provide low-cost additional protection for unexpected tragedies. The beneficiary receives a significantly larger payout, but only if the strict definition of "accidental" is met. Exam traps often focus on the cause of death; if an insured has a heart attack while driving and then crashes, the death is typically ruled "natural" (heart attack) rather than "accidental," and the rider does not pay.20

#### **5\. Term Riders**

Term riders allow a policyowner to attach a temporary term insurance policy to a permanent base policy. This provides a cost-effective way to increase total death benefit protection for a limited time, such as during the years of a mortgage or while children are in school.14 The rider works by adding a level or decreasing term component to the whole life policy. The premium for the rider is added to the base policy premium. If the insured dies during the rider's term, the beneficiary receives both the base death benefit and the rider benefit. If the insured survives the term, the rider expires, and the death benefit reverts to the base amount.

#### **6\. Other Insureds**

Other insured riders extend coverage to family members of the primary insured. The most common is the spouse term rider and the children’s term rider. The children’s rider is particularly notable as it usually covers all of the insured’s children—including those born or adopted after the policy is issued—for a single, flat premium.21 This exists to provide burial coverage and to guarantee the future insurability of the children. Most children’s riders allow for conversion to a permanent policy at age 18 or 21 without a medical exam.14

#### **7\. Long Term Care**

The long-term care (LTC) rider allows the policyowner to accelerate a portion of the death benefit to pay for qualified long-term care expenses, such as nursing home or home health care costs.14 This exists as an alternative to standalone LTC insurance. It works by reducing the eventual death benefit dollar-for-dollar by the amount of benefits paid out during the insured's lifetime. In Texas, agents must meet specific continuing education requirements if they sell policies with LTC riders that require personal care services.24 The benefit is generally received tax-free if the insured is chronically ill as defined by HIPAA standards.9

#### **8\. Return of Premium**

The return of premium (ROP) rider, typically attached to term life insurance, provides that if the insured is alive at the end of the term, the insurer will refund 100% of the premiums paid into the policy.25 It exists to overcome the "sunk cost" objection to term insurance. ROP policies are significantly more expensive than standard term—often 25-50% higher.26 The refund is generally tax-free because the IRS considers it a return of the policyowner's cost basis.25 However, if the policy is cancelled early, the owner may receive only a small portion or none of the premiums back.25

#### **9\. Disability Income Rider**

The disability income rider provides a monthly income stream to the insured if they become totally disabled, as defined by the contract. Unlike the waiver of premium, which merely pays the policy’s cost, this rider provides cash for the insured's living expenses. The benefit is typically calculated as a percentage of the face amount, such as $10 per month for every $1,000 of coverage. This rider is less common today due to the prevalence of standalone disability insurance, but it remains testable as a "living benefit" of a life policy.

#### **10\. Cost of Living**

The cost of living (COLA) rider automatically increases the policy's death benefit periodically to keep pace with inflation.14 The increases are usually tied to an index like the Consumer Price Index (CPI). As the death benefit increases, the insurer increases the premium accordingly.14 The policyowner does not need to provide evidence of insurability for these increases. This rider exists to ensure that the purchasing power of the death benefit remains stable over long periods.

## **B. Policy Provisions and Options**

#### **1\. Entire Contract**

The Entire Contract provision is a mandatory Texas law (TIC §1101.003) stating that the policy, including any attached papers and the application, constitutes the entire agreement between the insurer and the policyowner.2 It exists to prevent the insurance company from changing the contract or referring to external documents (like company bylaws) to deny a claim. It also prohibits the agent from verbally altering the contract.29 For the exam, remember that no statements made before the policy was issued can be used to contest a claim unless they are written in the application and attached to the policy.29

#### **2\. Insuring Clause**

The insuring clause is the insurer's fundamental promise to pay the death benefit to the beneficiary upon receiving due proof of the insured's death.30 It is usually found on the face page of the policy and identifies the parties to the contract, the amount of coverage, and the scope of the perils covered. It defines the "core" of the agreement.

#### **3\. Free Look**

The free look provision (or "Right to Examine") gives the policyowner a set number of days after policy delivery to review the contract.31 If they are unsatisfied for any reason, they can return the policy for a full refund of all premiums paid.30 In Texas, the standard period is 10 to 20 days, but for annuity contracts, it is 20 days (30 days if the contract is a replacement).33 The period begins only when the owner physically receives the policy, which is a common exam focus.31

#### **4\. Consideration**

The consideration provision identifies the value exchanged to make the contract legally binding. For the applicant, consideration is the initial premium and the statements made in the application.30 For the insurer, it is the promise to pay the death benefit. In Texas, premiums must be payable in advance (TIC §1101.004).2

#### **5\. Owner’s Rights**

The owner's rights provision clarifies that the policyowner (who may or may not be the insured) holds all the legal rights of the contract.4 These include the right to name/change beneficiaries, select settlement and dividend options, borrow from the cash value, and assign the policy to another party.4 These rights exist only during the insured's lifetime.

#### **6\. Beneficiary Designations**

Beneficiary designations determine who receives the proceeds upon the insured's death.

* **Primary and Contingent**: The primary beneficiary is the first in line. The contingent (or secondary) beneficiary only receives the proceeds if the primary beneficiary dies before the insured.36  
* **Revocable and Irrevocable**: The owner can change a revocable beneficiary at any time. An irrevocable beneficiary has a vested interest; the owner cannot change the beneficiary, borrow against the policy, or assign it without the irrevocable beneficiary's written consent.36  
* **Common Disaster**: Texas law (Estates Code §121.052) stipulates the 120-hour rule.5 If the insured and primary beneficiary die in a common accident and it cannot be determined who died first, or if the beneficiary dies within 120 hours of the insured, the beneficiary is legally presumed to have died *before* the insured.5 This ensures the proceeds go to the contingent beneficiary or the insured's estate rather than the primary beneficiary's estate.  
* **Minor Beneficiaries**: Insurers generally will not pay proceeds directly to a minor. A guardian or trustee must be appointed to receive the funds on the minor's behalf.36  
* **Designation by Class**: Instead of naming individuals (e.g., "John Doe"), the owner names a group (e.g., "all my children"). This automatically includes future children without requiring policy updates.21

#### **7\. Premium Payment**

This provision details the mechanics of funding the policy.

* **Modes**: The frequency of payments (annual, semi-annual, quarterly, monthly). The annual mode is always the least expensive as it avoids the "load" charges for administrative processing and lost interest.38  
* **Grace Period**: Texas law (TIC §1101.005) requires a grace period of at least 31 days (or one month) for all premiums after the first.2 The policy remains in force during this time. If the insured dies during the grace period, the insurer pays the death benefit minus the overdue premium.2  
* **Automatic Premium Loan (APL)**: This is an optional feature where the insurer automatically takes a loan from the cash value to pay an overdue premium at the end of the grace period to prevent a lapse.  
* **Level or Flexible**: Traditional whole life has level premiums; Universal Life features flexible premiums that the owner can adjust based on their current financial situation.4

#### **8\. Reinstatement**

The reinstatement provision (TIC §1101.156) allows a lapsed policy to be restored to its original status, usually within 3 to 5 years.29 The owner must provide evidence of insurability (a new medical exam), pay all back premiums plus interest, and repay any outstanding policy loans.29 This exists to allow a consumer to regain a policy with a lower "issue age" premium rather than buying a new one at their current, older age.

#### **9\. Policy Loans, Withdrawals, and Partial Surrenders**

Permanent life insurance policies allow the owner to access the cash value.

* **Policy Loans**: These are loans from the insurer secured by the policy's cash value. In Texas, the interest rate cannot exceed 10%.29 Loans are not taxable while the policy is in force, and any outstanding balance is deducted from the death benefit if the insured dies.40  
* **Withdrawals/Partial Surrenders**: Common in Universal Life, these are direct removals of cash value. They reduce the death benefit and are taxable to the extent they exceed the premiums paid (cost basis).41

#### **10\. Nonforfeiture Options**

Mandated by TIC Chapter 1105, these options protect the owner’s equity in a permanent policy if it lapses.10

1. **Cash Surrender**: The owner cancels the policy and takes the cash. All coverage ends.  
2. **Reduced Paid-Up**: The cash value is used to buy a smaller, permanent policy of the same type that is fully paid up to age 100\.  
3. **Extended Term**: The default option in Texas. The cash value is used to buy a term insurance policy for the *full* original face amount for as long a duration as the cash value can afford.10

#### **11\. Dividends and Dividend Options**

Participating policies (issued by mutual insurers) may pay dividends, which are essentially a return of excess premium.9 Dividends are not taxable, but the interest earned on them is.6

* **Cash**: Insurer sends a check.  
* **Reduction of Premium**: Applied toward the next premium.  
* **Accumulation at Interest**: Held by the insurer to earn interest (the interest is taxable).9  
* **Paid-Up Additions**: Used to buy small amounts of additional permanent insurance.14  
* **One-Year Term**: Used to buy a one-year term policy for the amount of the cash value.

#### **12\. Incontestability**

TIC §1101.006 mandates that after a policy has been in force for 2 years, the insurer cannot deny a claim due to misstatements in the application.2 It exists to provide certainty to beneficiaries. Exceptions include non-payment of premium or military service violations.2

#### **13\. Assignments**

Assignments involve the transfer of policy rights.15

* **Absolute Assignment**: Permanent transfer of all rights to a new owner.15  
* **Collateral Assignment**: Temporary transfer of partial rights to a creditor (e.g., a bank) as security for a debt.29

#### **14\. Suicide**

The suicide clause states that if the insured commits suicide within the first 2 years of the policy, the insurer will not pay the death benefit but will refund all premiums paid to the beneficiary.45 After 2 years, suicide is covered like any other cause of death.45

#### **15\. Misstatement of Age and Gender**

If the insured's age or gender is found to be incorrect, the insurer will adjust the death benefit to the amount that the premiums actually paid would have purchased at the correct age or gender (TIC §1101.008).2 This is *never* a reason to void the policy, even after the incontestability period.

#### **16\. Settlement Options**

These determine how the death benefit is paid out.6

* **Lump Sum**: Single cash payment (tax-free).6  
* **Interest Only**: Principal is held; interest is paid to the beneficiary (interest is taxable).6  
* **Fixed Period**: Paid over a set number of years.6  
* **Fixed Amount**: Paid in set dollar increments until exhausted.6  
* **Life Income**: Guaranteed payments for the beneficiary's entire life.6

#### **17\. Accelerated Death Benefits**

TIC Chapter 1111 allows for the prepayment of a portion of the death benefit if the insured is terminally ill (death expected within 24 months in Texas) or has a specified chronic illness.54 This exists to help pay for medical and end-of-life costs. Payments are typically tax-free.9

## **C. Policy Exclusions**

#### **1\. War**

The war exclusion protects insurers from the catastrophic risk of mass casualties during conflict. It may be a "status clause" (no coverage while in the military during war) or a "results clause" (no coverage only if death results directly from war).

#### **2\. Aviation**

This typically excludes coverage for non-commercial aviation, such as private pilots or crew members. It does *not* apply to fare-paying passengers on commercial flights.

#### **3\. Dangerous Occupation or Hobby**

Insurers may exclude coverage for deaths resulting from hazardous activities like skydiving, auto racing, or professional scuba diving. Often, the insurer will offer the coverage but charge a higher "rated" premium instead of excluding it.

## **SECTION 4: EXAM-FOCUSED TAKEAWAYS**

## **Major Subtopic: Beneficiaries and Common Disaster**

* **Absolutely Need to Know Cold**: The 120-hour rule (5 days) in Texas. If the beneficiary dies within 5 days of the insured, it’s like they died first.5  
* **Secondary**: Class designations (e.g., "all my children") and their administrative ease.  
* **Low Priority**: The specific legal history of the Uniform Simultaneous Death Act.  
* **Wrong-Answer Traps**: Test writers will say the proceeds go to the primary beneficiary's estate if they die 2 days after the insured. **Correct Answer**: Proceeds go to the contingent beneficiary or the insured's estate.  
* **Memory Hook**: "Five days to survive to thrive."

## **Major Subtopic: Nonforfeiture Options**

* **Absolutely Need to Know Cold**: Extended Term is the automatic default in Texas if the owner makes no choice.10  
* **Secondary**: Reduced Paid-Up insurance lasts the longest (to age 100\) but has the smallest death benefit.  
* **Low Priority**: Comparing specific mortality tables used in nonforfeiture calculations.  
* **Wrong-Answer Traps**: Confusing nonforfeiture options with dividend options. Remember: Nonforfeiture is your *own* money (equity); Dividends are the *company's* money (surplus).  
* **Memory Hook**: "REC the lapse" (Reduced Paid-Up, Extended Term, Cash).

## **Major Subtopic: Provisions**

* **Absolutely Need to Know Cold**: The incontestability period is 2 years. Misstatement of age is NOT a reason to contest; it only leads to a benefit adjustment.2  
* **Secondary**: The Free Look period starts only when the policy is *delivered*, not when it’s signed or issued.31  
* **Wrong-Answer Traps**: Suggesting an agent can change a policy provision if they get the owner's permission. Only a company officer can do this.29  
* **Memory Hook**: "Age is an adjustment, not a denial."

## **Major Subtopic: Riders**

* **Absolutely Need to Know Cold**: Waiver of Monthly Deduction (Universal Life) does *not* pay the full premium; it only covers expenses and mortality charges.7  
* **Secondary**: Guaranteed Insurability options are based on *attained age*, not original age.  
* **Wrong-Answer Traps**: AD\&D riders paying for death due to pneumonia. They only pay for accidental bodily injury.  
* **Memory Hook**: "Monthly Deduction \= Universal Life Minimal protection."

## **SECTION 5: OFFICIAL RULES AND AUTHORITIES**

1. **Texas Insurance Code (TIC) Chapter 1101 (Life Insurance Policy Provisions)**  
   * **Why it matters**: This is the primary statute governing mandatory life insurance clauses in Texas. It defines the grace period (1101.005), incontestability (1101.006), and entire contract (1101.003).2  
   * **Testability**: Highly direct. Expect questions on the specific 31-day grace period and 2-year incontestability rules.  
2. **Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Title 28, §4.2311 (Free Look Period)**  
   * **Why it matters**: Provides the specific consumer protection timelines for annuities and replacements, extending beyond the standard 10 days for life insurance.31  
   * **Testability**: Direct. Knowing the 20/30-day rules for annuities is a common separator between passing and failing scores.  
3. **Texas Estates Code Chapter 121 (Survival Requirements)**  
   * **Why it matters**: This non-insurance code creates the "120-hour rule" that life insurers must follow in Texas for beneficiary payouts in common disasters.5  
   * **Testability**: Highly direct. This is a "uniquely Texas" rule often featured on the state-specific portion of the exam.  
4. **Texas Insurance Code (TIC) Chapter 1111 (Accelerated Term Life Benefits)**  
   * **Why it matters**: Defines terminal illness as death expected within 24 months, which is longer than some other states’ 6 or 12-month requirements.55  
   * **Testability**: Direct. Pay close attention to the 24-month definition of "terminal."  
5. **IRS Publication 525 (Taxable and Nontaxable Income)**  
   * **Why it matters**: Defines the tax-free status of death benefits and the "cost basis" rules for withdrawals and loans.40  
   * **Testability**: Indirect to Direct. It supports questions on the tax treatment of dividend interest and MECs.

## **Top 10 Distinctions**

1. **Lapse vs. Surrender**: A lapse is unintentional (unpaid premium); a surrender is intentional (owner cancels).  
2. **Rider vs. Provision**: A rider is an add-on; a provision is a standard part of the contract.  
3. **Waiver of Premium vs. Monthly Deduction**: One pays the *whole* bill; the other just keeps the lights on.  
4. **Dividend vs. Settlement Option**: Dividends apply while the *insured is alive*; Settlement is for *after death*.  
5. **Revocable vs. Irrevocable**: One is a "suggestion" the owner can change; the other is a "partnership" requiring consent.  
6. **Primary vs. Contingent**: First-string vs. Second-string beneficiary.  
7. **Cost Basis vs. Gain**: Premiums paid (basis) are tax-free; earnings (gain) are taxable.  
8. **Principal Sum vs. Capital Sum**: Full death benefit vs. partial dismemberment benefit.  
9. **Absolute vs. Collateral Assignment**: Total ownership transfer vs. temporary debt security.  
10. **Extended Term vs. Reduced Paid-Up**: Max death benefit (short term) vs. Max duration (small benefit).

## **Top 10 Likely Multiple-Choice Traps**

1. **Trapping on "Verbal Promises"**: Suggesting an agent can change a policy verbally.  
2. **The "6-Day Beneficiary"**: A beneficiary who survives 2 days will be treated as if they died first in Texas.  
3. **The "10% Interest" Limit**: Forgetting the Texas-specific cap on policy loan interest.  
4. **The "Age is Fraud" Trap**: Trying to get you to say a policy is void because the age was wrong.  
5. **The "Dividend Tax" Trap**: Forcing you to choose whether a dividend is taxable (it isn't).  
6. **The "Annuity Free Look"**: Using the 10-day rule for an annuity question when it should be 20 or 30 days.  
7. **The "Suicide Pay-Out"**: Claiming the full death benefit is paid for suicide in year one.  
8. **The "GIR Medical" Trap**: Suggesting a GIR increase requires a physical exam.  
9. **The "MEC Reversal"**: Stating a MEC can be "un-done" by withdrawing the excess money.  
10. **The "Wait Time for Waiver"**: Testing the 6-month waiting period for the waiver of premium.

## **One-Hour Review Summary**

If you only have one hour:

* **Memorize the Texas Numbers**: 31-day grace, 2-year contestability, 120-hour survival, 10% loan interest, 10-day free look, 24-month terminal illness definition.  
* **Nonforfeiture Math**: Know that Extended Term uses the cash value to buy term insurance for the *full original face amount*.  
* **Beneficiary Hierarchy**: Primary \-\> Contingent \-\> Estate.  
* **Ownership Rights**: The owner, not the insured, makes all decisions.  
* **Taxation Basics**: Death benefits are tax-free; dividends are tax-free; dividend *interest* is taxable; MECs are bad (taxed first).

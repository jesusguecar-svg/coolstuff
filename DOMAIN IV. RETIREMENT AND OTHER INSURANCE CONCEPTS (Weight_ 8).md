# **Comprehensive Analysis of Retirement and Other Insurance Concepts for the Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent Examination**

## **SECTION 1: DOMAIN SNAPSHOT**

Domain IV of the Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent examination, titled Retirement and Other Insurance Concepts, accounts for approximately 8% of the total examination weight, which typically translates to eight scorable questions for the Life-only and Health-only exams, or a proportional representation in the combined General Lines exam.1 While the weight of this domain may appear lower than that of policy provisions or state law, it serves as the critical functional bridge where insurance products are applied to solve real-world financial planning problems. This domain is highly technical, focusing on the intersection of insurance law, federal tax codes, and social insurance systems. Mastery of this section is frequently the differentiator for candidates aiming for a first-time pass, as it requires a transition from understanding product features to understanding the strategic application of those features within complex legal and regulatory frameworks.2  
The subject matter of this domain encompasses three primary spheres of professional knowledge. First, it addresses legal ownership structures and the secondary market for life insurance, specifically third-party ownership and the rigorous statutory requirements of the Texas Life Settlements Act.4 Second, it covers the transition from group benefits to individual coverage via the conversion privilege, a topic that is universally tested for its precise timing requirements.6 Third, it explores the vast landscape of retirement planning—encompassing both private vehicles like qualified and nonqualified plans, IRAs, and SEPs, and the public social insurance system comprising Social Security and Medicare.8 Finally, the domain includes the methodology behind life insurance suitability and needs analysis, which dictates the professional standards for recommending coverage amounts and types.11  
High-yield topics within this domain include the specific statutory rescission and disclosure periods mandated by the Texas Insurance Code, the taxation of various retirement plan distributions, and the intricate eligibility rules for Social Security disability and survivor benefits.13 Candidates are most likely to encounter confusion in areas where numerical thresholds are similar but apply to different concepts, such as the 31-day window for group conversion versus the 15-day window for life settlement rescission.7 Furthermore, the distinction between the Human Life Value approach and the Needs approach is a common source of error on exam day, as candidates often struggle to separate the variables associated with income replacement from those associated with specific financial obligations.17 This report identifies these high-risk areas and provides the necessary technical depth to navigate application-based questions accurately.

## **SECTION 2: DEEP EXPLANATION BY SUBTOPIC**

## **A. Third-Party Ownership**

Third-party ownership is a legal arrangement in which the policyowner of a life insurance contract is an entity or individual other than the person whose life is being insured.5 In a standard life insurance policy, the insured and the owner are often the same person. However, in third-party arrangements, the contract becomes a three-party agreement involving the insurer, the insured (whose death triggers the benefit), and the owner (who possesses all the legal rights to the policy).5 This concept matters significantly in insurance planning because it allows for the strategic management of policy rights and the potential exclusion of death benefits from the insured's taxable estate.5 By separating ownership from the insured, families and businesses can ensure that the policy's cash values and death proceeds are controlled by the party that has the most significant financial interest in the policy's outcome.5  
The mechanism of third-party ownership generally begins at the time of application. The third party applies for the policy, pays the premiums, and retains all "incidents of ownership," which include the right to name and change beneficiaries, take policy loans, surrender the policy for its cash value, and assign the policy to another party.5 This structure typically applies to parents insuring their children, one spouse insuring the other, or a business insuring a key employee or business partner.5 A fundamental legal consequence of this arrangement is the requirement for "insurable interest" at the time of policy inception.5 The owner must have a legitimate reason for wanting the insured to remain alive, typically based on a familial relationship or a financial partnership.5 If the policy is owned by a business, the premiums are generally not tax-deductible as a business expense, but the death benefit is usually received income tax-free.22  
What makes third-party ownership distinct from similar concepts is the location of the legal rights. It is often confused with a beneficiary designation, but the distinction is critical: a beneficiary only has a "claim" to the proceeds after the insured's death, whereas a third-party owner has absolute control over the policy during the insured's lifetime.5 In Texas, third-party ownership is used extensively in business succession planning, such as cross-purchase buy-sell agreements, where each partner owns a policy on the life of every other partner.20 The most likely exam trap involves questions asking who has the right to change a beneficiary or take a loan in a third-party setup; candidates must remember that these rights belong exclusively to the owner, never the insured.5

## **B. Life Settlements**

A life settlement is a financial transaction where the owner of an existing life insurance policy sells the policy to a third party, known as a life settlement provider, for a lump-sum cash payment.25 The payment amount is always greater than the policy's cash surrender value but less than its expected death benefit.25 This concept is vital for life insurance planning as it provides an alternative for policyowners—typically seniors—who no longer need their coverage, can no longer afford the premiums, or require immediate liquidity for medical or retirement expenses.27 Once the transaction is finalized, the provider assumes all future premium payments and becomes the new owner and beneficiary, ultimately receiving the death benefit when the insured passes away.27  
The operation of life settlements in Texas is strictly governed by the Life Settlements Act, found in Chapter 1111A of the Texas Insurance Code.29 This subtopic applies primarily to senior citizens, often age 65 or older, with policies having a face value of at least $100,000.26 Legal consequences are rigorous: life settlement brokers represent the owner and owe them a fiduciary duty to act in their best interest.25 The owner has a statutory right to rescind the contract within 15 days of its execution.4 Furthermore, Texas law prohibits the settlement of a policy within the first two years of its issuance unless specific exceptions occur, such as the owner's divorce, the death of a spouse, or the owner's retirement from full-time employment.14  
A significant distinction exists between a life settlement and a viatical settlement; the latter involves an insured who is terminally or chronically ill, often with a life expectancy of two years or less, whereas life settlements generally involve healthier seniors.26 From a tax perspective, viatical settlement proceeds are generally received income tax-free, while life settlement proceeds may be taxable on the portion that exceeds the owner's tax basis.26 In Texas, the commissioner requires providers to provide written disclosures to the owner, including possible alternatives like accelerated death benefits and the potential impact on eligibility for government assistance.4 The most likely exam traps involve the 15-day rescission period and the specific list of "allowable exceptions" that permit a settlement before the two-year mark.3

## **C. Group Life Insurance: Conversion Privilege**

Group life insurance is a type of coverage provided to a specific group, such as employees of a single employer or members of a labor union, under a single master contract.6 The employer or organization is the policyowner, while the individual members are the insureds and receive certificates of insurance as evidence of coverage.7 The conversion privilege is a mandatory provision in Texas group life policies that allows an insured individual to convert their group coverage into an individual permanent life insurance policy without having to provide evidence of insurability.7 This concept is crucial because it ensures that employees who lose their job, leave their group, or see their group policy terminated do not become uninsurable due to a change in health status.7  
The conversion privilege generally works by giving the insured a 31-day window following the termination of their group coverage to apply for an individual policy.7 The new policy must typically be a permanent form of life insurance (such as whole life), and the premium is based on the insured's attained age.7 If the insured individual dies during this 31-day conversion period, the group policy is legally required to pay the death benefit, even if the conversion application has not been formally submitted or approved.7 This protection applies to employees, members of unions, and in many cases, their insured spouses and children.7  
The Texas-specific angle involves Section 1131.111 of the Insurance Code, which mandates that if the group policy is terminated or amended to end coverage for a class of insureds, anyone who has been covered for at least five years prior is entitled to convert.7 The amount available for conversion in this specific scenario is limited to the lesser of the individual's previous group coverage amount (minus any new group coverage obtained within 31 days) or $10,000.7 What makes this different from "policy replacement" is that conversion is a guaranteed right with no medical underwriting, whereas replacement typically requires a new application and proof of health.2 Likely exam traps focus on the duration of the conversion period (31 days) and the fact that no evidence of insurability is required.3

## **D. Retirement Plans**

Retirement planning represents a major subtopic of Domain IV, involving the accumulation and distribution of assets for post-working years.8

#### **1\. Qualified Plans**

A qualified retirement plan is an employer-sponsored plan that meets the stringent requirements of the Internal Revenue Code and the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA).10 These plans, such as 401(k)s and profit-sharing plans, must be in writing, communicated to employees, and established for the exclusive benefit of the participants.10 They are vital because they offer significant tax advantages: employer contributions are tax-deductible as a business expense, and employee contributions are made with pre-tax dollars, reducing current taxable income.21 Furthermore, earnings within the plan grow tax-deferred until distribution.41 To remain "qualified," a plan must be non-discriminatory, meaning it must be offered to a broad range of employees and cannot favor highly compensated executives over rank-and-file workers.10

#### **2\. Nonqualified Plans**

Nonqualified plans do not meet the ERISA and IRC requirements for tax-favorable treatment and are typically used as selective benefit tools for key executives.10 These plans, such as deferred compensation arrangements, are not subject to non-discrimination rules, allowing an employer to choose exactly which employees may participate.10 While they offer flexibility, they carry higher risks: employer contributions are not tax-deductible until the employee receives the benefit, and the assets are generally part of the employer's general fund, meaning they are subject to the claims of the company's creditors.10 The most significant planning consequence is that these plans are "unfunded" or "informally funded," lacking the trust-based protections of qualified plans.10

#### **3\. Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)**

Individual Retirement Accounts are personal savings vehicles that allow individuals to contribute earned income for retirement.8 Traditional IRAs provide a potential tax deduction for contributions, with all earnings and contributions taxed as ordinary income upon distribution.37 Roth IRAs, by contrast, are funded with after-tax dollars; they provide no immediate tax break, but all qualified distributions (including earnings) are entirely tax-free.37 A critical timing rule for both is the ![][image1] age threshold for penalty-free withdrawals, though Traditional IRAs also require "Required Minimum Distributions" (RMDs) starting at age 73 (for those reaching 72 after 2022).20

#### **4\. Simplified Employee Pension plans (SEPs)**

SEPs are specialized retirement plans designed primarily for small business owners and self-employed individuals.43 A SEP allows an employer to contribute directly to a traditional IRA established for each eligible employee.43 SEPs are notably simple to administer and have higher contribution limits than traditional IRAs—up to 25% of the employee's compensation or a maximum dollar limit ($69,000 in 2024; $72,000 in 2026).43 A critical distinction for SEPs is that they are funded exclusively by the employer; employees are not permitted to make their own elective deferrals into a SEP account.43

#### **5\. Tax treatment of contributions, distributions, and rollovers**

The tax treatment of retirement funds is the most testable portion of this subtopic.46 Contributions to qualified plans and traditional IRAs are generally pre-tax (deductible), whereas Roth IRA contributions are after-tax.41 Distributions from qualified plans and traditional IRAs are taxed as ordinary income.41 A rollover is the process of moving funds from one retirement account to another without triggering a tax event.37 A direct rollover (custodian-to-custodian) is preferred because it avoids the mandatory 20% federal income tax withholding that applies if the check is made out to the participant.38 If a participant takes a 60-day rollover, they must reinvest 100% of the funds into a new plan within 60 days to avoid taxation and a 10% early withdrawal penalty if they are under age ![][image1].20 Likely exam traps involve confusing the tax treatment of Traditional and Roth accounts or incorrectly identifying who can contribute to a SEP.43

## **E. Social Security Benefits**

Social Security, or the Old-Age, Survivors, and Disability Insurance (OASDI) program, is a federal social insurance system funded by payroll taxes under the Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA).9

#### **1\. Retirement**

Social Security retirement benefits are available to workers who have earned a sufficient number of work credits.47 For 2026, a worker earns one credit for every $1,890 in earnings, up to a maximum of four credits per year.47 To be "fully insured" and eligible for retirement benefits, a worker generally needs 40 credits (10 years of work).47 The benefit amount is based on the worker's Primary Insurance Amount (PIA), which is calculated using the average indexed monthly earnings (AIME) over their 35 highest-earning years.20 Full retirement age (FRA) is now 67 for anyone born in 1960 or later, though reduced benefits can be taken as early as age 62\.9

#### **2\. Disability**

Disability benefits are paid to workers who have a physical or mental impairment that is expected to result in death or last for a continuous period of at least 12 months.9 To be eligible, the worker must meet a "recent work" and "duration of work" test, which generally requires 20 credits earned in the 10 years before the disability began.9 A critical rule is the mandatory five-month waiting period: benefits do not begin until the worker has been disabled for five full calendar months, with the first payment issued in the sixth month.13 The benefit amount is equal to 100% of the worker's PIA.48

#### **3\. Survivorship**

Survivor benefits provide monthly income to the family of a deceased worker.9 Eligible survivors include a widow or widower age 60 or older (50 if disabled), a surviving spouse of any age who is caring for a child under age 16, and unmarried children under age 18 (or 19 if still in high school).15 A unique concept is the "Blackout Period," which is the period during which a surviving spouse is ineligible for Social Security benefits.15 This period begins when the youngest child turns 16 (ending the spouse's caregiver benefit) and continues until the surviving spouse reaches age 60 (the earliest age for widow/widower benefits).15

#### **4\. Medicare**

Medicare is the federal health insurance program for people age 65 and older, as well as for certain younger people with disabilities or end-stage renal disease.9 Medicare Part A (Hospital Insurance) is generally free for those who have 40 work quarters, whereas Medicare Part B (Medical Insurance) requires a monthly premium.58 A key connection for the exam is that an individual who has been receiving Social Security disability benefits for 24 months is automatically eligible for Medicare, regardless of their age.9 Medicare differs from private health insurance primarily because it does not offer family plans; coverage is strictly on an individual basis.59 Most likely exam traps include the specific ages associated with the Blackout Period and the 24-month waiting period for disability-based Medicare.15

## **F. Life Insurance Needs Analysis/Suitability**

Suitability is the ethical and legal requirement that an insurance recommendation must align with the client’s financial situation, goals, and risk tolerance.11

#### **1\. Personal insurance needs**

Personal needs analysis identifies the financial gap left by the death of a breadwinner.11 It involves calculating immediate cash needs—such as funeral expenses (averaging $10,000+), medical bills, and legal fees—as well as ongoing income replacement for survivors.12 The analysis should also account for long-term goals like mortgage liquidation and children's college education.12

#### **2\. Business insurance needs**

Businesses use life insurance to mitigate the financial risk of losing essential personnel.22 Key Person Insurance protects the company against the operational shock of an indispensable employee's death; the business owns the policy, pays the premiums, and is the beneficiary.22 Buy-Sell Agreements are legal contracts funded by life insurance that outline how ownership will transfer if a partner dies.24 In a cross-purchase plan, each partner buys a policy on every other partner; in an entity (stock redemption) plan, the business itself owns the policies on each partner.24

#### **3\. Human Life Value approach**

The Human Life Value (HLV) approach calculates the economic value of an individual based on their potential future earnings.11 It typically involves multiplying the individual's annual income by the number of years remaining until retirement, then discounting that total to its present value.12 This method focuses on the "asset" side of the individual—replacing the income they would have earned—rather than the specific expenses the family will face.11

#### **4\. Needs approach**

The Needs approach is the more common method today and focuses on the "liability" side of the family’s balance sheet.17 It calculates the total amount required for immediate, ongoing, and future expenses (such as the DIME method: Debt, Income, Mortgage, Education) and subtracts existing assets like savings and other life insurance policies to find the "gap" or true need.12 Likely exam traps involve questions that provide variables from both methods (e.g., salary and mortgage balance) and ask which method is being utilized; candidates must remember that HLV ignores the specific debt and focuses strictly on income replacement.17

## **SECTION 4: EXAM-FOCUSED TAKEAWAYS**

Professional analysis of Texas exam candidate performance reveals that success is predicated on the ability to memorize specific timeframes and distinguish between federal tax rules and state insurance laws.2

## **What I Absolutely Need to Know Cold**

The candidate must memorize the 31-day conversion period for group life insurance. This is a non-negotiable fact; the conversion must be to a permanent plan, and no medical exam is required.7 Equally important is the 15-day rescission period for Texas life settlements. If an insured dies within those 15 days, the contract is rescinded automatically upon repayment of proceeds.4 In the realm of Social Security, the five-month waiting period for disability is a universal test item; remember that benefits are "entitled" in the sixth month.13 Finally, understand that for a retirement plan to be "qualified," it must be non-discriminatory and follow ERISA guidelines.10

## **What is Secondary but Still Testable**

Candidates should be familiar with the "2-Year Rule" for Texas life settlements and its exceptions, such as divorce, bankruptcy, or retirement.14 The credit system for Social Security—specifically the 40-credit requirement for "fully insured" status and the four-credit annual maximum—is frequently tested.47 In business insurance, the specific tax treatment of Key Person insurance (non-deductible premiums but tax-free benefits) is a secondary but high-value concept.23 The "Blackout Period" ages (16 and 60\) are also secondary details that often appear in more difficult application-based questions.15

## **What is Low Priority**

Highly technical calculations of Social Security "bend points" or the exact present value formulas used in Human Life Value calculations are rarely required; the exam focuses on the *variables* in the formula rather than the calculation itself.18 The history of the 1983 Social Security amendments or specific legislative bill numbers (like HB 2277\) is for background only and is not directly testable.9

## **The Most Likely Wrong-Answer Traps**

Test writers frequently use the term "term insurance" as a distractor for group conversion questions; remember, conversion is almost always to a *permanent* policy.7 Another trap involves suggesting that a life settlement is for a "terminally ill" person only; that is a viatical settlement.26 In retirement questions, distractor choices often suggest that an employee can contribute to a SEP; the correct answer is that only the *employer* contributes.43 Lastly, watch for questions suggesting that a beneficiary can take a policy loan; only the *owner* has this right in a third-party ownership setup.5

## **Quick Memory Hooks or Mnemonics**

* **DIME for Needs:** **D**ebt, **I**ncome, **M**ortgage, **E**ducation.12  
* **The 31-15 Rule:** **31** days to convert group; **15** days to quit a settlement.7  
* **SEP \= Solo Employer Pays:** Reminds you that employees do not contribute.43  
* **16-60 Blackout:** Benefits stop at child age **16** and resume for spouse at age **60**.15  
* **5-6 Disability:** **5** months wait, **6th** month paid.13

## **SECTION 5: OFFICIAL RULES AND AUTHORITIES**

Professional research into the Texas General Lines exam indicates that the following authorities form the basis for all scorable questions.

## **1\. Texas Insurance Code Chapter 1111A (Life Settlements Act)**

This statute is the primary authority for all life settlement regulations in Texas.29 It mandates the licensing of brokers and providers and defines the consumer protections that are heavily tested, such as disclosure requirements and the right of rescission.4

* **Significance:** Directly testable. Expect 2-3 questions specifically on these statutory timeframes and definitions.

## **2\. Texas Insurance Code Chapter 1131 (Group Life Insurance)**

Chapter 1131 defines the eligible groups in Texas and mandates the conversion privilege.6

* **Significance:** Directly testable. The conversion privilege (Section 1131.112) is one of the most frequently asked questions on the entire exam.

## **3\. Texas Administrative Code (TAC), Title 28, Chapter 4**

TAC Title 28 contains the rules for annuity suitability and the required disclosure forms (such as Subchapter W).70

* **Significance:** Testable. Suitability is a major focus for TDI, especially with the 2024 updates to annuity disclosure requirements.73

## **4\. IRS Publication 590-A & 590-B**

These federal publications establish the rules for IRA contributions, distributions, and the ![][image1] age threshold.8

* **Significance:** Testable background. While these are federal rules, they are the basis for the "national" portion of the retirement planning questions.

## **5\. Social Security Act / SSA.gov**

The Social Security Administration's guidelines define the Primary Insurance Amount (PIA) and the "fully insured" credit system.9

* **Significance:** Directly testable. The Blackout Period and the five-month disability waiting period are based on these federal statutes.

## **One-Hour Review Summary**

If you have only one hour to review Domain IV, focus your efforts on the specific timing and tax rules that constitute the bulk of the questions. Start with the **Texas Life Settlements Act**; remember the 15-day rescission period and the fiduciary duty of the broker to the owner.4 Move immediately to **Group Life Conversion** and memorize the 31-day window and the "no medical" rule—this is a high-probability question.7 For **Retirement Plans**, understand the fundamental difference between Qualified (ERISA) and Nonqualified (Selective) plans, and contrast the taxation of Traditional and Roth IRAs.10 Spend ten minutes on **Social Security Disability** (5-month wait) and the **Blackout Period** (16-60).13 Finally, review the **Needs Approach** (DIME) and how it differs from the **Human Life Value** income replacement formula.12 This strategic review covers the approximately 8% of the exam weighted in this domain by prioritizing high-yield statutory and tax definitions.

## **Ready for Architecture**

This domain should be integrated into the following grouping during the course architecture phase:

* **Module A: Legal and Ownership Principles.** Group "Third-Party Ownership" and "Life Settlements" together to emphasize the role of the policyowner versus the insured.5  
* **Module B: Business and Group Concepts.** Combine "Group Life Conversion" with "Business Insurance Needs" (Key Person/Buy-Sell) to highlight insurance as a professional tool for organizations.7  
* **Module C: Retirement and Social Insurance.** Link "Retirement Plans" and "Social Security/Medicare" into a single comprehensive lesson on long-term financial security.9  
* **Module D: Sales and Suitability.** Position "Needs Analysis" as the capstone section that teaches the agent how to apply all previously learned policy types to a client's specific gap.11

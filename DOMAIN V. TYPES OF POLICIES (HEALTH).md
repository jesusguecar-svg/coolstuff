# **Comprehensive Research Analysis of Health Insurance Policy Types for the Texas General Lines Agent Examination**

## **SECTION 1: DOMAIN SNAPSHOT**

The "Types of Policies (Health)" domain represents a pivotal segment of the Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent examination, accounting for a significant 16 percent of the total scoreable questions.1 This domain is not merely a collection of product definitions but serves as the structural foundation upon which all other health insurance concepts—such as provisions, underwriting, and claims—are built. The Texas Department of Insurance (TDI) prioritizes this domain because it directly reflects an agent's ability to identify appropriate risk-transfer mechanisms for a diverse range of consumer needs, from individual paycheck protection to large-scale employer-sponsored health benefit plans.  
The high testing weight is justified by the increasing complexity of the health insurance landscape in Texas. The exam requires a nuanced understanding of how traditional indemnity plans have integrated with managed care models and how federal mandates, such as the Affordable Care Act (ACA) and COBRA, interact with the Texas Insurance Code (TIC).2 Candidates must navigate the subtle distinctions between different types of disability income policies, the specific eligibility triggers for long-term care, and the highly regulated world of Medicare Supplement plans, which are subject to rigorous standardization under the Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Title 28\.4  
Statistically, the highest-yield subtopics within this domain include Disability Income—specifically the variations in business-related policies—and the Managed Care spectrum (HMOs, PPOs, and POS plans). These areas are frequently the source of "application-style" questions where the candidate must analyze a scenario and select the most appropriate policy type or determine the tax implications of a benefit payout.6 Conversely, the subtopics most likely to be confused on exam day involve the overlapping characteristics of consumer-driven health accounts, such as Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) versus Health Reimbursement Accounts (HRAs), and the precise differences between federal COBRA and Texas State Continuation rules.7  
This research memo is designed to deconstruct these complexities, prioritizing what is most testable for the Texas exam. It moves beyond superficial descriptions to explore the underlying logic of policy design, the specific dollar and day thresholds mandated by Texas law, and the "exam traps" that frequently derail ill-prepared candidates. By focusing on the interplay between product features and regulatory requirements, this analysis provides the deep-dive necessary for an elite-level prep course.

## **SECTION 2: DEEP EXPLANATION BY SUBTOPIC**

## **A. Disability Income**

Disability income insurance is often described as "income protection" or "paycheck insurance." Its fundamental purpose is to mitigate the risk of financial catastrophe following the loss of earned income due to sickness or injury.9 In the Texas regulatory environment, these policies are strictly defined as providing periodic payments, usually weekly or monthly, during the continuance of a disability.9

#### **1\. Individual Disability Income Policy**

An individual disability income policy provides a personal safety net for those who do not have access to group coverage or who require supplemental protection. The problem it solves is the immediate cessation of income for an individual, which can lead to a rapid depletion of savings and potential loss of assets. It is designed for self-employed individuals, professionals, and high-income earners who want to protect their specific standard of living.  
These policies generally work through a combination of an elimination period and a benefit period. The elimination period acts as a time-based deductible, requiring the insured to wait a specific number of days (e.g., 30, 60, or 90\) after becoming disabled before benefits begin to accrue.10 The benefit period specifies how long those payments will continue, ranging from two years to age 65 or Social Security Normal Retirement Age.10 Texas law (TAC § 3.3075) establishes minimum standards for these policies, requiring periodic payments of at least $100 per month for ages up to 62, and $50 per month thereafter.9  
The most critical feature of an individual policy is the "definition of disability." The "Own-Occupation" definition pays benefits if the insured cannot perform the duties of their specific profession, even if they could work in another field. The "Any-Occupation" definition is more restrictive, paying only if the insured cannot perform the duties of any job for which they are reasonably suited.11 A unique Texas angle is that policies must use "result" language for injuries, avoiding the more restrictive "accidental means" test.12  
Taxation is a major testable point: if an individual pays 100% of the premium with after-tax dollars, the benefits are 100% tax-free.11 However, if the premiums are paid with pre-tax dollars (as in some employer-sponsored arrangements), the benefits are fully taxable as ordinary income.13

#### **2\. Business Overhead Expense (BOE) Policy**

The BOE policy is a specialized form of disability insurance designed specifically for small business owners and professionals in private practice (e.g., doctors, lawyers, accountants).10 The problem it solves is not the owner's personal income, but the survival of the business itself during the owner's disability. It ensures that the "lights stay on" so the business remains viable until the owner returns or decides to sell the practice.  
The policy works on a reimbursement basis, covering actual expenses such as rent, utilities, employee salaries (other than the owner’s), and leased equipment.10 It specifically excludes the owner's salary, profits, and the cost of inventory. Elimination periods are typically short (15 to 30 days), and benefit periods are likewise brief (12 to 24 months).10  
A significant exam trap is the tax treatment: BOE premiums are tax-deductible as a business expense, but the benefits received are taxable income to the business.11 However, because the benefits are immediately used to pay deductible business expenses, the net effect is often tax-neutral.

#### **3\. Business Disability Buyout Policy**

This policy is utilized to fund a buy-sell agreement among partners or shareholders in a closely held business.10 If a partner becomes permanently disabled, the policy provides the liquidity necessary for the remaining partners to purchase the disabled partner's interest. This prevents the disabled partner's spouse or family from becoming an unwanted "silent partner" in the business operations.  
These policies are characterized by very long elimination periods, often one to two years, to ensure that the disability is truly permanent before the buyout is triggered.10 Benefits are frequently paid in a lump sum, though they can be structured as monthly installments. Ownership is typically held by the business or the other partners.10  
Unlike BOE, premiums for disability buyout are not tax-deductible, but the benefits are received tax-free.15 This ensures that the full face value of the policy is available for the purchase price of the business interest without being depleted by income taxes.

#### **4\. Group Disability Income Policy**

Group disability insurance is an employer-sponsored benefit that covers a pool of employees under a single master contract. It provides a lower-cost alternative to individual policies due to the efficiencies of group underwriting and the law of large numbers.1 It is designed for the general workforce of an employer.  
These plans are usually divided into Short-Term Disability (STD), covering periods up to 13 or 26 weeks, and Long-Term Disability (LTD), which picks up where STD ends and can last for years.13 A key characteristic is the "coordination of benefits," where group disability payments are often reduced (offset) by other income sources like Social Security Disability or Workers' Compensation.13  
The tax treatment is a favorite exam topic: if the employer pays the entire premium, the benefits are 100% taxable to the employee. If the employee pays the entire premium with after-tax dollars, the benefits are tax-free. If the cost is shared, the benefits are taxable in proportion to the employer's contribution (e.g., if the employer pays 60% of the premium, 60% of the benefit is taxable).13

#### **5\. Key Employee Policy**

This policy protects a business against the financial loss resulting from the disability of a vital employee whose unique skills or contacts are essential to the firm's success.10 The business is the applicant, owner, and beneficiary. The problem it solves is the cost of replacing the employee or the loss of revenue during their absence.  
The definition of a key employee in Texas usually involves someone who is not a majority owner (less than 50%) but contributes significantly to the financial health of the firm.10 Benefit periods are typically short (6 to 18 months), giving the business enough time to recruit a successor.10  
As with the buyout policy, premiums are not deductible, but the benefits are tax-free to the business.10

## **B. Accidental Death and Dismemberment (AD\&D)**

AD\&D is a limited-benefit policy that pays a lump sum if the insured dies or suffers a specific loss of limb or sight due to a covered accident.17 It does not cover death or disability resulting from natural causes or sickness. It is designed for individuals seeking low-cost, high-limit protection for sudden accidental events.  
The policy uses two primary benefit amounts:

1. **Principal Sum:** The full face amount of the policy, paid upon the accidental death of the insured or the loss of two primary parts (e.g., both hands, both feet, or sight in both eyes).17  
2. **Capital Sum:** A percentage (usually 50%) of the Principal Sum, paid for the loss of one primary part (e.g., one hand or sight in one eye).17

Texas-specific regulations under TAC § 3.3008 are vital: Texas prohibits the use of an "accidental means" test. Policies must use "accidental bodily injury" or "result" language.12 In an "accidental means" jurisdiction, both the cause of the accident and the result must be unintended. In Texas, if the result was unintended (e.g., a person intentionally jumps off a wall but unintentionally breaks their leg), it is considered a covered accident.12 Common exclusions include suicide, self-inflicted injury, war, and illegal acts.

## **C. Medical Expense Insurance**

Medical expense insurance encompasses a broad range of products designed to cover the costs of healthcare services. The evolution of this market from traditional "First Dollar" indemnity plans to modern managed care is a central theme of the Texas exam.

#### **1\. Basic Hospital, Medical, and Surgical Policies**

These are traditional "base plans" that provide modest coverage for specific healthcare events.20 They are often called "First Dollar" plans because they usually do not have a deductible. They are designed for individuals who want coverage for routine medical events without high out-of-pocket costs.

* **Basic Hospital:** Covers hospital room and board and miscellaneous expenses (e.g., lab tests, X-rays) up to a daily limit and a maximum number of days.21  
* **Basic Surgical:** Pays the surgeon's fees based on a "schedule of operations," which lists the maximum dollar amount the policy will pay for each specific procedure.20  
* **Basic Medical:** Covers non-surgical physician visits, often on a per-visit or per-day basis.20

Texas law (TAC § 3.3071) sets minimum standards, such as requiring basic hospital coverage for at least 31 days of confinement with room and board benefits of at least 80% of semi-private room charges or $30 per day.21 These plans provide no protection against catastrophic loss.

#### **2\. Major Medical Policies**

Major Medical plans are designed to provide comprehensive, high-limit coverage for catastrophic medical events.20 They cover almost all types of healthcare services, from hospitalization and surgery to prescription drugs and therapy. They are designed for individuals and families seeking protection against life-altering medical bills.  
Key features include:

* **Deductibles:** The initial amount the insured must pay before the policy begins to pay.20  
* **Coinsurance:** The sharing of costs between the insurer and the insured after the deductible is met (e.g., 80% paid by the insurer, 20% by the insured).20  
* **Stop-Loss Provision:** A "ceiling" on the insured's out-of-pocket expenses. Once the insured’s costs reach a certain limit (e.g., $5,000), the insurer pays 100% of covered expenses for the remainder of the year.20

Texas mandates (TIC § 1201.109) that insurers provide at least 60 days' notice of a premium rate increase for individual Major Medical policies.23

#### **3\. Health Maintenance Organizations (HMOs)**

An HMO is a unique structure that acts as both the health care provider and the insurer.24 Members pay a fixed monthly premium in exchange for a broad range of healthcare services. HMOs emphasize "maintenance" through preventive care, such as annual checkups and immunizations, to reduce long-term medical costs.  
HMOs use a "gatekeeper" system where every member must select a Primary Care Physician (PCP). The PCP coordinates all care and must provide a referral for a member to see a specialist.24 HMOs typically cover only in-network care; out-of-network services are only covered in true medical emergencies.27  
In Texas, HMOs are regulated under TIC Chapter 843 and must obtain a Certificate of Authority (COA) to operate.24 They are prohibited from "balance billing" members for covered services and must pay clean claims from providers within 30 days (electronic) or 45 days (paper).24

#### **4\. Preferred Provider Organizations (PPOs)**

A PPO is a group of healthcare providers that contract with an insurance company to provide services at a pre-negotiated, discounted rate.26 It is designed for individuals who want more flexibility than an HMO.  
Unlike HMOs, PPO members do not need to select a PCP and do not need referrals to see specialists.25 Members are encouraged to use in-network providers (the "preferred" providers) for lower out-of-pocket costs, but the PPO will pay for out-of-network care at a reduced benefit level (e.g., higher deductible and lower coinsurance).27

#### **5\. Point of Service (POS) plans**

A POS plan is a hybrid of an HMO and a PPO. It utilizes an HMO-style gatekeeper and referral system for in-network care.27 However, at the "point of service" (the time the care is needed), the member can choose to seek care outside the network without a referral, similar to a PPO.28  
If the member follows the gatekeeper/referral path, they enjoy HMO-style low copayments. If they choose the out-of-network path, they face the higher deductibles and coinsurance typical of a PPO.27

#### **6\. Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs)**

An FSA is an employer-sponsored benefit that allows employees to set aside a portion of their pre-tax earnings to pay for qualified medical expenses.8 The problem it solves is the high cost of predictable healthcare items like copayments, deductibles, and prescriptions.  
For the 2026 tax year, the contribution limit is $3,400.7 FSAs are subject to the "Use It or Lose It" rule: any funds not spent by the end of the year are forfeited to the employer, though employers can allow a 2.5-month grace period or a small carryover amount (up to $680 for 2026).7 FSAs are not portable; if the employee leaves the company, the funds stay with the employer.8

#### **7\. High Deductible Health Plans (HDHPs) and Health Savings Accounts (HSAs)**

An HSA is a tax-advantaged savings account owned by the individual, which must be paired with a qualified HDHP.7 The HDHP features a high deductible and a limited out-of-pocket maximum. For 2026, the minimum individual deductible is $1,700, and the maximum out-of-pocket is $8,500.31  
HSAs offer a "triple tax advantage":

1. Contributions are tax-deductible (or pre-tax if through payroll).  
2. Earnings in the account grow tax-deferred.  
3. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free.7

Unlike FSAs, HSA funds roll over from year to year and are fully portable if the individual changes jobs.7 If funds are used for non-medical expenses before age 65, they are subject to income tax plus a 20% penalty. After 65, the penalty is removed, but non-medical withdrawals are still taxed as income.7

#### **8\. Health Reimbursement Accounts (HRAs)**

An HRA is an entirely employer-funded account used to reimburse employees for qualified medical expenses.8 Employees cannot contribute to an HRA. The employer owns the account and determines the rollover rules.8  
HRAs are often used by small employers to help employees pay for individual health insurance premiums or to "soften" the impact of a high deductible.8 Like FSAs, HRAs are not portable; the balance stays with the employer if the employee terminates employment.8

## **D. Medicare Supplement Policies**

Medicare Supplement (also known as Medigap) policies are sold by private insurance companies to cover the "gaps" in Original Medicare (Parts A and B).34 They cover expenses like Medicare’s deductibles, coinsurance, and copayments. They are designed for seniors and certain disabled individuals who find the out-of-pocket costs of Original Medicare to be unpredictable or burdensome.  
In Texas, these policies are strictly standardized by the TAC into plans labeled A through N.4 Every plan with the same letter must offer the same benefits, regardless of the insurance company.

* **Plan A:** The "Core Benefit" plan. It covers basic items like Part A coinsurance for hospitalization and the 20% Part B coinsurance. In Texas, any insurer that sells Medigap must at least offer Plan A.4  
* **Plan G:** Currently the most popular plan for new enrollees, as it covers everything except the Part B deductible.

Medicare Supplement policies are "guaranteed renewable," meaning the insurer cannot cancel the policy except for non-payment of premium or material misrepresentation.35 In Texas, individuals have a 6-month open enrollment window beginning the month they are 65 and enrolled in Part B. During this window, they have a "guaranteed issue" right, meaning they cannot be denied coverage or charged more based on health history.37 Texas also provides guaranteed issue rights to Plan A for those under 65 who lose Medicaid eligibility.4

## **E. Group Insurance**

Group health insurance involves a single policy that covers a defined group of people, typically the employees of a company or members of an association.

#### **1\. Differences between individual and group contracts**

The primary distinction is the nature of the legal agreement. In group insurance, the contract is between the insurer and the employer (the policyholder). The employer receives the "Master Policy," while the employees receive "Certificates of Insurance" as evidence of their coverage.1 Individual contracts are between the insurer and the person insured.  
Group underwriting focuses on the risk characteristics of the entire group rather than the health of each individual.1 This allows individuals who might be uninsurable on an individual basis to obtain coverage.

#### **2\. General Characteristics**

* **Eligibility:** Texas law (TIC § 1501.002) defines an "eligible employee" as one who works on a full-time basis, usually at least 30 hours per week.38  
* **Participation:** To prevent adverse selection, insurers require a minimum percentage of eligible employees to participate. For contributory plans (where employees share the cost), the standard requirement is 75%. For non-contributory plans (employer pays 100%), 100% participation is required.38  
* **Small Group:** In Texas, a "small employer" is defined as a business with 2 to 50 employees.39

#### **3\. COBRA and Texas State Continuation**

The Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (COBRA) is a federal law that allows employees to continue their group coverage for a limited time after a "qualifying event" such as job loss (unless for gross misconduct), divorce, or the death of the employee.2

* **Federal COBRA:** Applies to employers with 20 or more employees. Duration is typically 18 months (can be 36 months for dependents in certain cases).2  
* **Texas State Continuation (Mini-COBRA):** Applies to employers with 2 to 19 employees who are not subject to federal COBRA. The duration is 9 months.2  
* **Extension Rule:** Texas allows for an additional 6 months of continuation for those who have exhausted their federal COBRA coverage, potentially bringing the total duration to 24 months.2

Individuals on continuation must pay the full premium (including the employer’s portion) plus a 2% administrative fee.42

## **F. Individual/Group Long Term Care (LTC)**

LTC insurance provides coverage for the medical and personal care needs of individuals who have lost the ability to care for themselves due to chronic illness, aging, or cognitive impairment.5 It is designed to protect assets from being exhausted by nursing home or home health care costs.

#### **1\. Eligibility Triggers**

Eligibility for benefits is typically determined by two triggers:

1. **Activities of Daily Living (ADLs):** Texas law (TAC § 3.3804) recognizes 6 specific ADLs: Bathing, Continence, Dressing, Eating, Toileting, and Transferring.5 A policy usually triggers benefits when the insured is unable to perform at least 2 of these functions without substantial assistance.43  
2. **Cognitive Impairment:** Benefits are also triggered if the insured requires supervision or verbal cueing due to severe memory loss or disorientation (e.g., Alzheimer’s or dementia).5

#### **2\. Levels of Care**

Texas defines several levels of LTC:

* **Skilled Nursing Care:** Daily nursing and rehabilitative care performed by skilled medical personnel under the orders of a physician.5  
* **Intermediate Care:** Occasional nursing or rehabilitative care performed by skilled medical personnel.5  
* **Custodial (Maintenance) Care:** Assistance with ADLs provided by non-medical personnel. This is the most common level of care.5  
* **Home Health Care:** Services provided in the insured's residence, including nursing, therapy, or assistance with ADLs.5

LTC policies in Texas must offer an "Outline of Coverage" and a 30-day "Free Look" period for a full refund of premium.44

## **G. Other Policies**

#### **1\. Dental and Vision**

These are limited benefit policies. Dental plans often use a "Scheduled" approach (fixed dollar amount per procedure) or a "Non-scheduled" approach (usual, reasonable, and customary charges). Vision plans typically cover eye exams, lenses, and frames.45 Texas recently updated coordination of benefits (COB) rules to ensure that if a person has both a medical and a vision plan that cover the same procedure, the primary and secondary payers are clearly identified.45

#### **2\. Cancer and Critical Illness**

* **Cancer Policies:** These pay a lump sum or specific benefits for the diagnosis and treatment of cancer. Texas (TAC § 3.3077) requires minimum benefits such as at least $50/day for the first 10 days of hospitalization.47  
* **Critical Illness:** Pays a lump sum upon the initial diagnosis of a specified condition, such as a heart attack, stroke, or major organ failure.47 The benefit is paid directly to the insured to use however they wish (e.g., for non-medical bills like a mortgage).50

#### **3\. Worksite (Employer-sponsored)**

These are voluntary, employee-paid supplemental plans (like accident or hospital indemnity) offered through an employer via payroll deduction. They are portable, meaning the employee can keep the plan if they change jobs.50

#### **4\. Hospital Indemnity**

This is a "valued" policy that pays a fixed dollar amount for each day the insured is hospitalized (e.g., $200 per day), regardless of the actual medical costs.51 It is intended as a supplement to help with out-of-pocket costs.

#### **5\. Short-term medical**

Designed to fill temporary gaps in coverage (e.g., between jobs). Under new 2024 rules, these are limited to a 3-month term with a possible 1-month renewal (4 months total).53 They are not ACA-compliant and often exclude pre-existing conditions.3

#### **6\. Accident**

An accident policy provides benefits only for losses resulting from accidental injury and excludes any sickness-related claims. They are commonly sold as supplemental coverage for high-risk individuals.47

## **SECTION 4: EXAM-FOCUSED TAKEAWAYS**

The following takeaways summarize the most testable elements of health policy types for the Texas exam.

## **Disability Income: Critical Success Factors**

* **Must Know Cold:** The tax rules. If I pay, it’s tax-free. If the boss pays, it’s taxable. If we split, we split the tax.13  
* **Secondary:** The Texas minimum benefit is $100/month for those under 62\.9  
* **Low Priority:** Specific formulas for "residual disability" calculations, though the concept is testable.  
* **The Trap:** Test writers love to say BOE pays the owner's salary. It does NOT. It pays the rent.11  
* **Memory Hook:** **"B.O.E. \= Business Only Expenses"** (No owner salary).

## **Managed Care: Critical Success Factors**

* **Must Know Cold:** HMO \= Gatekeeper \+ Referrals \+ In-network only. PPO \= No Gatekeeper \+ No Referrals \+ Choice.25  
* **Secondary:** HMOs in Texas are regulated by TIC Chapter 843 and need a COA.24  
* **The Trap:** Thinking a POS plan is just an HMO. It’s an HMO with an "out-of-network" door you can use at a high price.30  
* **Memory Hook:** **"HMO \= Home (Stay in network)"** vs. **"PPO \= Preferred (Pick anyone)"**.

## **Consumer Accounts: Critical Success Factors**

* **Must Know Cold:** HSA requires a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). FSA is "Use it or Lose it".7  
* **Secondary:** 2026 limits: FSA \= $3,400; HSA \= $4,400 (individual).7  
* **The Trap:** Saying an employer can contribute to an FSA but not an HSA. They can contribute to both, but only an HSA is portable.7  
* **Memory Hook:** **"HSA \= Health SAVINGS"** (Keep the money). **"FSA \= FLEXIBLE Spending"** (Spend it or lose it).

## **Medicare Supplement: Critical Success Factors**

* **Must Know Cold:** Plan A is mandatory for all insurers in Texas. The open enrollment window is 6 months.4  
* **Secondary:** Policies are guaranteed renewable. Cancellation for health is prohibited.35  
* **The Trap:** Thinking Medigap covers prescription drugs. It does NOT; that’s Medicare Part D.4  
* **Memory Hook:** **"Medigap fills the Gaps"** (A through N).

## **Long-Term Care: Critical Success Factors**

* **Must Know Cold:** The 6 ADLs. You need 2 to trigger benefits. Cognitive impairment also triggers.5  
* **Secondary:** The 30-day "Free Look" and the levels of care (Skilled, Intermediate, Custodial).5  
* **The Trap:** Confusing Custodial care with Skilled care. Custodial is non-medical (assistance with ADLs).5

## **SECTION 5: OFFICIAL RULES AND AUTHORITIES**

1. **Texas Insurance Code (TIC) Chapter 843:** This is the primary authority for Health Maintenance Organizations in Texas. It defines the COA requirement and member protections.24 *Directly testable.*  
2. **Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Title 28, Chapter 3, Subchapter S:** Establishes minimum standards for individual health insurance policies, including cancer, hospital indemnity, and disability income.47 *Directly testable.*  
3. **TAC Title 28, Chapter 3, Subchapter Y:** Governs Long-Term Care insurance in Texas, specifically the 6 ADLs and the "Free Look" requirements.5 *Directly testable.*  
4. **TAC Title 28, Chapter 3, Subchapter T:** Standardizes Medicare Supplement policies in Texas, ensuring Plan A is always offered.4 *Directly testable.*  
5. **TIC Chapter 1501 (Small Employer Health Benefit Plans):** Defines the "Small Employer" (2-50) and eligibility (30 hours/week) rules.39 *Directly testable.*  
6. **Internal Revenue Code (IRC) Section 223:** Federal source for HSA and HDHP definitions and penalty structures. *Directly testable on the national portion.*

## **Top 10 Distinctions to Avoid Mixing Up**

1. **Deductible vs. Elimination Period:** Dollar cost (Medical) vs. Time wait (Disability).  
2. **BOE vs. Buyout:** Pays bills (Taxable) vs. Buys partner's share (Tax-free).  
3. **HMO vs. PPO:** Mandatory gatekeeper vs. No gatekeeper.  
4. **FSA vs. HSA:** Employer-owned (not portable) vs. Employee-owned (portable).  
5. **COBRA vs. State Continuation:** 20+ employees (18 mo) vs. 2-19 employees (9 mo).  
6. **Medigap vs. Medicare Advantage:** Supplemental coverage (Plans A-N) vs. Replacement of Original Medicare (Part C).  
7. **Principal Sum vs. Capital Sum:** 100% benefit (Death) vs. 50% benefit (Limb).  
8. **Skilled Care vs. Custodial Care:** Medical professional needed vs. Help with ADLs only.  
9. **Accidental Means vs. Accidental Result:** Intent of the act vs. Unintentional outcome.  
10. **Contributory vs. Non-contributory:** Employees pay part (75% participation) vs. Employer pays all (100% participation).

## **Top 10 Likely Multiple-Choice Traps**

1. **The "Salary" Trap:** Stating that BOE or Key Employee insurance covers the owner's salary.  
2. **The "Drug" Trap:** Suggesting a Medicare Supplement policy covers Part D drugs.  
3. **The "Medicaid" Trap:** Using Medicaid (poverty-based) as the answer for a senior citizen scenario.  
4. **The "Misconduct" Trap:** Claiming an employee fired for stealing (gross misconduct) is COBRA eligible.  
5. **The "LTC Level" Trap:** Identifying custodial care as medical treatment.  
6. **The "Tax Proportion" Trap:** Failing to tax a group disability benefit based on the employer's percentage of premium.  
7. **The "Texas AD\&D" Trap:** Denying a claim for an intentional jump with an unintentional broken leg.  
8. **The "Small Group" Trap:** Defining a small group as 1 to 50 employees (it must be at least 2).  
9. **The "HSA Penalty" Trap:** Stating the HSA penalty is 10% (it is 20%).  
10. **The "Short-term Medical" Trap:** Assuming a short-term plan must cover pre-existing conditions.

## **Concise One-Hour Review Summary**

If you only have one hour, focus on the "Tax and Trigger" points:

* **Taxation:** Memorize the matrix for Individual vs. Group vs. BOE vs. Buyout. Tax follows the premium payer.  
* **Triggers:** Know the 6 ADLs for LTC. Know that 2 ADLs or cognitive impairment triggers the benefits.  
* **Managed Care:** Contrast the HMO gatekeeper/referral rule against the PPO flexibility.  
* **COBRA:** Remember 20/18 (Federal) and 2/9 (Texas).  
* **Medicare Supplement:** Know Plan A is the mandatory Texas minimum and the 6-month open enrollment window.

# Texas GL Life, Accident & Health – Domain I: Types of Policies (Life)

## SECTION 1: DOMAIN SNAPSHOT

### What this domain covers

Domain I tests product knowledge of life insurance and annuities: how different policy types work, what problems they solve, and how they compare. It includes traditional whole life, interest‑sensitive and variable/adjustable products, term life types and features, individual annuity structures, and combination/joint life designs. The emphasis is on conceptual understanding (structure, guarantees, risk, cash value, and taxation basics), not detailed state‑law procedures.[^1][^2][^3]

### Why it is heavily tested

The Pearson VUE Texas outline assigns 15 scored questions (about 19 percent of the Life portion) to “Types of Policies,” making it one of the largest single domains. Product knowledge drives most other life‑insurance topics (provisions, riders, suitability, replacement, taxation), so exam writers use this domain to check whether candidates can recognize which product fits a scenario and how it behaves over time. Misunderstanding product mechanics is a primary cause of consumer complaints that TDI addresses in its life and annuity guides, reinforcing why regulators and exam writers stress this area.[^4][^2][^3][^5][^1]

### Highest‑yield subtopics

- Term life types (level, decreasing, annually renewable, return of premium) and features (renewable, convertible).[^2][^3]
- Whole life vs universal life, including guarantees vs flexibility and how cash value and premiums behave.[^3]
- Variable products (variable whole, variable universal) vs fixed/universal products—who bears investment risk and licensing requirements.[^4][^3]
- Indexed life and indexed annuities—fixed products whose interest is tied to an index but with floors and caps.[^6][^4]
- Annuity structures: single vs flexible premium; immediate vs deferred; fixed vs variable vs indexed; accumulation vs payout phases; standard payout options (life only, life with period certain, joint and survivor, fixed amount).[^7][^4]
- Joint life vs survivorship (first‑to‑die vs second‑to‑die) and typical estate/wealth planning uses.[^2]

### Most commonly confused areas

- Ordinary whole life vs limited‑pay vs single‑premium: same permanent chassis but different premium timing and tax treatment as “modified endowment contracts” (MECs) for single premium.[^3][^2]
- Whole life vs universal life: fixed, guaranteed structure vs flexible, interest‑sensitive structure.[^3]
- Universal life vs indexed life vs interest‑sensitive whole life: all interest‑driven but with different crediting mechanisms and flexibility.[^8][^9][^3]
- Variable whole vs variable universal: both use separate accounts and are securities; key difference is premium/death‑benefit flexibility and the “universal” chassis.[^10][^4][^3]
- Term vs whole life and term subtypes (level, decreasing, ART/annually renewable, ROP/return of premium) including which have cash value (only ROP, and even then limited).[^2][^3]
- Annuity types: fixed vs indexed vs variable; immediate vs deferred; single vs flexible premium; payout options (life only vs life with period certain vs joint and survivor).[^6][^4]


## SECTION 2: DEEP EXPLANATION BY SUBTOPIC

### A. Traditional whole life products

#### 1. Ordinary whole life

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Ordinary whole life is permanent life insurance with level premiums, guaranteed death benefit, and guaranteed cash value that lasts for the insured’s entire life as long as premiums are paid. It is sometimes called “straight life” or “continuous premium” whole life.[^3]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Provides lifetime coverage for final expenses, income replacement, estate liquidity, or legacy goals where the client wants guaranteed benefits and stable premiums.[^3]

3. **Who it is designed for**  
People needing lifelong coverage and who can afford higher, level premiums—often families wanting a permanent base of coverage, small‑business owners for key person or buy‑sell layers, and clients focused on guaranteed cash value accumulation.

4. **How it generally works**  
The insurer charges a level premium based on conservative assumptions about mortality, interest, and expenses; part of each premium covers current insurance cost, and the rest builds a cash value that grows at a guaranteed rate inside the policy. Coverage remains in force for life (typically to age 100 or beyond) if premiums are paid.[^3]

5. **Premium structure**  
Premiums are fixed and payable for life (or until the maturity age) under an ordinary whole life contract. They are higher than term premiums at younger ages because they pre‑fund lifetime guarantees.[^3]

6. **Cash value treatment**  
A portion of each premium is credited to the cash value account, which grows at a guaranteed minimum rate; some policies also pay nonguaranteed dividends. Cash value can be borrowed or surrendered; loans reduce death benefit and may be taxable if the policy lapses.[^3]

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Pays a guaranteed level death benefit to the beneficiary at the insured’s death, generally income‑tax free under Internal Revenue Code section 101. If loans exist, the insurer subtracts outstanding loan plus interest before paying.[^11][^3]

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
The insurer bears investment risk and guarantees the minimum interest and death benefit; policyowner does not choose investments.[^3]

9. **Tax treatment (exam‑relevant)**  
- Death benefit is excluded from beneficiary’s gross income if paid by reason of death, subject to transfer‑for‑value and employer‑owned exceptions.[^11]
- Cash value grows tax‑deferred; withdrawals/loans are generally treated as first‑in‑first‑out (premiums come out before gains) for non‑MEC policies.[^3]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: lifelong guaranteed coverage, level premiums, guaranteed cash value, potential dividends, and tax‑deferred accumulation.[^3]
- Disadvantages: much higher initial cost than term, relatively inflexible once issued, and slow cash value buildup in early years.[^3]

11. **What makes it different**  
Unlike term, it has cash value and lifetime duration; unlike universal or variable policies, its premiums, death benefit, and cash‑value growth pattern are guaranteed and not adjustable.[^3]

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
TDI requires standard policy provisions such as entire contract, grace period, incontestability, and cash‑value/nonforfeiture terms for individual life policies. Texas consumer materials emphasize guarantees and slow early cash‑value growth to warn buyers against unrealistic illustrations, a likely exam angle.[^12][^13][^3]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Calling ordinary whole life “temporary” coverage.  
- Saying premiums increase each year—on exam, they are level.  
- Confusing guaranteed cash value (whole life) with no cash value (pure term) or interest‑sensitive crediting (universal life).

***

#### 2. Limited‑pay life

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Limited‑pay life is whole life insurance where premiums are paid only for a limited period (for example, 10‑pay, 20‑pay, or to age 65), but coverage remains in force for the insured’s entire life.[^2][^3]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Allows a client to fully pay up a permanent policy during high‑earning years (or before retirement) while still guaranteeing lifetime coverage.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
People who want permanent coverage but dislike paying premiums in retirement; business owners wanting to load premiums during profitable years; parents/grandparents funding policies for children.

4. **How it generally works**  
Premiums are compressed into a shorter payment period, so each payment is higher than for ordinary whole life; after the pay‑period, the policy is “paid‑up” and stays in force using built‑in values and guarantees.[^2][^3]

5. **Premium structure**  
Level but higher premiums during the limited period; no premiums due afterward.

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Cash value accumulates faster than ordinary whole life because of higher early funding; otherwise behaves like whole life (guaranteed schedule plus possible dividends).[^3]

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Same as ordinary whole life—guaranteed level death benefit for life as long as the policy remains in force.[^3]

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
Company bears investment risk; guaranteed interest schedule like other non‑participating, fixed whole life.[^3]

9. **Tax treatment**  
Same as non‑MEC whole life as long as it is not funded aggressively enough to become a MEC under federal tests; limited‑pay designs are closer to MEC thresholds but exam questions usually just contrast with ordinary whole life.[^11][^3]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: paid‑up coverage by a target date, accelerated cash‑value buildup, lifetime guarantees.  
- Disadvantages: significantly higher required premiums during limited‑pay period; risk of lapse if client cannot sustain payments.

11. **What makes it different**  
Key distinction from ordinary whole life is premium payment duration, not length of coverage (coverage is still for life).  

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
Limited‑pay whole life must still comply with Texas standards for nonforfeiture and cash‑value disclosures; in practice, this matters more in product filing than in exam questions.[^13][^12]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Treating limited‑pay as term coverage ending when premium payments end.  
- Calling it “short‑term” insurance.  
- Ignoring that cash value and death benefit remain after the premium period.

***

#### 3. Single‑premium life

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Single‑premium life is whole life insurance paid for with one large, lump‑sum premium; the policy is fully funded at issue. It is usually classified as a modified endowment contract (MEC) for tax purposes.[^2]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
One‑time funding creates immediate, fully paid‑up death benefit and cash value—useful for estate liquidity, wealth transfer, or repositioning low‑yield assets into a life‑insurance chassis.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Clients with significant liquid assets (CDs, savings, inheritances) who want to maximize tax‑favored death benefits and are less focused on income‑tax‑favored access to cash values because of MEC rules.

4. **How it generally works**  
The client pays one premium; the insurer issues a permanent policy with immediate cash value close to the premium and a guaranteed death benefit; policy values grow over time according to the product’s guarantees and dividends, if participating.

5. **Premium structure**  
Single, up‑front payment; no further premiums due.

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Substantial immediate cash value (often close to the premium minus charges), growing at guaranteed and possibly excess/dividend rates; access via withdrawals or loans is subject to MEC tax rules (LIFO taxation and possible 10 percent penalty before age 59½).[^11]

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Death benefit is generally income‑tax free to the beneficiary under IRC section 101, regardless of MEC status.[^11]

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
Depends on chassis: most exam references assume a traditional fixed whole‑life chassis where insurer bears investment risk and guarantees minimum interest.[^2]

9. **Tax treatment**  
For exam purposes, the key is that a single‑premium life policy almost always fails the federal 7‑pay test and is treated as a MEC; as a result, distributions (withdrawals/loans) are taxed on a last‑in‑first‑out basis and may incur a 10 percent penalty if taken before age 59½, even though the death benefit remains income‑tax free.[^11]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: one‑and‑done premium, immediate paid‑up coverage, strong death‑benefit leverage, and tax‑deferred growth.  
- Disadvantages: MEC tax treatment, reduced liquidity, and concentration risk if a large portion of liquid assets goes into a single policy.

11. **What makes it different**  
Distinguished from limited‑pay and ordinary whole life by its single lump‑sum premium and MEC implications; it is the most “front‑loaded” version of whole life.

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
Texas exempts many life‑insurance benefits (including annuities) from creditors and bankruptcy proceedings, which can make single‑premium policies attractive as protected assets, but this is more practical than exam‑focused.[^14][^3]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Treating single‑premium life as non‑MEC.  
- Saying distributions are taxed like ordinary whole life (FIFO).  
- Confusing single‑premium life with a single‑premium annuity (no death benefit focus).


### B. Interest/market‑sensitive/adjustable life products

#### 1. Universal life (UL)

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Universal life is a flexible‑premium, adjustable‑benefit permanent life policy in which premiums, expenses, and the cost of insurance are “unbundled,” and cash value earns interest at a credited rate subject to a guaranteed minimum.[^8][^3]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Provides lifetime coverage with flexible premiums and adjustable death benefit, suitable for people whose income or insurance needs change over time.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Clients who want permanent coverage but need flexibility in when and how much they pay, such as self‑employed individuals, business owners, or those expecting income fluctuations.

4. **How it generally works**  
Premiums go into a cash‑value account; insurer deducts monthly charges (cost of insurance and expenses) and credits interest at a declared rate not lower than a guaranteed minimum; if premiums paid plus interest fall short of charges, the policy draws from cash value and can eventually lapse. Death benefit and premiums can be adjusted within contract limits.[^3]

5. **Premium structure**  
Flexible: policyowner can vary premium timing and amount (subject to minimums to keep the policy in force); some policies include a “no‑lapse guarantee” that requires specific premium payments to keep coverage even if cash value is insufficient.[^3]

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Cash value accumulates based on credited interest minus charges; annual reports show projected duration of coverage depending on current cash value, cost of insurance, and assumed interest. Policyowners must monitor statements.[^3]

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Policies often offer two basic options: a level death benefit (face amount) or an increasing option (face amount plus cash value); changing options usually requires underwriting and affects cost of insurance.

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
Interest crediting is tied to the insurer’s portfolio performance and declared rates; the company guarantees a minimum interest rate but can change current crediting rates, and the policyowner bears the risk that low rates will require higher premiums to sustain coverage.[^8][^3]

9. **Tax treatment**  
Tax rules are similar to other non‑MEC life policies: death benefit generally income‑tax free, and cash value grows tax‑deferred; policyowner can usually take withdrawals up to basis without tax and borrow against the rest if the policy is not a MEC.[^11][^3]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: premium and death‑benefit flexibility, potential for higher credited interest than traditional whole life, and tax‑deferred growth.[^3]
- Disadvantages: complexity, need for ongoing management, and lapse risk if underfunded or if interest rates fall; benefits and cash value are less guaranteed than in whole life.[^8][^3]

11. **What makes it different**  
Compared with whole life, UL explicitly separates premiums, cost of insurance, and interest crediting and allows flexible premiums and adjustable death benefits; compared with variable UL, crediting is tied to declared interest rather than separate account investment performance.[^3]

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
TDI’s universal life checklist requires provisions on guaranteed minimum interest rate, maximum cost of insurance rates, grace period (minimum 60 days), and clear cash‑surrender value definitions, highlighting regulators’ focus on non‑guaranteed elements and consumer disclosure.[^8]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Treating UL premiums as fixed and non‑adjustable like whole life.  
- Saying cash value is guaranteed like whole life.  
- Ignoring lapse risk when premiums are insufficient to cover charges.

***

#### 2. Variable whole life

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Variable whole life is permanent life insurance where cash values and death benefits vary with the performance of investment subaccounts (separate accounts) chosen by the policyowner; it keeps a whole‑life premium structure but shifts investment risk to the policyowner.[^10][^3]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Designed for long‑term protection plus growth potential for cash value and possibly death benefit, for clients comfortable with market volatility.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Younger or more investment‑oriented clients who want permanent insurance and are willing to accept market risk inside the policy.

4. **How it generally works**  
Premiums (often fixed) are allocated to separate accounts investing in stocks, bonds, or similar vehicles; policy values and sometimes death benefits rise or fall with investment performance, subject to a guaranteed minimum death benefit in many designs.[^10]

5. **Premium structure**  
Typically level, similar to ordinary whole life, though some designs permit limited flexibility; premiums are generally higher than term but comparable to or slightly above traditional whole life because of separate account costs.

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Cash value is not guaranteed; it depends entirely on separate‑account performance, net of charges. Poor investment performance can reduce cash value significantly.[^10]

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Most contracts guarantee a minimum death benefit but allow higher benefits if separate‑account values perform well; death benefit usually remains income‑tax free to beneficiaries.[^10][^11]

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
Policyowner chooses among subaccounts and bears investment risk; no guaranteed interest rate applies to separate accounts.[^4][^10]

9. **Tax treatment**  
Still treated as life insurance under section 101: death benefit excludable from income, cash value growth tax‑deferred; trading among subaccounts does not cause current taxation.[^11]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: upside potential for cash value and death benefit, tax‑deferred market growth inside the policy.  
- Disadvantages: risk of poor performance and reduced values, complexity, higher fees, and suitability concerns for conservative clients.[^4][^10]

11. **What makes it different**  
Compared to traditional whole life, both premiums and guarantees are less rigid: investment risk shifts to policyowner; compared to variable universal life, premiums are generally not flexible, and the policy does not have the same transparent unbundling as UL.

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
TDI and the SEC jointly regulate variable products; Texas requires agents selling variable life or annuities to hold both a state insurance license and a federal securities registration with FINRA.[^4][^3]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Saying variable whole life guarantees cash value.  
- Ignoring securities‑licensing requirement.  
- Confusing variable whole life with variable universal life (VUL) regarding premium flexibility.

***

#### 3. Variable universal life (VUL)

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
VUL combines universal life’s flexible premiums and adjustable death benefit with variable products’ separate‑account investing; cash value and death benefit vary with investment performance.[^10][^3]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Offers maximum flexibility and growth potential for sophisticated clients who want control over funding and investments.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Investment‑savvy, higher‑income clients comfortable with market risk and ongoing management of premium funding and subaccount allocation.

4. **How it generally works**  
Premiums flow into a separate‑account cash‑value pool; insurer deducts monthly charges (cost of insurance and expenses) from cash value; the owner chooses subaccounts, and policy values rise or fall with performance; death benefit and premium patterns can be adjusted within contract constraints.

5. **Premium structure**  
Flexible: owner can vary payment amounts and timing as long as there is enough cash value to cover charges; underfunding can cause lapse even without missed formal premiums.

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Fully market‑driven; no guaranteed cash value; poor performance can exhaust cash value quickly, especially if charges are high.[^10]

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Usually at least a guaranteed minimum death benefit if premiums meet specified funding patterns; otherwise, death benefit can vary and may decrease if cash value shrinks significantly.

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
Owner bears all investment risk; no guaranteed crediting rate in separate accounts; insurer may guarantee only administrative features (e.g., no‑lapse riders) if funding rules are met.[^10]

9. **Tax treatment**  
Same basic tax treatment as other life insurance: tax‑deferred growth and generally income‑tax‑free death benefits under section 101, subject to MEC rules if overfunded.[^11]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: investment choice, potential for high growth, flexible premiums and death benefits, tax‑deferred accumulation.  
- Disadvantages: high risk of lapse or poor long‑term performance if underfunded or markets decline; complexity and higher internal costs; suitability scrutiny.

11. **What makes it different**  
Combines the universal life chassis (flexible premiums, adjustable DB, explicit monthly charges) with variable investing; compared with variable whole life, VUL offers flexibility but fewer guarantees.[^10][^3]

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
As with variable whole life, Texas requires both an insurance license and FINRA securities registration for agents selling VUL; TDI coordinates with the State Securities Board regarding such products.[^4]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Treating VUL as having guaranteed minimum cash values or interest.  
- Ignoring that policy can lapse when cash value is depleted even if “scheduled” premiums were paid earlier.  
- Confusing securities‑licensing requirements with fixed UL.

***

#### 4. Interest‑sensitive whole life (current‑assumption whole life)

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Interest‑sensitive whole life—also called current‑assumption whole life—is permanent insurance whose cash values and sometimes premiums are based on current interest‑rate assumptions rather than entirely fixed, traditional tables, while still providing guarantees similar to whole life.[^9][^8]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Aims to offer permanent coverage with potential for better cash‑value performance than traditional whole life when interest rates are favorable, without full flexibility of UL.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Clients who like the basic whole‑life structure but are willing to accept some interest‑rate variability to potentially reduce premiums or increase cash values.

4. **How it generally works**  
Premiums are often still scheduled or level, but the insurer credits interest to cash value based on current portfolio yields; if actual interest is higher than guaranteed, the company may use this to support lower required premiums or higher cash values.[^9][^8]

5. **Premium structure**  
Premiums may be adjustable within limits (e.g., reduced or skipped if current interest is strong) but are not as flexible as universal life; TDI treats certain interest‑sensitive designs similarly to UL for filing purposes.[^8]

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Cash value has both guaranteed minimums and current, interest‑sensitive projections; if interest rates fall, projected cash values can be lower than illustrated.[^9][^8]

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Death benefit is usually guaranteed like whole life, with the cash‑value volatility affecting internal funding rather than nominal face amount.

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
Insurer bears investment risk but passes through some interest‑rate changes to policyowners in the form of changing credited interest or required premiums; non‑guaranteed elements must be disclosed under Texas law for policies with non‑guaranteed charges.[^15]

9. **Tax treatment**  
Same as other non‑MEC permanent life: tax‑deferred cash value and income‑tax‑free death benefit.[^11]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: potentially lower premiums or higher cash values than traditional whole life in strong interest‑rate environments, with underlying guarantees.  
- Disadvantages: projections may not materialize; risk of disappointment if interest falls—similar consumer concerns led regulators to tighten disclosure rules.[^15][^8]

11. **What makes it different**  
Sits between traditional whole life and UL: has more sensitivity to current interest than whole life but not the full flexibility and transparency of UL; exam questions may refer to it as “current‑assumption whole life.”

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
The universal life filing checklist explicitly notes it may also be used for interest‑sensitive whole life, showing that Texas treats these policies similarly for regulatory purposes regarding guaranteed interest, cost of insurance, and surrender‑value definitions.[^8]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Calling it the same as universal life.  
- Treating all illustrated values as guaranteed.  
- Ignoring that premiums can change based on interest‑rate experience.

***

#### 5. Indexed life (usually indexed universal life, IUL)

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Indexed life—most often indexed universal life (IUL)—is a permanent policy whose interest crediting is tied partly to a market index (such as the S&P 500) but with limits such as caps, participation rates, and floors; it is legally a type of fixed policy, not a security.[^6][^8]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Targets permanent coverage with potential for index‑linked upside while protecting against negative index returns through a guaranteed minimum credited interest.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Clients seeking growth potential linked to equity indexes but unwilling to accept full market downside, and who can commit to long‑term funding.

4. **How it generally works**  
Premiums go into a UL‑style cash‑value account; each crediting period, the insurer calculates interest based on index changes, then applies participation rates (percentage of index gain) and caps (maximum credited rate) along with a floor (often zero) so that credited interest cannot be negative; the insurer invests in its general account and uses options strategies rather than directly purchasing the index.[^6]

5. **Premium structure**  
Flexible premiums similar to UL, subject to minimums to keep the policy in force; some designs include no‑lapse guarantees requiring specific premium funding patterns.

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Cash value earns index‑linked interest subject to caps, spreads, and floors; if the index is flat or negative, the floor prevents losses in the credited interest but policy charges can still erode value.[^6]

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
IUL usually offers level or increasing death‑benefit options like UL; death benefit may grow over time if cash value grows, depending on option selected.

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
Formally a fixed product: the insurer, not the policyowner, chooses investments; policyowner bears the risk that credited interest may be low due to caps/participation formulas; no direct market losses, but poor index results or high charges can still cause lapse.[^6]

9. **Tax treatment**  
Same as other permanent life: tax‑deferred cash‑value growth and generally income‑tax‑free death benefit, assuming policy meets life‑insurance definition and is not disqualified by MEC rules.[^11]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: principal protection at the credited‑interest level (floor) with index‑linked upside, flexible funding, and tax‑deferred accumulation.[^6]
- Disadvantages: complex formulas (caps, participation, spreads), potential for low credited interest even in moderate markets, high fees, and strong illustration assumptions that may not hold.[^6]

11. **What makes it different**  
Unlike fixed UL, crediting is tied to an external index; unlike variable or VUL, it remains a fixed general‑account product with no direct investment in securities and typically no negative index‑linked returns.

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
Texas annuity and life‑product disclosures emphasize understanding index‑linked formulas, caps, floors, and surrender conditions, reflecting regulatory concerns over consumer misunderstanding of indexed features.[^4][^6]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Treating indexed life as a security like variable life.  
- Saying the policy can lose cash value due directly to negative index performance (the credited interest floor is usually zero, though charges can still reduce value).  
- Ignoring caps/participation limits when describing potential returns.


### C. Term life

#### 1. Types of term

##### a. Level term

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Level term is temporary life insurance with a fixed death benefit and a level premium for a specified term (for example, 10, 20, or 30 years).[^3]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Provides inexpensive, predictable coverage for a defined period, such as years with a mortgage, young children, or income‑replacement needs.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Younger families, people with temporary obligations, and cost‑sensitive buyers wanting maximum death benefit per premium dollar.

4. **How it generally works**  
If the insured dies during the term, the beneficiary receives the stated death benefit; if the insured survives the term and coverage is not renewed or converted, the coverage ends and there is usually no value.

5. **Premium structure**  
Premiums stay level during the guaranteed term; if the contract is renewable after the initial term, renewed premiums increase based on the insured’s attained age.[^3]

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Standard level term has no cash value; all premium pays for pure insurance cost.

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Pays a level death benefit during the term, generally income‑tax free to the beneficiary under section 101.[^11]

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
No cash value or investment component; no investment risk for owner.

9. **Tax treatment**  
No tax on premiums or internal buildup since there is no cash value; death benefit exclusion under section 101 applies.[^11]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: lowest cost per dollar of coverage, simplicity, predictable protection during the term.[^3]
- Disadvantages: no cash value, premiums may become very high after the level period if renewed, and coverage eventually ends.

11. **What makes it different**  
Compared with whole or UL, level term has no savings element and is not designed to last for life.

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
TDI consumer materials emphasize that term is best for temporary needs and highlight conversion options to permanent coverage.[^3]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Implying that level premiums continue unchanged if renewed beyond the initial term.  
- Claiming term builds cash value (it does not).

***

##### b. Decreasing term

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Decreasing term features a death benefit that declines over time while the premium typically remains level. It is often used to match a declining obligation such as a mortgage.[^2]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Covers debts or obligations that shrink over time so that full initial coverage is not needed later.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Borrowers with amortizing loans, such as home mortgages, or businesses with declining obligations.

4. **How it generally works**  
If the insured dies early in the term, the full initial face amount (per schedule) is paid; as time passes, the policy’s death benefit steadily declines according to a predetermined schedule.

5. **Premium structure**  
Typically level premiums throughout the term despite decreasing benefit.

6. **Cash value treatment**  
No cash value; pure term coverage.

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Decreasing death benefit over time, generally designed to be zero at the end of the term.

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
No investment component.

9. **Tax treatment**  
Same as other term coverage: death benefit generally income‑tax free; no cash value, so no tax on buildup.[^11]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: can be cheaper than level term for the same initial face amount since benefit declines; matches certain obligations.  
- Disadvantages: shrinking protection, no flexibility if needs later increase.

11. **What makes it different**  
Unlike level term, the benefit decreases; on exam, often contrasted with level term in mortgage‑protection scenarios.

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
Shows up primarily in consumer education about credit and mortgage life; not heavily emphasized in TDI guides but standard in exam outlines.[^2]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Confusing decreasing term with decreasing premium (premiums typically stay level).  
- Assuming it is convertible for the full initial face amount late in the term (conversions usually follow remaining face).

***

##### c. Return of premium (ROP) term

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
ROP term is level term insurance in which, if the insured survives the term and keeps the policy in force, the insurer refunds some or all of the premiums paid.[^2]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Appeals to people who dislike “wasting” premiums on term—offering a refund if they outlive the term while still providing term protection.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Cost‑sensitive buyers willing to pay higher premiums in exchange for eventual premium refund and modest forced savings.

4. **How it generally works**  
If the insured dies during the term, the beneficiary receives the death benefit; if the insured survives the full term, the insurer returns premiums per contract formula; if the policy lapses or is surrendered early, the refund is reduced or zero.

5. **Premium structure**  
Premiums are substantially higher than comparable level term to fund the refund feature.[^2]

6. **Cash value treatment**  
Some ROP terms accumulate a limited cash value or nonforfeiture value that supports the refund; however, they are still primarily treated as term products and not standard permanent policies.

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Standard level term death benefit during the term, income‑tax free to beneficiaries; the premium refund at maturity is generally not taxable to the policyowner if it does not exceed premiums paid (return of basis).[^11]

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
No explicit investment choices; implicit internal rate of return on refunded premiums is usually modest.

9. **Tax treatment**  
Refund of premiums is normally treated as a return of the insured’s own after‑tax payments and not taxable income; exam questions tend to focus on cost/benefit trade‑offs rather than tax details.

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: psychological appeal of getting premiums back if still alive; still provides term protection.  
- Disadvantages: considerably higher premiums, opportunity cost of tying money up at a low implied return.

11. **What makes it different**  
Unique feature is the premium refund if the insured survives; still term, not permanent, even though there may be a cash value component.

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
Not singled out in TDI consumer guides; tested as a variant of term with a refund feature.[^2]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Treating ROP term as permanent insurance.  
- Forgetting that premiums are higher than for standard level term.  
- Assuming refund occurs if policy is surrendered or lapses early.

***

##### d. Annually renewable term (ART)

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
ART is term insurance that renews each year with a one‑year term and a premium that increases annually based on the insured’s attained age.[^2]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Provides short‑term, flexible coverage where the insured may need only a brief period of protection or expects to convert to permanent coverage soon.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
People needing very short‑term coverage or bridge coverage before longer‑term planning, or group term plans that reset annually.

4. **How it generally works**  
Policy is issued for one year; the insured can renew each year up to a maximum age without new underwriting; premiums increase annually.

5. **Premium structure**  
Premiums start very low but rise each year as age increases; long‑term, this becomes more expensive than level term.

6. **Cash value treatment**  
No cash value.

7. **Death benefit treatment**  
Death benefit remains level (per contract) during each one‑year term.

8. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
No investment component.

9. **Tax treatment**  
Same as other term policies; death benefit generally excluded from income.[^11]

10. **Advantages and disadvantages**  
- Advantages: very low initial cost, guaranteed renewability without evidence of insurability within limits.  
- Disadvantages: premiums become high at older ages; not ideal for long‑term needs.

11. **What makes it different**  
Unique feature is year‑by‑year renewability and annually increasing premiums.

12. **Texas‑specific angle**  
Shows up mostly in exam questions contrasting level vs annually increasing premiums and discussing renewable features.[^2]

13. **Most likely exam traps**  
- Misstating that premiums stay level.  
- Confusing ART with non‑renewable one‑year term.

***

#### 2. Special term features

##### a. Renewable

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
A renewable term provision allows the policyowner to extend the policy for additional terms without evidence of insurability, typically at higher premiums based on attained age.[^3]

2. **Problem it solves / who it is for**  
Protects against losing coverage if the insured’s health deteriorates by guaranteeing the right to continue coverage.

3. **How it works and exam focus**  
Renewal is guaranteed during the specified renewable period; premiums at renewal are based on age at renewal (attained age), not original age; no new medical exam is required.[^3]

4. **Likely exam traps**  
- Saying renewal premiums are based on original age.  
- Confusing renewable feature with convertible feature.

***

##### b. Convertible

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
A convertible term provision lets the policyowner exchange a term policy for a permanent policy (whole life or UL) without evidence of insurability.[^3]

2. **Problem it solves / who it is for**  
Allows a client who starts with low‑cost term to later obtain permanent coverage even if health has declined.

3. **How it works and exam focus**  
Conversion may be based on attained age or original age, depending on the policy; conversion must occur within a stated time or before a specific age; new premiums will be higher because permanent coverage is more expensive.[^3]

4. **Likely exam traps**  
- Claiming conversion requires a new medical exam.  
- Failing to note that premiums for the new permanent policy are higher and depend on age (attained or original per contract).


### D. Annuities

#### 1. Single premium vs flexible premium

1. **Plain‑English definitions**  
- Single‑premium annuity: funded by one lump‑sum contribution at the start.  
- Flexible‑premium annuity: allows multiple contributions over time during the accumulation phase.[^4]

2. **Financial/planning problems solved**  
Single‑premium annuities convert a lump sum (e.g., inheritance, rollover) into future income or tax‑deferred growth; flexible‑premium annuities help people save gradually for retirement.

3. **Who they are designed for**  
Single‑premium: retirees or individuals with large, one‑time sums; flexible‑premium: workers contributing regularly from earnings.

4. **How they work**  
Both accumulate value tax‑deferred; funding pattern differs (one‑time vs ongoing contributions). When annuitized, both follow the same payout mechanics.[^4]

5. **Tax treatment (exam‑relevant)**  
Earnings grow tax‑deferred; when paid out, a portion is excluded from income as return of principal and the rest taxed as ordinary income under the exclusion‑ratio rules of IRC section 72 and related regulations.[^16][^17]

6. **Likely exam traps**  
- Confusing single‑premium annuity with single‑premium life insurance.  
- Saying flexible‑premium annuities are “short‑term” products.

***

#### 2. Immediate vs deferred annuities

1. **Plain‑English definitions**  
- Immediate annuity: income payments start within one year of purchase.[^4]
- Deferred annuity: has an accumulation phase; income begins at a future date, often years later.[^4]

2. **Problems solved / design**  
Immediate annuities solve the problem of converting a lump sum into guaranteed income now; deferred annuities solve long‑term savings and future‑income needs.

3. **How they work**  
Immediate: one premium followed quickly by periodic payments for life or a set period; deferred: premiums accumulate with tax‑deferred growth, then the contract is annuitized (or taken as withdrawals) later.[^4]

4. **Likely exam traps**  
- Stating that a deferred annuity must always be annuitized (many are never annuitized and instead used for withdrawals).  
- Saying an immediate annuity has a long accumulation phase.

***

#### 3. Fixed vs variable annuities

1. **Plain‑English definitions**  
- Fixed annuity: insurer guarantees a minimum interest rate and a fixed payout; insurer bears investment risk.[^6][^4]
- Variable annuity: contract value and income vary with underlying separate‑account investments chosen by the owner; owner bears investment risk; classified as a security.[^10][^4]

2. **Problems solved / who they fit**  
Fixed suits conservative investors seeking stability; variable suits investors seeking growth and able to tolerate market risk.

3. **How they work**  
Fixed: insurer invests in general account and credits at least a minimum interest rate; variable: premiums purchase accumulation units in separate accounts, with values fluctuating based on market performance.[^6][^4]

4. **Tax treatment**  
Both: tax‑deferred growth; distributions taxed according to exclusion‑ratio rules; for nonqualified annuities, early withdrawals typically taxed LIFO and may incur a 10 percent penalty before age 59½.[^17][^16]

5. **Texas‑specific angle**  
Texas treats variable annuities as securities; agents must hold both a state insurance license and FINRA registration; TDI and SEC jointly regulate companies selling them.[^4]

6. **Likely exam traps**  
- Saying fixed annuities can lose principal due to poor investment performance (insurer guarantees minimum value, though surrender charges and early withdrawals can reduce what the owner receives).  
- Treating variable annuities as non‑securities requiring no securities registration.

***

#### 4. Indexed annuities

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Indexed or “index‑linked” annuities are fixed deferred annuities whose interest credited is tied to a market index (e.g., S&P 500), subject to floors, caps, participation rates, and spreads.[^6][^4]

2. **Financial/planning problem**  
Appeal to people wanting some market‑linked upside but protection against downside losses.

3. **Who they are designed for**  
Near‑retirees and conservative investors looking for more potential interest than traditional fixed annuities while still avoiding direct market losses.

4. **How they work**  
The insurer credits interest using an indexing method (e.g., annual point‑to‑point), applies a participation rate (portion of index gain), rate caps (maximum credited rate), and possibly margins/spreads; most contracts guarantee that credited index interest will not be negative and guarantee minimum contract values.[^6][^4]

5. **Investment‑risk/interest‑crediting**  
Insurer invests in general account assets and options; owner does not own index securities; index losses do not typically reduce principal, but low or zero credited interest plus fees can erode real value over time.[^6][^4]

6. **Tax treatment**  
Same as other deferred annuities: earnings tax‑deferred until withdrawn; early withdrawals generally taxable on earnings first and may incur 10 percent penalty.[^16][^17]

7. **Texas‑specific angle**  
TDI’s annuity guide stresses understanding indexed‑annuity formulas, caps, floors, and surrender features, and emphasizes the 20‑day free‑look (30 days for replacements) for annuities sold in Texas.[^4]

8. **Likely exam traps**  
- Calling indexed annuities “variable” or securities.  
- Saying account value can be reduced by negative index returns (crediting floors usually prevent index‑driven losses, though charges and withdrawals still matter).

***

#### 5. Accumulation period vs annuity period

1. **Plain‑English definitions**  
- Accumulation period: time when annuity owner pays premiums and earnings grow.[^4]
- Annuity period (payout or annuitization phase): time when insurer makes periodic payments to annuitant.[^4]

2. **How they work**  
During accumulation, owner may make single, flexible, or fixed payments; during payout, insurer uses actuarial factors and interest assumptions to convert accumulated value into periodic income.

3. **Tax treatment**  
Accumulation: earnings tax‑deferred; payout: each payment is partly nontaxable return of principal and partly taxable earnings according to the exclusion ratio.[^17][^16]

4. **Likely exam traps**  
- Confusing accumulation with deferred status only; an immediate annuity effectively has almost no accumulation period.  
- Treating annuity period as optional once annuitization occurs (annuitization is generally irrevocable).

***

#### 6. Payout options

1. **Plain‑English definitions (TDI)**[^7][^4]
- Fixed amount: company pays a fixed dollar amount for a specified period or until funds are exhausted.  
- Life‑only: payments for as long as annuitant lives, then stop—no payments to heirs.  
- Life with period certain: life income with a minimum guaranteed payout period; if annuitant dies before end of period, beneficiary receives payments for the remainder.  
- Joint and survivor: payments continue as long as at least one of two annuitants lives; amount may reduce after first death.

2. **Financial problems solved**  
Life‑only maximizes income but offers no legacy; period‑certain and joint‑and‑survivor reduce income in exchange for guarantees to beneficiaries or spouses.[^7]

3. **Tax treatment**  
All options follow the exclusion‑ratio rules: part of each payment is return of basis (not taxed), part is earnings (taxed as ordinary income); once basis is fully recovered, entire payment is taxable.[^16][^17]

4. **Likely exam traps**  
- Saying life‑only continues payments to heirs.  
- Confusing fixed amount with fixed period.  
- Ignoring that joint‑and‑survivor payments often reduce on second life.


### E. Combination plans and variations

#### 1. Joint life (first‑to‑die)

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Joint life (first‑to‑die) covers two or more lives on one contract and pays the death benefit at the first death; coverage then typically terminates.[^2]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Provides protection for the surviving spouse or business partner at the first death—often used for income replacement or business buy‑sell funding.

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Married couples with income dependence, and co‑owners in buy‑sell or key‑person arrangements.

4. **How it works**  
Single policy, one death benefit; premium is lower than buying two separate individual policies with the same total face amount because there is only one benefit paid.

5. **Likely exam traps**  
- Confusing first‑to‑die with second‑to‑die (survivorship) coverage.  
- Saying benefits are paid at the last death.

***

#### 2. Survivorship life (second‑to‑die)

1. **Plain‑English definition**  
Survivorship life (second‑to‑die) covers two lives and pays the death benefit only after the last insured dies.[^2]

2. **Financial/planning problem it solves**  
Used primarily for estate‑planning and wealth‑transfer strategies where liquidity is needed at the second death (for example, estate taxes or inheritance equalization).

3. **Who it is designed for**  
Affluent married couples or co‑owners focused on legacy/estate needs rather than income at the first death.

4. **How it works**  
Policy remains in force after the first death; premiums may continue or may be structured as paid‑up; benefit paid only after second death.

5. **Likely exam traps**  
- Treating survivorship life as paying at the first death.  
- Assuming it is primarily for income‑replacement at the survivor’s death instead of estate liquidity.

## SECTION 4: EXAM‑FOCUSED TAKEAWAYS

### Traditional whole life (ordinary, limited‑pay, single‑premium)

- **Know cold**  
  - Whole life = permanent, level premium, guaranteed cash value and death benefit. Ordinary = pay for life; limited‑pay = pay for shorter period, coverage for life; single‑premium = one lump‑sum, typically MEC.[^11][^2][^3]
  - Whole life vs term (permanent vs temporary; cash value vs none).  
- **Secondary but testable**  
  - Dividends are not guaranteed; options for use (cash, reduce premium, accumulate interest, buy paid‑up additions) show up more in provisions domain but can be cross‑tested.[^3]
  - Limited‑pay builds cash value faster; single‑premium life emphasizes MEC tax treatment.  
- **Low priority**  
  - Detailed dividend options and participating vs non‑participating mechanics—usually tested elsewhere.  
- **Likely wrong‑answer traps**  
  - “Premiums increase each year” on whole life.  
  - Statements that term builds guaranteed cash value.  
  - Single‑premium life described as non‑MEC with FIFO taxation.  
- **Memory hooks**  
  - *Ordinary* = “ordinary paycheck schedule” (pay every year for life).  
  - *Limited‑pay* = “pay more, pay sooner, paid‑up forever.”  
  - *Single‑premium* = “one and done, usually a MEC.”

### Universal, interest‑sensitive, indexed, and variable products

- **Know cold**  
  - Universal life = flexible premium, adjustable death benefit, interest‑sensitive cash value with minimum guaranteed interest.[^3]
  - Variable products (variable whole, VUL, variable annuities) use separate accounts, policyowner bears investment risk, and require securities + insurance licenses in Texas.[^4][^3]
  - Indexed life and indexed annuities are fixed products with index‑linked interest, caps, participation rates, and floors (often zero so no negative crediting).[^6][^4]
- **Secondary but testable**  
  - Interest‑sensitive whole life (current‑assumption) bridges traditional whole and UL.[^9][^8]
  - No‑lapse guarantees in UL: policy stays in force if special premium tests are met even when cash value is low.[^8][^3]
- **Low priority**  
  - Detailed formulas for participation rates, caps, and spreads beyond concept level.  
  - Technical details of non‑guaranteed charges disclosures (Subchapter E of TIC 1101) beyond recognizing that non‑guaranteed elements can change.[^15]
- **Likely wrong‑answer traps**  
  - Treating indexed products as securities.  
  - Claiming variable products guarantee principal or interest.  
  - Stating UL premiums are fixed like whole life.  
  - Confusing current illustrated interest in UL/IUL with guaranteed returns.  
- **Memory hooks**  
  - **UL = “You (U) Decide”**—you can flex premiums and death benefit.  
  - **Variable = “Varies with market”** and **“Verify (securities) license.”**  
  - **Indexed = “In‑between”** fixed and variable: index‑linked upside, floor for downside.

### Term life and term features

- **Know cold**  
  - Term types: level, decreasing, ART, ROP; none (except limited ROP value) have traditional cash value.[^2][^3]
  - Renewable: extend term without new medical exam, but premiums rise at attained age.[^3]
  - Convertible: switch from term to permanent without evidence of insurability, within a time/age limit.[^3]
- **Secondary but testable**  
  - ART vs level term cost patterns over time.  
  - Decreasing term’s use with mortgage debt.  
  - ROP term’s higher premiums and refund only if policy is kept to maturity.  
- **Low priority**  
  - Detailed premium tables; specific terms (e.g., 10 vs 20 years) are not tested as math problems.  
- **Likely wrong‑answer traps**  
  - “Renewable term premiums stay level.”  
  - “Conversion requires proof of insurability.”  
  - “Decreasing term has decreasing premiums.”  
- **Memory hooks**  
  - **Renewable = “Re‑up Same Health.”**  
  - **Convertible = “Change Coat, Same Body”**—term (coat) becomes permanent (coat), person (health) unchanged.  
  - **ART = “Age‑Rated Term”** (annual increases).

### Annuities

- **Know cold**  
  - Immediate vs deferred; single vs flexible premium; fixed vs variable vs indexed; accumulation vs payout phases.[^6][^4]
  - Annuity payout options: life only, life with period certain, joint and survivor, fixed amount.[^7][^4]
  - Tax basics: earnings tax‑deferred; distributions are partly taxable earnings and partly return of principal according to exclusion ratio; early withdrawals generally incur a 10 percent penalty on taxable portion.[^17][^16]
- **Secondary but testable**  
  - Texas suitability standards for annuity recommendations (Chapter 1115) and TDI’s emphasis on disclosure documents and buyer’s guides.[^18][^19]
  - Surrender charges and free‑withdrawal provisions (e.g., 10 percent per year), as highlighted in TDI’s annuity guide.[^4]
- **Low priority**  
  - Complex indexing formulas and exact surrender‑charge schedules; exam sticks to concepts.  
- **Likely wrong‑answer traps**  
  - “Variable annuities guarantee principal.”  
  - “Indexed annuities lose value when the index is negative” (crediting floor prevents index‑driven losses, though fees/withdrawals matter).[^6][^4]
  - “Annuity earnings are taxed as capital gains” (they are ordinary income).[^16][^17]
- **Memory hooks**  
  - **F‑V‑I: Fixed = Floor, Variable = Volatile, Indexed = In‑between.**  
  - **Now vs Later:** Immediate = income now; Deferred = income later.

### Combination plans (joint and survivorship)

- **Know cold**  
  - Joint (first‑to‑die) vs survivorship (second‑to‑die): timing of death benefit and typical use (income/buy‑sell vs estate liquidity).[^2]
- **Secondary but testable**  
  - Relative premium cost (second‑to‑die often cheaper for same total face amount because benefit is delayed).  
- **Low priority**  
  - Specific estate‑tax planning details and trust structures.  
- **Likely wrong‑answer traps**  
  - Swapping first‑to‑die and second‑to‑die definitions.  
  - Stating that both policies pay a benefit at each insured’s death.  
- **Memory hooks**  
  - **“Joint jumps at the first.”**  
  - **“Survivorship waits for the survivor.”**


## SECTION 5: OFFICIAL RULES AND AUTHORITIES

### Pearson VUE Texas Insurance Content Outlines

- **Source:** Texas Insurance Supplement – Examination Content Outlines (Pearson VUE), Life‑General Knowledge and Life & Health‑General Knowledge sections.[^2]
- **Why it matters:** Official exam blueprint specifying that “Types of Policies” (Life) constitutes 15 scored questions and listing each tested product type by name (ordinary whole life, limited‑pay, single‑premium, UL, variable, interest‑sensitive, indexed, term, annuities, joint, survivorship).  
- **Exam relevance:** Directly testable; guides what to prioritize.

### Texas Department of Insurance Life Insurance Consumer Guide

- **Source:** TDI “Shopping for Life Insurance” (Life Insurance Guide), including sections on types of life insurance, term vs permanent, whole life, universal life, and comparison table.[^3]
- **Why it matters:** Explains in plain language how term, whole, and universal life work; describes cash value, dividends, convertibility, and renewability; includes Texas‑specific notes (maturity ages, free‑look period, licensing requirements).[^3]
- **Exam relevance:** Highly testable conceptual content and some state‑specific nuances.

### TDI Annuities Guide (“Annuity Phases”)

- **Source:** TDI annuity guide covering types of annuities, fixed/index‑linked/variable structures, accumulation and payout phases, payout options, surrender charges, and free‑look rules.[^7][^4]
- **Why it matters:** Provides official definitions and explanations used by exam writers for annuity classification and payout options.  
- **Exam relevance:** Very testable for annuity types, payout options, Texas free‑look timeframes, and licensing/securities requirements.

### Texas Insurance Code – Life Insurance General Provisions

- **Source:** TIC Chapter 1101, including required policy provisions such as entire contract, grace period, incontestability, nonforfeiture standards, and settlement provisions.[^12]
- **Why it matters:** Establishes basic legal framework for individual life policies in Texas; cross‑supports understanding of whole‑life and permanent‑policy structures.  
- **Exam relevance:** Indirect for this domain; more directly tested under provisions/options/exclusions, but helps justify discussions about guaranteed values and standard clauses.

### Texas Insurance Code – Standard Nonforfeiture for Certain Annuities

- **Source:** TIC Chapter 1107, especially section 1107.003 on required nonforfeiture provisions and guaranteed minimum values.[^20]
- **Why it matters:** Sets minimum standards for annuity paid‑up values and cash‑surrender benefits when contributions stop.  
- **Exam relevance:** Mostly background; exam typically expects recognition that annuities must offer certain nonforfeiture benefits rather than detailed formulas.

### Texas Insurance Code – Suitability of Certain Annuity Transactions

- **Source:** TIC Chapter 1115 (Suitability of Certain Annuity Transactions).[^19]
- **Why it matters:** Requires that annuity recommendations match consumers’ insurance needs and financial objectives, a key regulatory theme around annuity products.  
- **Exam relevance:** Indirect but may be referenced in questions comparing product suitability; detailed suitability factors are more likely in later domains.

### Texas Insurance Code – Benefits Exempt from Creditors

- **Source:** TIC Chapter 1108 on exemption of life‑insurance and annuity benefits from seizure by creditors and in bankruptcy proceedings.[^14]
- **Why it matters:** Explains why certain permanent policies (including single‑premium contracts) can be used as protected assets.  
- **Exam relevance:** Mostly background; occasionally appears in conceptual questions about creditor protection.

### Texas Administrative Code, Title 28, Chapter 4 (Life and Annuity)

- **Source:** 28 TAC Chapter 4, especially subchapters on individual life‑policy requirements, cash‑value provisions, annuity contracts, and replacement consumer notices.[^13]
- **Why it matters:** Contains detailed rules on premium payments, grace periods, cash values, nonforfeiture, and replacement notices, which shape product design.  
- **Exam relevance:** Primarily supports policy‑provision and replacement domains; for this domain, it underpins that fixed and permanent products must meet minimum standards.

### Texas Administrative Code, 28 TAC § 3.9704 – Definitions (Annuity Disclosure)

- **Source:** 28 TAC § 3.9704, defining “agent,” “buyer’s guide,” “contract owner,” and related terms for annuity disclosure rules based on NAIC model.[^18]

- **Why they matter:** Provide federal tax basis for exam statements that life‑insurance death benefits are generally income‑tax free and that annuity payments are partly taxable earnings and partly nontaxable return of investment.

# **Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent: Comprehensive Research Report on Statutes and Rules**

## **SECTION 1: DOMAIN SNAPSHOT**

The regulatory landscape of the Texas insurance industry is defined by a rigorous set of statutes and administrative rules designed to balance the competitive interests of insurers with the paramount need for consumer protection. For the candidate preparing for the Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent exam, the domain titled "Texas Statutes and Rules Common to Life and Health Insurance" represents approximately 14% of the total exam weight, translating to 14 scorable questions that serve as the "gatekeeper" for licensure. This domain is not merely a test of technical knowledge but an evaluation of an agent's understanding of their legal standing within the state's administrative hierarchy. It covers the broad authority of the Commissioner of Insurance, the specific definitions that categorize insurers and their activities, the multi-faceted requirements for obtaining and maintaining a license, and the stringent ethical boundaries governing marketing and claims practices.  
The heavy weighting of this domain stems from the Texas Department of Insurance (TDI) mandate to ensure that every individual "transacting insurance" is competent, trustworthy, and compliant with the Texas Insurance Code (TIC) and the Texas Administrative Code (TAC). Subtopics such as Licensing Requirements and Marketing Practices are high-yield areas because they represent the most frequent touchpoints for regulatory friction in an agent's career. Conversely, areas like the Commissioner's general powers and the mechanics of the Life and Health Guaranty Association are critical because they define the limits of the state's safety net and administrative oversight.  
Candidates often find themselves confused by the subtle distinctions between different disciplinary actions, such as the difference between a license suspension and a revocation, or the specific timelines required for reporting administrative actions versus criminal convictions. Furthermore, the transition from a training environment to a professional practice requires an applied understanding of concepts like "rebating" and "defamation," which are not always intuitive in a high-pressure sales environment. While significant portions of this domain rely on rote memorization—such as the 24-hour continuing education requirement—the exam increasingly utilizes scenario-based questions to test the agent’s ability to apply these black-letter rules to real-world dilemmas.

## **SECTION 2: DEEP EXPLANATION BY SUBTOPIC**

### **A. Commissioner of Insurance**

The Commissioner of Insurance occupies the role of the chief executive and administrative officer of the Texas Department of Insurance (TDI). This position is not elected by the public but is appointed by the Governor with the advice and consent of the Senate for a two-year term, expiring on February 1 of each odd-numbered year. The legislative intent behind this appointment structure is to ensure a degree of professional stability and executive accountability within the department. To qualify for this office, an individual must possess at least five years of experience in business administration, government, or law, emphasizing the need for a competent administrator who is well-versed in the complexities of insurance regulation. The Commissioner is the primary enforcer of the Texas Insurance Code and is tasked with ensuring that all laws regarding insurance and insurance companies are executed faithfully to protect consumers and foster a competitive market.  
The power to examine records is one of the Commissioner's most potent tools for oversight. Under TIC 401.051 and 401.052, the TDI must visit and examine the financial condition and affairs of every carrier authorized to do business in Texas at least once every five years. This "five-year rule" serves as a minimum standard; the Commissioner retains the authority to conduct examinations as frequently as necessary for the public good. For domestic carriers that are newly organized (less than five years in operation), the scrutiny is even more intense, with mandatory examinations typically conducted in the first, third, and fifth years of the company's existence. During these examinations, the Department has the authority to investigate the carrier's ability to meet liabilities and its compliance with state laws.  
When the TDI suspects a violation of the Insurance Code or a Department rule, it initiates an investigation that may lead to a formal notice of hearing. A critical procedural hurdle for any license holder is the "Notice of Allegations." Under TAC 1.47 and TIC 82.055, a respondent has exactly 20 days from the date the notice was mailed to provide a written response. Failure to respond within this window constitutes a waiver of the right to a hearing and allows the Commissioner to issue a default order, in which all factual allegations are deemed admitted as true. The Commissioner’s enforcement arsenal includes administrative penalties, which under TIC 84.022 are capped at $25,000 per violation, though the specific amount is determined by the "gravity" of the offense and the history of the violator. Additionally, the Commissioner can issue cease and desist (C\&D) orders under TIC 83.051 to immediately halt unauthorized or harmful conduct that poses an immediate danger to public safety or economic welfare.

### **B. Insurance Definitions**

Precision in legal definitions is the foundation of Texas insurance law, as these terms determine who is subject to regulation and what activities require licensure. A "Certificate of Authority" (COA) is the essential document issued by the Commissioner that authorizes an insurance company to "engage in the business of insurance" in Texas. Operating without this certificate makes an insurer "unauthorized," a status that carries significant legal and financial risks for both the company and any agent who assists them. The definition of "transacting insurance" (or the "business of insurance") is intentionally broad under TIC 101.051 to capture all stages of the insurance lifecycle, including soliciting or negotiating a contract, taking an application, receiving or collecting premiums, issuing or delivering a policy, and even investigating or adjusting a claim.  
Texas categorizes insurers based on their geographic origin and their ownership structure. A "domestic" insurer is one formed under the laws of Texas, while a "foreign" insurer is formed under the laws of another U.S. state, and an "alien" insurer is formed under the laws of another country. From an ownership perspective, "stock" insurers are owned by shareholders who seek a profit and are managed by a board of directors elected by those shareholders. In contrast, "mutual" insurers are owned by their policyholders, who may be entitled to receive dividends if the insurer's financial performance exceeds its obligations.  
Fraternal Benefit Societies represent a unique segment of the market defined under TIC 885\. A Fraternal is a non-profit corporation or association organized solely for the mutual benefit of its members and their beneficiaries. To be recognized as such, it must have a "lodge system," where members are admitted into subordinate units (lodges, camps, or councils) through ritualistic ceremonies, and a "representative form of government". Fraternals are exempt from most state and local taxes, including occupation taxes, because they are classified as charitable and benevolent institutions. They do not have capital stock and are governed by a supreme body that meets at least every four years. It is vital for agents to know that Fraternals are typically excluded from the protections of the Texas Life and Health Guaranty Association, as they are not considered "member insurers" under that specific act.

### **C. Licensing Requirements**

Licensing is the process by which Texas ensures that those representing the insurance industry possess the requisite knowledge and integrity. The TDI issues several types of licenses, with the General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent license being the most comprehensive for this domain. While an individual license is held by a single person, an "Agency" license is required for corporations or partnerships that wish to act as agents. One of the most testable nuances is the "Temporary License" defined in TIC 4001.151. This 180-day license allows an applicant to begin transacting business while preparing for the permanent exam, provided they are sponsored by a licensed agent, insurer, or HMO who agrees to supervise their training. Crucially, a temporary license cannot be renewed or issued more than once to the same person in any six-month period, and the holder is prohibited from receiving certain commissions or replacing existing life insurance policies.  
Exemptions from the licensing requirement are narrow and strictly enforced under TIC 4001.002. The most common exception applies to salaried employees who perform only clerical or administrative duties in an agent's office, such as taking customer information or receiving premiums. To qualify for this exemption, the employee must not solicit or negotiate insurance, their compensation cannot be based on sales volume, and they cannot receive commissions. Once an agent is licensed, they cannot legally act on behalf of an insurer until they are "appointed." Under TIC 4001.201 and 4001.202, an insurer must notify the TDI of an appointment within 30 days of the effective date. An agent is permitted to act as an agent for the insurer before the TDI receives the formal notice, provided the notice is filed within the 30-day window.  
Maintenance of an active license requires compliance with Continuing Education (CE) mandates. Most resident agents must complete 24 hours of CE every two-year cycle, expiring on the last day of their birth month. A mandatory 3-hour component of this total must be dedicated to Ethics and Consumer Protection. A key "classroom equivalent" rule requires that at least 50% of these hours (12 hours) be completed in a classroom setting or an interactive online format approved by the TDI. Texas offers a "longevity exemption" for those who have held a Texas license continuously for at least 20 years. Failure to meet CE requirements results in a fine of $50 per deficient hour and a 90-day grace period to comply; failing this, the license is inactivated, and the agent must start the application process from scratch.  
The Department maintains strict standards for conduct, with TIC 4005.101 listing grounds for license denial, suspension, or revocation. These include willful violations of insurance law, material misstatements on an application, misappropriation of funds, fraud, or a felony conviction. Agents have an affirmative duty to notify the TDI of certain changes or events. A change of address, a felony conviction, or an administrative action taken by another state or federal regulator must be reported promptly to the Department. Records maintenance is another critical duty, with agents generally required to keep records of their transactions for at least five years. A license may be "suspended" for up to one year, "revoked" (a more permanent removal of authority), or "terminated" through expiration or voluntary surrender.

### **D. Marketing Practices**

Marketing practices in Texas are primarily governed by Chapter 541 (Unfair Methods of Competition and Unfair or Deceptive Acts) and Chapter 542 (Processing and Settlement of Claims) of the Insurance Code. These laws are designed to ensure that the solicitation and servicing of insurance remain transparent and fair. Unfair claim settlement practices, as defined in TIC 541.060, include misrepresenting policy provisions to claimants, failing to settle when liability is reasonably clear, and failing to provide a reasonable explanation for a denial. Furthermore, TIC 542, known as the "Prompt Payment of Claims Act," sets strict timelines: insurers must acknowledge a claim within 15 days, begin an investigation, and request all necessary items from the claimant. Once a claim is accepted, payment must be made within 5 business days (or 20 for certain surplus lines). Failure to meet these deadlines triggers an 18% annual interest penalty plus attorney's fees.  
Prohibited trade practices during the solicitation phase include false advertising, misrepresentation, and defamation. False advertising involves disseminating any statement regarding the business of insurance that is untrue or misleading. Misrepresentation, under TIC 541.051, involves making false statements about the terms, benefits, or advantages of a policy. A specific form of misrepresentation is "twisting," where an agent uses misleading comparisons to induce a policyholder to lapse or surrender an existing policy to buy a new one. Defamation is the act of making or circulating false or maliciously critical statements regarding the financial condition of an insurer to injure their reputation.  
Other critical prohibitions include rebating, fraud, and commingling. Rebating is an illegal inducement where an agent offers a portion of their commission or any benefit not specified in the insurance contract to a prospect. Fraud, a criminal offense under TIC 701, involves intentional deception for gain, such as filing false financial statements or reporting fraudulent claims. Agents have a legal "duty to report" suspected fraud to the TDI's insurance fraud unit. Commingling occurs when an agent fails to keep insurer or client funds separate from their own personal or business operating funds, a violation of fiduciary duty. Finally, unfair discrimination is prohibited, meaning insurers cannot charge different rates or offer different benefits to individuals of the same class and risk level based on race, religion, or national origin.

### **E. Agent Duties and Responsibilities**

The relationship between an agent and the public, as well as the agent and their insurer, is one of fiduciary trust. A core responsibility involves the handling and sharing of commissions. Under TIC 4005.053, an insurer or agent may only pay a commission or other valuable consideration for a service performed by an agent to a person who holds a valid license. This rule prevents "splitting" commissions with unlicensed individuals for the purpose of soliciting or negotiating insurance. However, an agent is permitted to pay a "referral fee" to an unlicensed person, provided the fee is a fixed amount not dependent on whether the referred individual actually purchases an insurance product.  
Agents must also navigate rules regarding double compensation. If an agent receives a fee from a customer for the placement or renewal of an insurance product, they generally cannot also receive a commission from the insurer for that same transaction unless they have obtained the customer's documented acknowledgment. This disclosure ensures that the client is aware of the agent's financial incentives. Additionally, agents must avoid "controlled business," which is the practice of obtaining a license primarily to write insurance for themselves, their family members, or their business associates. Under TIC 4001.104, an agent must be "actively engaged" in soliciting insurance for the general public, and the TDI may deny or revoke a license if it determines that a significant portion of the agent's business is controlled business.

### **F. Texas Life and Health Guaranty Association**

The Texas Life and Health Guaranty Association (TIC 463\) acts as a critical safety net for policyholders in the event that a member insurer becomes insolvent and is ordered liquidated by a court. Membership in the association is mandatory for all insurers licensed to write life, health, or annuity contracts in Texas. Crucially, Fraternal Benefit Societies, health maintenance organizations (HMOs), and certain charitable gift annuities are generally not members and thus their policyholders are not covered by this association. Protection is generally provided only to Texas residents at the time the company is declared insolvent.  
The Association’s coverage is subject to specific statutory caps per "insured life," regardless of the number of policies held. For life insurance, the limits are $300,000 in death benefits and $100,000 in net cash surrender or withdrawal value. Health insurance limits vary: $500,000 for basic hospital or major medical plans, $300,000 for disability or long-term care insurance, and $200,000 for other types of health insurance. Annuity benefits are capped at $250,000 in present value. There is an individual aggregate limit of $300,000 per person, though this limit can increase to $500,000 for those with high-limit health benefit plans. Agents must be aware that using the existence of the Guaranty Association as an inducement to purchase insurance is a violation of Texas law, as it creates a false sense of security that undermines the fundamental principles of risk assessment.

## **SECTION 5: EXAM-FOCUSED TAKEAWAYS**

### **What I Absolutely Need to Know Cold**

* **Continuing Education**: 24 hours total; 3 hours Ethics; 50% classroom rule.  
* **Temporary License**: 180 days; no exam; no renewal.  
* **Appointment Window**: 30 days to notify the TDI.  
* **Maximum Penalty**: $25,000 per violation.  
* **Prompt Payment**: 15 days to acknowledge; 5 business days to pay after acceptance.  
* **Commissioner's Term**: Appointed; 2-year term; expires Feb 1 of odd years.  
* **Guaranty Association Limits**: $300k Death; $100k Cash Value; $500k Major Medical.  
* **Examination Frequency**: Every 5 years (standard); 1st, 3rd, 5th (new carriers).

### **Secondary but Testable**

* **Longevity Exemption**: 20 years continuous licensure exempts you from CE.  
* **Fraternal Society Characteristics**: Lodge system; Representative government; Non-profit.  
* **controlled Business**: License cannot be used primarily for self/family/associates.  
* **Referral Fees**: Legal if a fixed amount and not based on a purchase.  
* **Commingling**: Fiduciary violation of mixing personal and business funds.

### **Low Priority**

* **Commissioner Qualifications**: 5 years experience (admin/law).  
* **Sunset Provision**: TDI renewal date (2029).  
* **Surplus Lines Timelines**: The 20-day extension (focus on the standard 5-day rule).

### **Most Likely Wrong-Answer Traps**

* **The "Elected" Trap**: The Commissioner is appointed, not elected.  
* **The "90-Day Grace" Trap**: While there is a 90-day grace period for CE, the deadline for completion is the license expiration date.  
* **The "Fraternal Coverage" Trap**: Fraternals are usually NOT covered by the Guaranty Association.  
* **The "Sales Pitch" Trap**: It is a violation to use the Guaranty Association to close a sale.  
* **The "Renewal" Trap**: Temporary licenses are one-time and non-renewable.  
* **The "Clerical" Trap**: Clerical staff can take information but CANNOT receive commissions.

### **Quick Memory Hooks**

* **"15/15/5"**: The Prompt Payment rhythm (15 days to acknowledge, 15 days to decide, 5 days to pay).  
* **"3 on 24"**: 3 hours of Ethics inside the 24 hours of CE.  
* **"Odd Feb 1"**: When the Commissioner’s term ends.  
* **"Five-Year Itch"**: How often the Commissioner must examine a carrier.  
* **"Re-Bate \= Give Back"**: Rebating is giving money back to the client.  
* **"Twisting \= Turning"**: Twisting is turning a client away from their current policy using lies.

### **Top 15 Distinctions to Avoid Mixing Up**

1. **Appointed vs. Elected**: The Commissioner is appointed by the Governor.  
2. **COA vs. License**: COA is for companies; License is for individuals/agencies.  
3. **Foreign vs. Alien**: Foreign is U.S.-based; Alien is non-U.S. based.  
4. **Suspension vs. Revocation**: Suspension is max 1 year; Revocation is permanent.  
5. **Rebating vs. Referrals**: Rebating is illegal; Referrals are legal if not sales-based.  
6. **Twisting vs. Churning**: Twisting involves replacement via lies; Churning is internal replacement.  
7. **Business Days vs. Calendar Days**: Decisions take 15 business days; Acknowledgments take 15 calendar days.  
8. **Individual vs. Aggregate Limit**: Guaranty Assoc. has per-benefit caps AND a $300k overall cap.  
9. **Domestic vs. Resident**: Domestic applies to the company's origin; Resident applies to the agent's home.  
10. **Agent vs. Agency**: An agent is the person; an agency is the licensed legal entity.  
11. **Notice vs. Decision**: You have 15 days to acknowledge a claim, but 15 *business* days to decide.  
12. **Classroom vs. Classroom Equivalent**: Both count toward the 50% interactive rule.  
13. **Fine vs. Penalty**: CE deficiencies lead to $50 "fines"; code violations lead to $25k "penalties".  
14. **Controlled vs. General Business**: You must sell to the public, not just yourself.  
15. **Member vs. Non-Member**: Only member insurers are in the Guaranty Association (Fraternals are not).

### **Top 15 Likely Multiple-Choice Traps**

1. "The Commissioner serves at the pleasure of the Governor for a four-year term." (False: 2 years).  
2. "A temporary license can be converted to a permanent license without an exam." (False: Exam required).  
3. "All 24 hours of CE can be completed via correspondence courses." (False: 50% classroom rule).  
4. "The Commissioner may fine an agent up to $100,000 for misrepresentation." (False: $25,000).  
5. "A foreign insurer is one incorporated in Mexico or Canada." (False: That is an Alien insurer).  
6. "The Guaranty Association protects policyholders of bankrupt Fraternal societies." (False: Usually excluded).  
7. "An agent can pay a $100 referral fee to a client for every policy the client’s friends buy." (False: Must be fixed, not sales-based).  
8. "A domestic insurer must be examined by the TDI every three years." (False: Every 5 years).  
9. "An agent has 60 days to report a felony conviction." (False: Promptly/30 days).  
10. "Claims must be paid within 30 days of the company receiving proof of loss." (False: 5 business days after acceptance).  
11. "Misrepresentation only applies to written advertisements." (False: Applies to oral statements too).  
12. "A stock insurer is owned by the people who buy the insurance." (False: That is a Mutual insurer).  
13. "Rebating is allowed if the client signs a waiver." (False: Always prohibited).  
14. "An agent can start selling for a company as soon as they mail the appointment form." (True: Provided it is mailed within 30 days).  
15. "The Commissioner can put an agent in jail for code violations." (False: Commissioner has administrative/civil power, not criminal).

### **Concise One-Hour Review Summary**

To pass this domain, focus on the following core pillars:

* **The Power Center**: The Commissioner is the appointed administrative leader who enforces the code, examines carriers every 5 years, and can levy $25,000 penalties or issue C\&D orders.  
* **The Licensing Lifecycle**: Pass the exam (or get a 180-day temporary), get appointed within 30 days, maintain 24 hours of CE (3 ethics), and report address/criminal changes promptly.  
* **The Ethical Boundary**: Avoid the "Big Three" marketing sins—Misrepresentation (lying about benefits), Defamation (lying about rivals), and Rebating (sharing commissions).  
* **The Protection System**: Handle claims with 15/15/5 precision (Acknowledge/Decide/Pay) and understand that the Guaranty Association is a quiet $300k safety net for member insurer failures.

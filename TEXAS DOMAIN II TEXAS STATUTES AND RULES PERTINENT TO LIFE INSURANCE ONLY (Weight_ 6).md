# **Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent Exam: Comprehensive Research Report on Life Insurance Statutes and Rules**

## **SECTION 1: DOMAIN SNAPSHOT**

The Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent examination prioritizes a deep understanding of the regulatory landscape specifically governing life insurance contracts. This domain, titled Texas Statutes and Rules Pertinent to Life Insurance Only, is assigned a weight of six, reflecting its foundational role in agent licensing and consumer protection within the state. The legal framework is primarily constructed through the Texas Insurance Code (TIC) and the Texas Administrative Code (TAC), Title 28, which together define the mandatory boundaries within which insurers must operate.1 The purpose of these statutes is twofold: to ensure the solvency and fair dealing of insurance companies and to protect the equitable interests of Texas policyholders and their beneficiaries.4  
This domain encompasses the entire lifecycle of a life insurance policy, beginning with the requirements for individual and group policies and extending through the complexities of policy loans, nonforfeiture options, and the eventual settlement of claims.1 Within the context of the Texas exam, certain subtopics emerge as high-yield areas that demand absolute precision. These include the mandatory provisions found in TIC Chapter 1101—such as the grace period, incontestability, and misstatement of age rules—which are frequently tested using specific numerical timeframes and standardized legal outcomes.1 Furthermore, the conversion rights associated with group life insurance under TIC Chapter 1131 represent a critical area of applied understanding, as these rules provide essential protections for individuals transitioning between employment and private coverage.10  
One of the most significant challenges for exam candidates is the tendency to confuse similar yet distinct concepts. The mechanical differences between a policy loan and a nonforfeiture benefit, for instance, are often blurred. A policy loan maintains the contract in force while creating a debt against the cash value, whereas nonforfeiture provisions trigger only after a premium default to determine how the policy’s remaining equity will be utilized to provide continuing coverage.6 Similarly, the distinctions between life settlements and viatical settlements are nuanced, relying heavily on the health status of the insured and specific state-mandated definitions of terminal and chronic illness.15  
For the Texas General Lines exam, success is predicated on a mix of rote memorization and the ability to apply legal principles to practical scenarios. Candidates must memorize specific deadlines, such as the two-year incontestability window or the 31-day conversion period, while also understanding the "Representation" rule, which prevents insurers from voiding a policy for innocent mistakes made by the insured during the application process.1 This report provides the exhaustive detail and insight necessary to master these concepts and navigate the likely exam traps set by test writers.

## **SECTION 2: DEEP EXPLANATION BY SUBTOPIC**

## **A. Life Policy Provisions**

The mandatory provisions for individual life insurance in Texas are designed to create a transparent and stable contractual relationship between the insurer and the policyholder. These rules ensure that coverage is not easily terminated and that minor errors do not lead to a total loss of benefits.

#### **1\. Grace Period**

The grace period is a statutory requirement under TIC Section 1101.005, mandating that a life insurance policy remain in full force for a specified duration after a premium due date has passed without payment.1 In Texas, the grace period must be at least one month. If the period is expressed in days, it must be at least 31 days.3 This rule exists to prevent the unintentional lapse of coverage due to administrative delays or temporary financial hardship. A Texas life and health agent must recognize that during this period, the insurer is permitted to charge interest on the overdue premium, and if the insured dies during the grace period, the insurer is legally entitled to deduct the overdue premium and any accrued interest from the final death benefit settlement.3 This provision applies to all premiums after the first; the initial premium must be paid to put the policy in force.2

#### **2\. Notice of Default**

The concept of a "notice of default" in Texas life insurance is closely tied to the grace period and the potential for policy cancellation, particularly when a premium finance agreement is involved. Under such agreements, the finance company must provide a written notice of intent to cancel at least 10 days before the cancellation becomes effective.20 In standard individual policies, the end of the grace period serves as the functional default point. If the premium is not paid by the end of the grace period, the policy will lapse unless a nonforfeiture option or an automatic premium loan (APL) is triggered.10 Agents must be wary of "negative" exam questions that imply a separate notice of default is required for all life policies; the grace period itself often satisfies the primary notice requirement for individual contracts.3

#### **3\. Assignment**

Assignment is the legal process by which a policyholder transfers some or all of their ownership rights to another party. Texas law, anchored in TIC Section 1103.055, permits individuals of legal age to assign their policy or an interest in the policy to any individual or entity, provided the transfer is not prohibited by the policy terms.22 There are two primary types of assignment tested: absolute assignment, which is a permanent transfer of all rights, and collateral assignment, which is a temporary transfer used to secure a loan.23 For an assignment to be valid against the insurer, the company must generally receive written notice of the transfer.22 In the context of group life insurance, an insured may assign their rights to any entity except their employer.25

#### **4\. Entire Contract**

TIC Section 1101.003 requires that every life insurance policy contain an "Entire Contract" provision.1 This rule states that the policy, together with the attached application, constitutes the complete legal agreement between the parties. The significance of this rule for an agent is that the insurer cannot incorporate outside documents—such as company bylaws or board resolutions—by reference to deny a claim or modify the contract unless those documents are physically attached to the policy.1 Furthermore, this provision dictates that only authorized executive officers of the insurer have the power to alter the contract, and any changes must be in writing and signed by such an officer.1

#### **5\. Misstatement of Age**

The misstatement of age provision is a mandatory protective clause under TIC Section 1101.008.1 If the age of the insured was understated at the time of application, the insurer is not permitted to void the policy or deny the claim. Instead, the insurer must adjust the death benefit to the amount that the actual premium paid would have purchased if the insured's age had been stated correctly.2 If the age was overstated, the insurer typically refunds the excess premium.2 This is a frequent area for exam "traps" where a distractor answer suggests the policy will be cancelled for misrepresentation; under Texas law, the remedy is always an adjustment of benefits, not cancellation.1

#### **6\. Incontestability**

Under TIC Section 1101.006, a life insurance policy must include an incontestability provision stating that the insurer cannot challenge the validity of the contract after it has been in force for two years during the lifetime of the insured.1 This two-year "window" protects beneficiaries from claims being denied due to unintentional errors in the original application. The only statutory exceptions that allow an insurer to contest a policy after this two-year period are the nonpayment of premiums or, at the insurer's option, violations of conditions related to naval or military service during a time of war.1 This rule effectively creates a statute of limitations on the insurer's right to investigate application fraud or misrepresentation.1

## **B. Beneficiaries**

Beneficiary rules in Texas are structured to ensure that the policy proceeds reach the intended parties without the delays and costs of probate.

#### **1\. Who May Designate**

Any individual of legal age who is insured under a life insurance policy may designate one or more beneficiaries to receive the proceeds upon their death.22 In Texas, the law is liberal regarding who can be a beneficiary, allowing for individuals, partnerships, corporations, and trust estates to be named, provided they have an insurable interest at the time of policy issuance.22 Texas law specifically recognizes the insurable interest of corporations in the lives of their officers and stockholders, and of partnerships in the lives of their members.22

#### **2\. Revocable / Irrevocable**

The distinction between revocable and irrevocable beneficiaries is a core concept in policy ownership. A revocable beneficiary is one whose designation can be changed by the policyholder at any time without the beneficiary’s notice or consent.31 This is the standard designation in most life insurance contracts. Conversely, an irrevocable beneficiary has a vested interest in the policy. The policyholder cannot change the beneficiary, assign the policy, or take out a policy loan without the written consent of the irrevocable beneficiary.31 For exam purposes, it is critical to understand that an irrevocable designation significantly limits the policyowner’s "Bundle of Rights".31

#### **3\. Facility of Payment**

The "Facility of Payment" clause is an administrative provision that allows the insurer to pay a portion of the death benefit to a person who is deemed "equitably entitled" to it, even if they are not the named beneficiary.32 This typically occurs if the named beneficiary is a minor, the estate of the insured, or if the beneficiary cannot be located. The clause is often used to pay for the insured's funeral expenses or final medical bills. In many Texas-approved group certificates, the amount payable under this clause is limited to a relatively small sum, such as $5,000, and a payment made in good faith by the insurer under this provision discharges the company from further liability.33

## **C. Policy Loans / Nonforfeiture**

These subtopics focus on the equity (cash value) that accumulates within permanent life insurance policies and the rules governing how that equity can be accessed or preserved.

#### **1\. Standard Nonforfeiture Law**

Texas Insurance Code Chapter 1105, known as the Standard Nonforfeiture Law for Life Insurance, requires that every permanent life insurance policy provide nonforfeiture benefits to the policyholder in the event of a premium default.6 The policyholder must be given up to 60 days after the premium due date to select one of three standard options:

* **Cash Surrender Value**: The policy is cancelled, and the insurer pays the accumulated cash value to the policyholder in a lump sum.6  
* **Reduced Paid-Up Insurance**: The cash value is used as a single premium to purchase a smaller face amount of the same type of insurance as the original policy, which remains in force for the rest of the insured's life.10  
* **Extended Term Insurance**: The cash value is used to purchase a term policy for the full face amount of the original policy for as long a duration as the cash value allows. In Texas, this is typically the automatic option if the policyholder fails to make a selection within the 60-day window.14

A key Texas provision is that the insurer reserves the right to defer the payment of a cash surrender value for a period of up to six months after the demand is made.6

#### **2\. Policy Loan Interest Rate**

TIC Section 1101.009 and Chapter 1110 govern the rules for policy loans.1 Insurers must provide policy loans once the policy has been in force for at least three full years.1 The loan is secured solely by the policy itself. Texas law allows for either a fixed interest rate (not to exceed 10% per year) or an adjustable interest rate.38 If an adjustable rate is used, the insurer must determine the rate at regular intervals at least once every 12 months, but no more than once every three months.38 The insurer must also provide at least 30 days' advance notice of any interest rate increase to the policyholder.38

#### **3\. Automatic Premium Loan (APL)**

The Automatic Premium Loan (APL) is an optional provision that can be elected by the policyholder at the time of application or added later.10 It authorizes the insurer to automatically take a loan against the policy's cash value to pay any premium that remains unpaid at the end of the grace period.14 This ensures that the policy does not lapse unintentionally. The APL functions exactly like a standard policy loan, accruing interest and creating a debt that will be deducted from the final death benefit if not repaid.13

## **D. Individual Life Insurance**

Individual life insurance policies in Texas must comply with the foundational requirements of TIC Chapter 1101, Subchapter A, which sets the minimum standards for all policies issued in the state.

#### **1\. Required Policy Provisions**

In addition to the grace period and incontestability provisions discussed above, individual life policies must provide for the "Settlement of Claim" not later than two months after the receipt of proof of death.1 Texas law also requires that if the policy proceeds are payable in installments, the policy must include a table showing the amount of those installments.1 Furthermore, a "Statements of Insured" provision must state that all statements made by the insured are considered representations and not warranties, meaning they must only be true to the best of the insured's knowledge to avoid voiding the policy for fraud.1

#### **2\. Texas Required Coverages or Protections**

A specific protection under Texas law is the requirement that interest must be paid on death benefit proceeds from the date the insurer receives due proof of loss until the date the insurer offers to settle the claim.8 The interest rate is typically the rate specified in the policy or the rate the insurer pays on funds left on deposit.22 This ensures that the beneficiary is compensated for any delay in the payment of the death benefit.8

## **E. Group Life Insurance**

Group life insurance in Texas is governed by TIC Chapter 1131, which addresses the unique relationship between the employer (policyholder) and the employees (certificate holders).

#### **1\. Required Policy Provisions**

Group life policies must include several mandatory provisions, including a 31-day grace period for the policyholder.32 Because group life is often issued without individual medical underwriting, the policy may include an "Evidence of Insurability" provision, allowing the insurer to request health information for individuals who enroll late or for amounts exceeding a certain "Guarantee Issue" limit.25 Every individual insured under a group policy must be provided with a "Certificate of Insurance" that describes their benefits, the person to whom benefits are payable, and their conversion rights.32

#### **2\. Conversion Rights**

The conversion privilege is perhaps the most critical protection in group life insurance.10 If an individual's group life coverage terminates because they leave their job or because they are no longer part of an eligible class, they have the right to convert their group coverage to an individual permanent life policy without having to provide evidence of insurability (i.e., no medical exam).10 This conversion must be initiated within 31 days of the termination of group coverage.10 If the insured dies during this 31-day conversion period, the group policy must pay the death benefit as if the individual were still covered.10

#### **3\. Eligibility and Certificate Concepts**

Employer groups in Texas must cover at least two employees on the date of issue.43 For groups where the employees contribute to the premium (contributory plans), at least 75% of the eligible employees must participate.43 If the employer pays 100% of the premium (non-contributory plans), 100% of the eligible employees must be covered.43 Texas also allows for dependent life insurance on the lives of a spouse or children, though statutory limits historically capped coverage on minor children at low amounts, such as $2,000.43

## **F. Credit Life Insurance**

Credit life insurance is a specialized form of insurance designed to protect both lenders and borrowers in connection with a specific debt.

#### **1\. Purpose**

The primary purpose of credit life insurance, as outlined in TIC Chapter 1153, is to provide funds to pay off a specific debt if the debtor dies before the loan is repaid.4 This protects the creditor from a financial loss and protects the debtor's estate from the burden of the outstanding loan.4

#### **2\. Basic Regulatory Framework**

Credit life insurance in Texas is generally limited to credit transactions of 10 years' duration or less.4 The amount of insurance may not exceed the total amount of the indebtedness.4 The primary beneficiary of the policy is the creditor, who receives only the amount necessary to extinguish the debt; any excess proceeds must be paid to a secondary beneficiary or the debtor's estate.4

#### **3\. Consumer-Protection Rules**

To prevent predatory pricing, the Texas Commissioner of Insurance establishes "presumptive premium rates" for credit life insurance.47 Insurers may file rates that deviate from these presumptive rates by up to 30% (either above or below) without prior written approval from the Commissioner, provided they follow established filing procedures.47 Additionally, credit life premiums are not legally considered "interest" or "finance charges" on the underlying loan.4

## **G. Life Settlements**

Life settlements involve the sale of an existing life insurance policy to a third-party investor, a transaction governed by the Life Settlements Act (TIC Chapter 1111A).

#### **1\. Basic Legal Framework**

A life settlement occurs when the owner of a life insurance policy sells the policy to a "provider" for a lump-sum payment that is more than the cash surrender value but less than the total death benefit.15 The provider then takes over the premium payments and collects the full death benefit when the insured dies.15 This option is commonly used by seniors who no longer need their insurance or by individuals facing significant medical expenses.18

#### **2\. Required Disclosures**

Before a life settlement contract is signed, the provider must give the owner a separate written disclosure document.50 This document must state that alternatives to the settlement exist, including accelerated death benefits offered by the original insurer, and that the settlement proceeds may be taxable or affect the owner's eligibility for government benefits like Medicaid.5 The owner must also be informed of their right to rescind the settlement contract within 15 days of its execution.50

#### **3\. Prohibited Practices**

Texas prohibits entering into a life settlement contract within the first two years of a policy’s issuance, a rule intended to prevent "stranger-originated life insurance" (STOLI).52 However, exceptions to this two-year rule exist if the owner or insured can prove a significant change in circumstances, such as terminal or chronic illness, divorce, retirement, or bankruptcy.52

#### **4\. Distinction from Viaticals**

Under Texas law, a viatical settlement is effectively a subset of life settlements where the insured is terminally or chronically ill.17 A "terminal illness" is defined as a medical condition expected to result in death within 24 months or less.51 A "chronic illness" is defined as being unable to perform at least two activities of daily living (ADLs), such as eating or bathing, or requiring substantial supervision due to severe cognitive impairment.15 Generally, proceeds from a viatical settlement are free from federal income tax, whereas life settlement proceeds for healthy seniors are taxable.17

## **SECTION 5: EXAM-FOCUSED TAKEAWAYS**

## **What to Know Cold (High Priority)**

* **The "Rule of 31"**: Texas grace periods and group conversion windows are 31 days. This is the most common number on the life insurance portion of the exam.3  
* **The "Rule of 2"**: Incontestability and suicide exclusion windows are 2 years from the date of policy issue.1  
* **Representations vs. Warranties**: Statements in life insurance applications are representations. This means a policy can only be voided for intentional fraud, not for simple mistakes.1  
* **Misstatement of Age Remedy**: The remedy is ALWAYS to adjust the benefit. It is NEVER to cancel the policy.2  
* **The 3-Year Loan Rule**: Policy loans are not a mandatory provision until the policy has been in force for 3 years.13

## **Secondary Knowledge (Moderately Testable)**

* **Claim Settlement Time**: The insurer has 2 months to pay a death claim once they have proof of loss.1  
* **Cash Value Deferral**: The insurer can delay paying out a cash surrender for up to 6 months.6  
* **Facility of Payment Limit**: Know that this clause is used for minor amounts (e.g., $5,000) to pay relatives for final expenses.33  
* **Interest on Proceeds**: Interest starts accruing as soon as the company receives proof of death.8

## **Low Priority (Background Only)**

* The exact mortality tables used for nonforfeiture (e.g., 1980 CSO Table).  
* The specific internal actuarial formulas used to calculate "Net Death Benefit."  
* Specific filing fees for credit life insurance forms.

## **Likely Wrong-Answer Traps**

* **Trap: "The grace period is 30 days."** (Wrong—in Texas, it is one month or 31 days).  
* **Trap: "Conversion rights are available only if you pass a physical."** (Wrong—conversion is guaranteed regardless of health).  
* **Trap: "The insurer can cancel a policy for misstatement of age if caught in the first 2 years."** (Wrong—the age rule is separate from incontestability and results only in benefit adjustment).  
* **Trap: "Credit life proceeds are paid to the debtor's spouse."** (Wrong—they go to the creditor first).

## **Memory Hooks and Mnemonics**

* **"31 Days to Pay or Convert"**: Links the grace period and group conversion window.  
* **"2 Years to be True"**: Links incontestability and suicide exclusion.  
* **"Age Adjusts, Fraud Fails"**: Reminds that age errors result in recalculated benefits, while only fraud can void a policy (and only for 2 years).  
* **"E.C.R." (Extended Term, Cash, Reduced Paid-Up)**: The three nonforfeiture options.

## **Top 10 Distinctions to Avoid Mixing Up**

1. **31 Days (Grace) vs. 2 Years (Incontestability)**: Don't flip the numbers for these time-based protections.  
2. **Conversion (Group to Individual) vs. Portability (Group to Group)**: Conversion is a statutory right to a *permanent* policy.12  
3. **Representations (Life) vs. Warranties (Other types of contracts)**: Life insurance uses the "Representation" standard.1  
4. **Policy Loan (Owner's Debt) vs. Nonforfeiture (Equity preservation)**: One is for cash now; one is for after a lapse.  
5. **Fixed (Max 10%) vs. Adjustable Loan Rates**: Know that fixed rates have a hard cap in Texas.38  
6. **Terminal Illness (24 months) vs. Chronic Illness (2 ADLs)**: These define eligibility for viatical settlements.15  
7. **Absolute Assignment (Ownership) vs. Collateral Assignment (Security)**: Permanent vs. Temporary transfer.23  
8. **Revocable (Changeable) vs. Irrevocable (Consent required)**: How much control does the owner have?.31  
9. **Primary (First) vs. Contingent (Second) Beneficiary**: The "backup" only gets paid if the first is dead.10  
10. **Facility of Payment (Relative) vs. Named Beneficiary**: The clause is for "equitable" payment of small sums.32

## **Concise “One-Hour Review” Summary**

Focus on the **Standard Provisions** first: Grace Period (31 days), Incontestability (2 years), and Misstatement of Age (Benefit Adjustment). Next, master **Group Life Conversion**: 31 days to move to an individual policy without a medical exam. For **Nonforfeiture**, remember the "Big Three": Cash, Reduced Paid-Up, and Extended Term (the automatic one). For **Loans**, remember the 3-year rule and the 10% fixed interest cap. Finally, review **Life Settlements**: They require 15-day rescission rights, a 2-year waiting period, and specific disclosures about taxes and alternatives.

# **Texas Statutes and Rules Pertinent to Accident and Health Insurance: Comprehensive Domain Analysis**

## **SECTION 1: DOMAIN SNAPSHOT**

The regulatory landscape of accident and health insurance in the State of Texas is defined by a rigorous set of statutes designed to protect the consumer while ensuring a stable and predictable marketplace for insurers. This specific domain, which carries a significant weight on the Texas General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent exam, encompasses the legal requirements for individual and group policies, specialized coverages such as Medicare Supplement and Long-Term Care, and the mandatory rights afforded to policyholders regarding claims and renewals. The primary statutory authority resides in the Texas Insurance Code (TIC), specifically Chapters 1201, 1202, 1251, 1252, 1651, and 1652, supplemented by the Texas Administrative Code (TAC), Title 28\.1  
For the prospective agent, this domain is critical because it moves beyond general insurance theory into the specific "black-letter" rules of Texas. It is not enough to understand what a grace period is in a general sense; the agent must know the precise day-counts mandated by Texas law for different premium payment frequencies.4 This section represents a transition from conceptual learning to legal compliance. The highest-yield subtopics typically involve the twelve mandatory individual health policy provisions, the nuances of Texas "Mini-COBRA" state continuation, and the strict disclosure requirements for seniors purchasing supplemental or long-term care products.6  
Confusion frequently arises on exam day regarding the timeline of a claim. Candidates often struggle to distinguish between the time an insured has to notify the insurer of a loss (Notice of Claim) and the time the insurer has to provide the paperwork for that claim (Claim Forms).3 Furthermore, the distinction between "Guaranteed Renewable" and "Noncancelable" is a perennial stumbling block, as both offer high levels of consumer protection but differ significantly in their treatment of premium adjustments.9  
Mastering this domain requires a blend of rote memorization for specific timelines and an applied understanding of the agent’s ethical and legal duties. While the "Notice of Claim" is 20 days, the "Proof of Loss" is 90 days, and the "Legal Actions" window opens at 60 days but closes at 3 years, these numbers must be understood within the context of the claims process.6 The agent must view these statutes as a protective shield for the public and a professional boundary for their own conduct.

## **SECTION 2: DEEP EXPLANATION BY SUBTOPIC**

### **A. Required Provisions in Individual Accident and Health Policies**

The Texas Insurance Code Chapter 1201 mandates that every individual accident and health insurance policy delivered or issued for delivery in Texas must contain specific provisions.3 These provisions are designed to ensure consistency and fairness in the insurance contract, preventing insurers from burying restrictive terms in fine print. These are "Uniform Policy Provisions" and must appear in the policy in the words prescribed by law unless the insurer uses substitute wording that is no less favorable to the insured and is approved by the Commissioner of Insurance.11

#### **1\. Grace Period**

The grace period is a mandatory provision that provides the policyowner with a window of time after the premium due date during which the policy remains in force even if the premium is unpaid.4 In Texas, the length of the grace period is strictly dependent on the frequency of the premium payments. For policies where premiums are paid weekly, the grace period must be at least 7 days. For monthly premium policies, the period is at least 10 days. For all other payment modes—quarterly, semi-annually, or annually—the grace period must be at least 31 days.4 This rule exists to prevent the inadvertent lapse of coverage due to temporary financial hardship or administrative oversight. For the agent, it is vital to know that if a claim occurs during the grace period, the insurer may deduct the overdue premium from the benefit payment.

#### **2\. Reinstatement**

When a policyholder fails to pay the premium within the grace period, the policy lapses. The reinstatement provision outlines the process for restoring coverage.12 In Texas, if an insurer or an authorized agent accepts a renewal premium without requiring an application for reinstatement, the policy is reinstated immediately. However, if an application is required and a conditional receipt is issued, the insurer has 45 days to approve or deny the application. If the insurer does not notify the applicant of a denial within 45 days of the conditional receipt, the policy is "deemed" reinstated on the 45th day.12 To prevent "anti-selection" (people only buying insurance when they are sick), a reinstated policy covers accidental injuries immediately but has a 10-day waiting period for sickness-related claims.12

#### **3\. Notice of Claim**

The notice of claim provision requires the insured to provide written notice of a loss to the insurer within 20 days of the occurrence, or as soon thereafter as is reasonably possible.6 This notice can be mailed to the insurer’s home office or given to any authorized agent. This requirement allows the insurer to begin the investigation process while the circumstances of the loss are fresh. Failure to provide notice within 20 days does not necessarily invalidate a claim if the insured can show it was not reasonably possible to provide notice sooner.

#### **4\. Claim Forms**

Once the insurer receives a notice of claim, it has a statutory obligation to provide the claimant with the necessary forms for filing a proof of loss. Under Texas law, the insurer must provide these forms within 15 days of receiving the notice.3 If the insurer fails to meet this 15-day deadline, the claimant is relieved of the obligation to use the insurer’s specific forms and can instead submit written proof of the character and extent of the loss in any reasonable format.

#### **5\. Proof of Loss**

Written proof of loss must be provided to the insurer at its designated office within 90 days after the date of the loss.6 For claims involving periodic payments contingent on continuing loss (such as disability income), the 90-day window begins at the termination of the period for which the insurer is liable. If meeting the 90-day deadline is not reasonably possible, the claim remains valid if proof is provided as soon as possible, and in no event later than one year after the time proof is otherwise required, except in cases of legal incapacity.6

#### **6\. Time of Payment of Claims**

This provision establishes the speed at which an insurer must pay a claim once proof of loss is received. For lump-sum benefits, such as accidental death or dismemberment, payment must be made "immediately" upon receipt of written proof.6 For benefits that provide periodic payments, such as disability or long-term care, payments must be made at least monthly.6 This prevents insurers from unreasonably delaying payments that the insured may rely on for survival or medical care.

#### **7\. Payment of Claims**

While "Time of Payment" focuses on the schedule, "Payment of Claims" specifies the recipient of the funds. Indemnity for loss of life is paid to the designated beneficiary or the insured’s estate if no beneficiary is named. All other indemnities are paid to the insured.14 Texas law also allows for an optional "facility of payment" clause, permitting the insurer to pay up to $1,000 to a relative or anyone who appears to be entitled to the money due to having incurred medical or funeral expenses for the insured.

#### **8\. Physical Examination and Autopsy**

The insurer, at its own expense, has the right to examine the person of the insured while a claim is pending as often as it may reasonably require.3 Additionally, where not prohibited by law, the insurer has the right to perform an autopsy in the event of death. This is a crucial tool for investigating potential fraud or verifying the cause of death in accidental death claims.

#### **9\. Legal Actions**

This provision prevents a claimant from initiating a lawsuit against the insurer too early or too late. A claimant must wait at least 60 days after providing written proof of loss before filing a lawsuit.3 This waiting period gives the insurer time to investigate and settle the claim. Conversely, no legal action may be brought more than 3 years after the date on which written proof of loss was required to be furnished.3

#### **10\. Change of Beneficiary**

The insured has the right to change the beneficiary at any time unless an irrevocable beneficiary designation was made. The consent of the beneficiary is not required for the insured to change the beneficiary, assign the policy, or surrender the policy.3 This provision ensures that the policyowner maintains ultimate control over the contract's death benefit.

#### **11\. Entire Contract**

The entire contract provision states that the policy, including any attached papers (such as the application) and riders, constitutes the full agreement between the insurer and the insured.2 No changes to the policy are valid until approved by an executive officer of the insurer and endorsed on or attached to the policy. Agents do not have the authority to change the policy or waive any of its provisions. This protects both parties from verbal misrepresentations or misunderstandings.

#### **12\. Time Limit on Certain Defenses (Incontestability)**

This provision limits the insurer's ability to challenge the validity of the policy based on misstatements in the application. In Texas, after a policy has been in force for two years, the insurer cannot use any misstatement (except fraudulent ones) made by the applicant to void the policy or deny a claim for a loss occurring after that two-year period.3 Furthermore, no claim for loss occurring after two years can be denied on the ground that a pre-existing condition (not specifically excluded by name) existed prior to the policy's effective date.

#### **13\. Misstatement of Age**

If the age of the insured was misstated on the application, the benefits payable under the policy will be adjusted to what the premium paid would have purchased at the correct age.2 If the insured’s true age would have made them ineligible for the policy altogether, the insurer’s liability is limited to a refund of premiums paid.3

### **B. Renewability**

Renewability provisions determine how long a policy can remain in force and under what conditions the insurer can cancel it or raise premiums. These are essential consumer protections that dictate the longevity of a health insurance contract.

#### **1\. Noncancelable**

A noncancelable policy offers the highest level of stability. The insurer cannot cancel the policy, cannot change any of its terms, and—critically—cannot increase the premium as long as the insured pays the premiums on time.9 This type of renewability is typically reserved for disability income policies and generally lasts until the insured reaches age 65\.

#### **2\. Guaranteed Renewable**

Under a guaranteed renewable policy, the insurer must renew the policy until a certain age (usually 65). While the insurer cannot cancel the policy or change the benefits, it *can* increase the premium.10 However, premiums can only be increased for an entire class of insureds, not for an individual based on their specific health history or claims. This is the standard for Medicare Supplement and Long-Term Care policies in Texas.9

#### **3\. Conditionally Renewable**

This provision allows the insurer to terminate the policy if certain conditions specified in the contract are met. These conditions must be unrelated to the insured’s health, such as the insured reaching a specific age or changing to a hazardous occupation.15

#### **4\. Optionally Renewable**

Optionally renewable policies give the insurer the right to refuse renewal on any premium due date or policy anniversary. If the insurer decides not to renew, it must provide the insured with a written notice. In Texas, this notice must typically be delivered at least 5 days before the premium due date.4

#### **5\. Nonrenewable (Term Concepts)**

Nonrenewable policies provide coverage for a specific, short-term period (e.g., 6 months). There is no provision for renewal; once the term expires, the coverage ends. These are often used as "gap" coverage between employer-sponsored plans.

### **C. Group and Blanket Accident and Health Insurance**

Group insurance is a significant portion of the Texas health market, governed primarily by TIC Chapter 1251\. It involves a single contract between an insurer and a policyholder for the benefit of a specific group.14

#### **1\. General Legal Framework**

The group policyholder is typically an employer, a labor union, or an association. The policyholder is the one who holds the "Master Policy".14 In Texas, a group cannot be formed solely for the purpose of obtaining insurance; it must have a "bona fide" purpose outside of insurance.

#### **2\. Eligibility Concepts**

Eligibility for group coverage is determined by the group policyholder. For employer groups, the policy may cover active employees, retired employees, and their dependents.14 A critical Texas rule for eligibility in state continuation is that an individual must have been continuously covered by the group policy for at least three months prior to the termination of their employment.7

#### **3\. Certificate Concepts**

Unlike individual insurance where the insured receives the actual policy, members of a group receive a "Certificate of Insurance".17 This document summarizes the benefits, the coverage provided, and the rights of the insured member under the master policy. The certificate is evidence of coverage but is not the contract itself.

#### **4\. Blanket Accident and Health**

Blanket insurance is a unique form of group coverage for changing groups of people where individual names are not required on the policy.14 Common examples include students at a school, passengers on a common carrier (like an airline), or members of a volunteer fire department. Because the members of the group change frequently, individual certificates are often not issued.

### **D. Medicare Supplement Insurance**

Medicare Supplement (Medigap) policies are private insurance plans designed to "fill the gaps" in Original Medicare (Part A and Part B). In Texas, these are regulated under TIC Chapter 1652 and TAC Title 28, Subchapter T.19

#### **1\. Texas Regulatory Framework**

All Medigap policies sold in Texas must be standardized into lettered plans (A through N). Plan A is the "core" plan, and any insurer that sells Medigap in Texas must at least offer Plan A.19 Standardization ensures that Plan G from Company X has the exact same benefits as Plan G from Company Y, allowing consumers to shop based on price and service.

#### **2\. Standardization and Consumer Protections**

* **30-Day Free Look:** Every Medicare Supplement policy in Texas must include a 30-day "free look" period. If the insured is dissatisfied for any reason, they can return the policy within 30 days of delivery for a full refund of all premiums paid.21  
* **Guaranteed Renewable:** All Medigap policies in Texas must be guaranteed renewable. The insurer cannot cancel the policy except for non-payment of premium or material misrepresentation.10  
* **Pre-existing Conditions:** Insurers cannot exclude coverage for pre-existing conditions for more than 6 months after the policy's effective date.13

#### **3\. Replacement and Disclosure Concepts**

When an agent is replacing a client's existing Medigap policy, they must provide a "Notice Regarding Replacement" to ensure the client understands any changes in coverage or new waiting periods.23 Additionally, agents must deliver a "Shopper’s Guide" and an "Outline of Coverage" at the time of application.23

### **E. Long-Term Care Insurance**

Long-Term Care (LTC) insurance provides coverage for individuals who need help with daily activities due to chronic illness or disability. This is regulated by TIC Chapter 1651 and TAC Title 28, Subchapter Y.24

#### **1\. Texas Regulatory Framework**

LTC policies in Texas must be at least guaranteed renewable.9 Benefits are triggered when the insured is unable to perform a certain number of "Activities of Daily Living" (ADLs)—such as bathing, eating, or dressing—or if they suffer from a cognitive impairment.25

#### **2\. Required Disclosures**

* **Outline of Coverage:** This must be a freestanding document delivered to the applicant at the time of initial solicitation.8 It provides a summary of the policy's benefits and limitations.  
* **Shopper’s Guide:** The agent must provide the NAIC Long-Term Care Shopper’s Guide to the applicant prior to the presentation of an application.27

#### **3\. Suitability and Replacement**

In Texas, agents must make a reasonable effort to determine if an LTC policy is suitable for the applicant's needs and financial circumstances. If a policy is being replaced, the agent must ensure the replacement is in the best interest of the consumer and provide a replacement notice. LTC policies also feature a 30-day free look period.8

### **F. Other Texas Accident and Health Rules**

#### **1\. Continuation and Conversion Rights**

Texas has specific rules for continuing group health coverage, often called "Mini-COBRA" for smaller employers.7

* **State Continuation (TIC 1251.251):** For employers with 2 to 19 employees who are not subject to federal COBRA, Texas law allows terminated employees to continue their group coverage for up to 9 months.7  
* **Eligibility:** To qualify, the employee must have been continuously covered by the group policy for at least 3 months prior to the qualifying event (e.g., termination).7  
* **Election Window:** The employee has 60 days from the date of the notice to elect continuation coverage.7

#### **2\. Consumer Protections**

Texas law prohibits an insurer from canceling or refusing to renew a health benefit plan solely because the insured has been diagnosed with or has a history of HIV or AIDS.16 Furthermore, if a policy covers a family, it must provide that the death of the primary policyholder or a divorce does not automatically terminate the coverage for the spouse or children, provided they follow the required continuation procedures.16

## **SECTION 3: TEXAS-LAW BREAKDOWN BY CITATION**

### **TIC 1201.051-.260: Individual Accident and Health Policies**

This core section of the Texas Insurance Code establishes the foundation for individual health insurance regulation.2 It mandates the twelve uniform policy provisions that are the primary focus of the health exam.

* **Controlling Rule:** Every individual health policy must include specific, consumer-friendly provisions regarding grace periods, reinstatement, and claims handling.6  
* **Exam Significance:** This is the most heavily tested portion of the Texas health statutes. Candidates must know the specific day-counts (e.g., 20 days for notice, 15 days for forms).6  
* **Takeaway:** Individual health policies in Texas are standardized to protect consumers from unfair claim practices and sudden lapses in coverage.

### **TIC 1202.001-.155: Cancellation and Continuation**

This chapter addresses the insurer's rights and limitations regarding the termination of health policies.16

* **Controlling Rule:** Insurers cannot cancel policies for specific health conditions like HIV/AIDS and must provide 90 days' notice if they intend to discontinue a specific type of health plan for all enrollees.16  
* **Exam Significance:** Often tested in the context of "uniform termination" and non-discrimination rules.  
* **Takeaway:** Health coverage cannot be cancelled solely due to a diagnosis of a serious illness; broad plan terminations require significant advance notice.

### **TIC 1251.001-.255: Group and Blanket Accident and Health**

This section outlines the legal structure for group insurance, defining eligible groups and the responsibilities of the policyholder.14

* **Controlling Rule:** Group policies are issued to the employer (Master Policy), and employees receive a Certificate of Insurance.17  
* **Exam Significance:** Focuses on the distinction between employer-held policies and individual-held certificates.  
* **Takeaway:** In a group setting, the contract is between the insurer and the employer, while the employee is the beneficiary of that contract.

### **TIC 1251.251-.260: Texas Mini-COBRA**

These statutes provide continuation rights for employees of small Texas businesses.7

* **Controlling Rule:** Employees at firms with 2-19 people have the right to 9 months of continuation coverage if they have 3 months of prior history with the group.7  
* **Exam Significance:** A high-yield "Texas-only" topic that differentiates state law from federal COBRA.  
* **Takeaway:** Texas law fills the gap left by federal law for small business employees, offering 9 months of protection.

### **TIC 1652.001-.202: Medicare Supplement Benefit Plans**

This chapter governs the sale and standardization of Medigap policies in Texas.19

* **Controlling Rule:** Medigap policies must be standardized and must offer a 30-day free look period.19  
* **Exam Significance:** Frequently tested on the 30-day free look and the mandatory offering of Plan A.  
* **Takeaway:** Medicare Supplement insurance is highly regulated to prevent confusion among seniors and ensure plan comparability.

### **TIC 1651.001-.060: Long-Term Care Insurance**

This section establishes the framework for LTC policies, including triggers for benefits and consumer disclosures.24

* **Controlling Rule:** LTC policies must be guaranteed renewable and provide clear "Outline of Coverage" documents.8  
* **Exam Significance:** Focuses on ADL triggers and the timing of the Shopper's Guide.  
* **Takeaway:** LTC insurance in Texas prioritizes "guaranteed renewability" and full disclosure before an application is signed.

## **SECTION 5: EXAM-FOCUSED TAKEAWAYS**

### **What I Absolutely Need to Know Cold (High Priority)**

* **20-15-90 Rule:** 20 days for notice of claim, 15 days for insurer to send forms, 90 days for proof of loss.3  
* **7-10-31 Grace Period:** 7 days for weekly premium, 10 days for monthly, 31 days for all others.4  
* **45-Day Reinstatement:** Reinstatement is automatic if the insurer doesn't deny it within 45 days of the conditional receipt.12  
* **30-Day Free Look:** Mandatory for both Medicare Supplement and Long-Term Care in Texas.8  
* **Mini-COBRA (9 Months):** Texas state continuation lasts 9 months for employers with 2-19 employees.7  
* **ADLs for LTC:** Benefit triggers are based on "Activities of Daily Living" or cognitive impairment.25  
* **Entire Contract:** Only an executive officer can change the policy; an agent cannot.3

### **What is Secondary but Still Testable**

* **Incontestability Period (2 Years):** After two years, only fraudulent misstatements can be used to void a health policy.3  
* **Legal Action Window:** At least 60 days after proof of loss, but no later than 3 years.3  
* **Medigap Plan A:** Must be offered by any company selling Medigap in Texas.19  
* **LTC Shopper's Guide:** Must be delivered *before* the application is presented.27  
* **Facility of Payment Clause:** Optional provision allowing insurer to pay up to $1,000 for medical/funeral costs.14

### **What is Low Priority**

* The specific numeric legislative act identifiers (e.g., "Acts 2003, 78th Leg").  
* The history of the Texas Manufactured Housing Standards Act (often appears in search results for TIC 1201 but is unrelated to health insurance).31

### **Most Likely Wrong-Answer Traps**

* **Trap:** An agent can waive a policy provision if it's in writing. **Fact:** Only an executive officer can change the policy.3  
* **Trap:** Reinstatement covers everything immediately. **Fact:** Sickness has a 10-day waiting period; only accidents are covered immediately.12  
* **Trap:** All health policies have a 31-day grace period. **Fact:** Weekly (7) and Monthly (10) are different.4  
* **Trap:** The Shopper's Guide is delivered when the policy is delivered. **Fact:** It must be delivered *at the time of solicitation*.8

### **Memory Hooks or Mnemonics**

* **"20-15-90":** The Claim Timeline (Notice-Forms-Proof).  
* **"7-10-31":** The Grace Period (Weekly-Monthly-Other).  
* **"45-10":** Reinstatement (45 days to approve, 10-day wait for sickness).  
* **"60-3":** Legal Action (Wait 60 days, finish by 3 years).

## **SECTION 6: OFFICIAL RULES AND AUTHORITIES**

### **1\. Texas Insurance Code (TIC) Chapter 1201**

This is the "Bible" for individual accident and health provisions in Texas. It ensures standardization across the industry.

* **Citation:** TIC 1201.051-.260.2  
* **Testability:** Directly testable. Expect multiple questions on the specific uniform provisions and their timelines.

### **2\. Texas Insurance Code (TIC) Chapter 1251**

This governs group and blanket insurance and is the source of the Texas "Mini-COBRA" continuation rules.

* **Citation:** TIC 1251.001-.255.14  
* **Testability:** Directly testable, especially regarding the 9-month continuation rule and eligibility.7

### **3\. Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Title 28, Subchapter Y**

This contains the specific standards for Long-Term Care insurance as promulgated by the TDI.

* **Citation:** 28 TAC § 3.3801-3.3874.24  
* **Testability:** Likely background support, but specific rules like the "Outline of Coverage" delivery are directly testable.8

### **4\. Texas Administrative Code (TAC) Title 28, Subchapter T**

This regulates Medicare Supplement insurance standardization and marketing.

* **Citation:** 28 TAC § 3.3301-3.3325.19  
* **Testability:** Directly testable regarding the 30-day free look and replacement disclosures.21

### **5\. TIC Chapter 1202**

Governs the cancellation and continuation of health benefit plans.

* **Citation:** TIC 1202.001-.155.16  
* **Testability:** Primarily tested on the prohibition of cancellation for HIV/AIDS and the 90-day non-renewal notice.16

## **SECTION 7: FINAL RESEARCH MEMO**

### **1\. Top 20 Facts to Memorize**

1. Individual health grace periods are 7 (weekly), 10 (monthly), or 31 (other) days.4  
2. The "Entire Contract" includes the policy, the application, and any riders.2  
3. Agents have zero authority to alter the terms of an insurance contract.3  
4. Reinstatement applications are deemed approved if not rejected in 45 days.12  
5. Reinstated policies cover sickness starting 10 days after reinstatement.12  
6. Notice of Claim must be given to the insurer within 20 days of the loss.6  
7. The insurer has 15 days to provide claim forms once notified of a loss.3  
8. Proof of Loss must be submitted within 90 days of the date of loss.6  
9. Legal action cannot be filed until 60 days after proof of loss is provided.3  
10. The time limit to file a lawsuit is 3 years from the proof of loss deadline.3  
11. The incontestability period for health insurance misstatements is 2 years.3  
12. Misstatement of age results in adjusted benefits, not policy cancellation.3  
13. Medicare Supplement policies feature a mandatory 30-day free look.21  
14. Plan A is the "core" Medigap plan and must be offered by all Medigap insurers.19  
15. Texas "Mini-COBRA" state continuation lasts for exactly 9 months.7  
16. Small employers (2-19 employees) are subject to Texas state continuation.7  
17. LTC policies must be at least guaranteed renewable.9  
18. Benefit triggers for LTC include the inability to perform 2+ ADLs.25  
19. The LTC Shopper’s Guide must be delivered prior to an application being taken.27  
20. HIV/AIDS diagnosis cannot be the sole grounds for policy cancellation in Texas.16

### **2\. Top 10 Distinctions to Avoid Mixing Up**

1. **Notice of Claim (20 days) vs. Proof of Loss (90 days):** Telling them vs. Proving it.6  
2. **Federal COBRA (18+ mo) vs. Texas Mini-COBRA (9 mo):** Employer size 20+ vs. 2-19.7  
3. **Guaranteed Renewable vs. Noncancelable:** Can premiums go up? Yes vs. No.10  
4. **Master Policy vs. Certificate:** Who holds the document? Employer vs. Employee.17  
5. **Reinstatement Accidents vs. Sickness:** Coverage starts immediately vs. 10 days.12  
6. **Medigap Free Look (30 days) vs. Ordinary Life (varies):** Seniors get more time.21  
7. **Facility of Payment ($1,000) vs. Total Benefit:** Small medical payment vs. Full claim.14  
8. **Conditionally Renewable vs. Optionally Renewable:** Insurer needs a reason vs. Insurer just decides.15  
9. **Time of Payment (When) vs. Payment of Claims (Who):** Schedule vs. Recipient.6  
10. **Plan A (Medigap) vs. Part A (Medicare):** Private supplement vs. Government hospital care.19

### **3\. Top 10 Likely Multiple-Choice Traps**

1. Suggesting the grace period for a monthly premium is 31 days (It's 10).4  
2. Claiming an agent can accept an oral notice of claim (It must be written).6  
3. Saying an insurer can deny a claim for a mistake in the application 5 years later (Max is 2).3  
4. Indicating an LTC policy can be cancelled if the insured gets Alzheimer's (Prohibited).10  
5. Suggesting the Shopper's Guide and Outline of Coverage are the same thing (They are separate).8  
6. Arguing that state continuation applies to an employee with 1 month of tenure (Needs 3).7  
7. Stating the insurer pays for the autopsy (Correct, they do—don't pick "the estate pays").3  
8. Asserting that change of beneficiary requires the current beneficiary's signature (Only if irrevocable).3  
9. Claiming the 30-day free look applies to all health policies (It's specifically for Medigap/LTC).21  
10. Saying misstatement of age always voids the policy (It usually just adjusts the benefit).3

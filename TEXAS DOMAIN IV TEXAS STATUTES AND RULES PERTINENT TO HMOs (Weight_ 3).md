# **Comprehensive Analysis of Texas Statutes and Rules Pertinent to Health Maintenance Organizations**

## **SECTION 1: DOMAIN SNAPSHOT**

The regulatory environment governing Health Maintenance Organizations (HMOs) in Texas represents one of the most specialized and technically dense segments of the General Lines Life, Accident, and Health Agent exam. While this domain, officially designated as Texas State-Specific Domain IV, is assigned a weight of only three questions, its significance extends far beyond its quantitative representation on the test. This segment serves as the critical legal boundary between traditional indemnity insurance, where the insurer acts as a financier of risk, and managed care, where the entity functions as an active participant in the delivery of healthcare services.1 For a Texas agent, mastering this domain requires a shift in perspective from understanding "coverage" to understanding "access" and "delivery systems".3  
The primary focus of this domain is the Texas Health Maintenance Organization Act, codified under Chapter 843 of the Texas Insurance Code (TIC). This act is supplemented by Title 28, Chapter 11 of the Texas Administrative Code (TAC), which provides the granular, operational requirements that HMOs must satisfy to maintain their Certificate of Authority (COA).5 The domain is structurally divided into five core pillars: the fundamental legal architecture of an HMO, the contractual requirements of the Evidence of Coverage (EOC), mandatory consumer disclosures, the rigorous framework for grievances and appeals, and the overarching regulatory oversight maintained by the Texas Department of Insurance (TDI).8  
High-yield subtopics within this domain are concentrated in areas where Texas law imposes specific, non-negotiable timelines or distance standards. For example, the "30/75 Mile Rule" for network adequacy and the "5-business-day/30-calendar-day" complaint resolution sequence are frequently tested because they represent black-letter rules with little room for ambiguity.4 Conversely, the subtopics most likely to be confused involve the distinction between "Complaints" and "Adverse Determinations." Students often erroneously apply the standard grievance timelines to medical necessity denials, failing to realize that Adverse Determinations trigger a separate "Independent Review Organization" (IRO) process that operates under its own distinct set of rules and clinical standards.2  
For the purpose of exam preparation, this domain is largely a exercise in memorization of statutory durations and geographical requirements. However, a deeper, applied understanding is necessary to navigate "Exception" style questions, particularly those concerning emergency care and continuity of treatment.1 An elite agent must recognize that in the eyes of Texas law, the HMO is not merely a payor of claims but a guarantor of medical availability. This principle of "availability" is the thread that connects the definition of an HMO to the consumer protections and access standards that define the Texas regulatory landscape.3

## **SECTION 2: DEEP EXPLANATION BY SUBTOPIC**

## **Definition and Basic Structure of an HMO**

The Texas Health Maintenance Organization Act defines an HMO not as an insurance company in the traditional sense, but as an entity that provides or arranges for health care services on a prepaid basis.2 This "prepaid" nature is the fundamental distinction between an HMO and indemnity-style coverage. In an indemnity model, the insurer is responsible for reimbursing the insured for financial losses incurred due to illness or injury. In contrast, the Texas HMO is responsible for the actual delivery of the service itself, often through a mechanism known as capitation.2 Capitation represents a method of compensating a physician or provider for arranging or providing a set of covered services to enrollees for a specified period, based on a predetermined payment per enrollee rather than the quantity of services rendered.2  
The structure of an HMO is inherently network-based. Under Texas law, an HMO must maintain a delivery network that is adequate and complies with the requirements for the approval of its application for a Certificate of Authority.11 This means that the HMO is legally obligated to ensure a sufficient number of primary care physicians and specialists are available and accessible within the defined service area.4 This differs significantly from Preferred Provider Organization (PPO) models. While a PPO offers financial incentives to use "preferred" providers, it still allows for out-of-network care at a higher cost-sharing level. A Texas HMO, by definition, generally provides zero coverage for out-of-network care, with the strictly regulated exceptions of emergency care or instances where the HMO’s own network is deficient in a specific specialty.3  
A central figure in the HMO structure is the Primary Care Physician (PCP). Texas law views the PCP as the "gatekeeper" or coordinator of care.3 Enrollees are typically required to select a PCP from the HMO’s list of participating providers. This PCP is then responsible for overseeing the enrollee's general health and, crucially, for providing referrals to specialists within the HMO's network.3 This referral system is a mandatory component of the managed care model, though Texas law carves out specific protections, such as the right for female enrollees to visit an obstetrician or gynecologist without a referral from their PCP.12

| Comparison Factor | Indemnity Insurance | PPO (Preferred Provider) | Texas HMO |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Primary Goal** | Reimbursement of loss | Cost control via discounts | Managing care delivery 2 |
| **Provider Choice** | Unlimited | Restricted but flexible | Network-only (generally) 11 |
| **Gatekeeper** | None | Rarely used | Mandatory PCP selection 3 |
| **Payment Method** | Fee-for-Service | Discounted Fee-for-Service | Capitation/Prepaid 2 |
| **Out-of-Network** | Covered at standard rate | Covered at lower rate | Not covered (except ER) 12 |

The rationale for this rigid structure is to provide a predictable cost environment for both the HMO and the enrollee while maintaining a baseline quality of care. By limiting the network and utilizing capitation, the HMO can control medical inflation and theoretically focus on preventive health services, which are mandatory under Texas basic health care standards.12 For the agent, the most critical takeaway is that the HMO's legal identity is inseparable from its provider network; if the network fails to provide access, the HMO is in violation of the Texas Insurance Code.8

## **Evidence of Coverage**

The Evidence of Coverage (EOC) is the primary contractual document in the Texas HMO ecosystem. It is defined as any certificate, agreement, or contract, including a "blended contract" that provides a combination of indemnity and HMO benefits, which is issued to an enrollee and states the coverage to which the enrollee is entitled.2 In the context of the Texas insurance exam, the EOC is the functional equivalent of a health insurance policy, but it is subject to specialized filing and content requirements under 28 TAC Chapter 11\.5  
Texas law requires that an HMO file its EOC forms, along with any member handbooks or group contracts, for approval by the Texas Department of Insurance prior to issuance.22 This approval process ensures that the document meets "Plain Language" requirements, making it readable and understandable for the average consumer.5 One unique technical aspect of HMO filings is the use of "Matrix Filings." This approach allows an HMO to file various provision "blocks" once and combine them to create multiple different EOC documents, which reduces administrative costs while ensuring that every possible benefit configuration has been pre-approved by state regulators.5  
An EOC must contain several non-negotiable elements to be compliant with Texas law. The "Face Page" of the document must clearly display the HMO's name, address, website, and a toll-free number for information and complaints.20 Furthermore, every EOC must include a "Schedule of Benefits" that lists the services available, associated copayments or deductibles, and a description of where and how to obtain care.21 The EOC also serves as the legal source for defining the "Service Area"—the specific geographic region where the HMO is authorized to provide care and where its network adequacy is measured.4  
A particularly testable consumer protection found in the EOC is the "10-Day Right to Examine," or the "Free Look" period. Under Texas law, an enrollee has ten days from the date of delivery to review the EOC. If the enrollee is unsatisfied for any reason, they may return the document to the HMO or the agent and receive a full refund of any premiums paid, provided that no services have been rendered during that window.21 This protection underscores the state’s commitment to ensuring enrollees are not trapped in a managed care contract they do not fully understand.

## **Required Disclosures and Consumer Protections**

Texas law imposes extensive disclosure obligations on HMOs to mitigate the inherent restrictions of a network-based care model. Under TIC 843.201, an HMO must provide an accurate written or electronic description of health care plan terms and conditions to both prospective and current enrollees.20 This disclosure must include a current list of physicians and providers, clearly delineating any "limited provider networks" or "delegated networks" within the plan.27 The goal of this disclosure is to allow consumers to make informed comparisons and decisions before committing to a specific HMO.20  
The "Access and Availability" standards are perhaps the most rigorous consumer protections in the Texas HMO framework. These are not merely suggestions but codified distance and time requirements found in 28 TAC 11.1607.4 For basic health care services, an HMO must ensure that the distance from any point in its service area to a point of service is no greater than 30 miles for primary care and general hospital services.4 For specialty care, special hospitals, and single health care service plans, the maximum travel distance is 75 miles.4 These mileage standards are the bedrock of network adequacy; if an HMO cannot meet them, it must file a formal "Access Plan" with the TDI to explain how it will facilitate care for enrollees in those "gap" areas.4  
In addition to distance, Texas law mandates "Wait-Time" standards for appointments. These standards reflect the clinical urgency of the care required:

* **Urgent Care:** Must be available within 24 hours for medical, dental, and behavioral health conditions.4  
* **Routine Care:** Must be available within three weeks for medical conditions, two weeks for behavioral health, and eight weeks for dental.4  
* **Preventive Care:** Must be available within two months for children and three months for adults.4

The "Prudent Layperson" standard for emergency care is another foundational protection. Texas defines "Emergency Care" as services provided in an emergency facility to evaluate and stabilize medical conditions of a recent onset and severity, including severe pain, that would lead a "prudent layperson" possessing average health knowledge to believe that failure to seek immediate care would result in serious jeopardy to their health, bodily functions, or, in the case of a pregnant woman, the health of the fetus.2 Crucially, an HMO cannot require "prior authorization" for emergency services and must cover such care regardless of whether the facility is in-network or out-of-network.3 Once the enrollee is stabilized, the HMO may arrange for a transfer to a network facility, but the initial emergency intervention must be covered.3  
"Continuity of Care" is a specialized protection designed for enrollees whose provider is terminated from the HMO network. Under TIC 843.362, if an HMO terminates a contract with a provider (except for reasons of medical competence or professional behavior), the HMO is obligated to continue reimbursing that provider for ongoing treatment of enrollees with "Special Circumstances".16 A special circumstance includes conditions where discontinuing care could cause harm, such as a terminal illness, an acute condition, or a pregnancy past the 24th week.31 For pregnancy, the obligation to continue reimbursement extends through delivery, immediate postpartum care, and a follow-up checkup within six weeks of delivery.31 For terminal illness, the obligation can extend up to nine months.31

## **Complaint and Grievance Procedures**

The Texas HMO complaint and grievance framework is designed to provide enrollees with a clear, fast, and structured path to resolve disputes without immediate litigation. A "Complaint" is defined as any dissatisfaction expressed orally or in writing regarding any aspect of the HMO’s operation, excluding misunderstandings that are resolved promptly to the enrollee’s satisfaction.2 This definition is intentionally broad, covering everything from plan administration and provider behavior to disenrollment decisions.2  
Texas law mandates a "Two-Step" internal resolution process for complaints:

1. **Initial Complaint and Resolution:** When a complaint is filed, the HMO has five business days to send an acknowledgment letter.10 This letter must include a description of the complaint procedures and a one-page form if the complaint was made orally.10 The HMO must then investigate and resolve the complaint within 30 calendar days of receiving it.10  
2. **Appeal to a Complaint Appeal Panel:** If the enrollee is not satisfied with the initial resolution, they have the right to an appeal.8 The HMO must acknowledge the appeal within five business days and resolve it within 30 calendar days.13 The appeal is heard by a "Complaint Appeal Panel," which is uniquely composed of an equal number of HMO staff, physicians/providers, and enrollees.37 To ensure impartiality, the enrollee members of the panel cannot be employees of the HMO, and no panel member can have been involved in the original disputed decision.37

Special provisions exist for emergencies. If a complaint involves an ongoing emergency or a denial of continued hospitalization, the HMO must resolve the matter in accordance with the medical immediacy of the case, but no later than one business day after receiving the complaint.10  
It is vital for an agent to distinguish between a "Complaint" and an "Adverse Determination." An Adverse Determination is a decision by the HMO or its utilization review agent that a service is not "medically necessary" or "appropriate".2 While complaints are handled via the internal appeal panel described above, Adverse Determinations are subject to clinical review and, ultimately, can be appealed to an Independent Review Organization (IRO).4 The IRO is a third-party medical panel, and its decision is legally binding on the HMO.12

## **Texas HMO Regulatory Framework**

The regulatory framework for HMOs in Texas is characterized by strict oversight by the Texas Department of Insurance. No person or entity may operate an HMO in the state without a "Certificate of Authority" (COA).7 Obtaining a COA requires a comprehensive application that includes the applicant's basic organizational documents, a list of responsible persons (officers and directors), a financial statement, a description of the service area, and a description of the complaint procedures to be used.7  
HMOs are held to a higher standard of fiduciary responsibility than many other business entities. Under TIC 843.401, any director, officer, member, or employee of an HMO who receives, collects, disburses, or invests funds in connection with the HMO's activities is responsible for those funds in a fiduciary capacity to the enrollees.8 To ensure financial stability, Texas law imposes minimum net worth requirements and mandates a "Deposit with the Comptroller" to protect enrollees in the event of the HMO’s insolvency.8  
The TDI also regulates "Prohibited Practices" to prevent unfair trade and anti-competitive behavior. An HMO is prohibited from using insurance-related terms (like "insurance" or "casualty") in its name or advertising in a way that would mislead consumers about the nature of its business.8 Furthermore, an HMO may not engage in "Retaliatory Action." Specifically, an HMO cannot terminate or refuse to renew a contract with a physician, provider, or enrollee solely because they filed a complaint or an appeal against the HMO.8  
One of the most critical prohibited practices involves "Financial Inducements." An HMO cannot include in its contracts any provision that offers an incentive or inducement to a physician or provider to provide less than medically necessary health care services to an enrollee.8 This ensures that the capitation model does not compromise patient safety or clinical outcomes.

## **SECTION 3: TEXAS-LAW BREAKDOWN BY CITATION**

The following breakdown translates the core statutory requirements of Chapter 843 into the high-impact takeaways essential for the Texas life and health agent exam.

## **TIC 843.002: Definitions**

This section establishes the legal vocabulary for all HMO operations in Texas. It is the most frequently cited authority for terminology questions on the exam.2

* **Controlling Rule:** Defines "Adverse Determination" (medical necessity denial), "Evidence of Coverage" (the enrollee contract), "Capitation" (prepaid provider fees), and "Basic Health Care Services" (the mandatory core benefits).2  
* **Exam Significance:** Directly tested. Exam writers often use these definitions to construct "Which of the following is TRUE" or "Which of the following is defined as..." questions.  
* **Takeaway:** A Texas HMO is a "delivery system" that provides "basic health care" via "capitation" and documents this through an "EOC".2

## **TIC 843.082: Requirements for Approval of Application**

This section outlines what an HMO must prove to the TDI to get its Certificate of Authority.7

* **Controlling Rule:** The HMO must demonstrate an adequate health care delivery network, a sound financial plan, and a valid complaint system.4  
* **Exam Significance:** Indirectly tested as background for "Network Adequacy" and "Complaints."  
* **Takeaway:** An HMO cannot start operations without proving to the TDI that it has enough doctors and a process to hear grievances.4

## **TIC 843.201: Disclosure of Information**

This section governs the transparency an HMO owes to its members.8

* **Controlling Rule:** HMOs must provide enrollees with a written description of plan terms, including network limitations and a list of participating providers.20  
* **Exam Significance:** Directly tested. Often appears as a question regarding what must be included in the "Member Handbook."  
* **Takeaway:** HMOs must give every enrollee a clear "map" of how to get care and who is in the network before they sign up.20

## **TIC 843.251 \- 843.254: The Complaint System**

These sections provide the specific timelines for resolving member disputes.8

* **Controlling Rule:** HMOs must acknowledge a complaint in 5 business days and resolve it within 30 calendar days.10 Appeals follow the same 5/30 timeline.34  
* **Exam Significance:** Highly testable. These are "number" questions that are prime targets for multiple-choice items.  
* **Takeaway:** Remember "5 for the hello (acknowledgment) and 30 for the goodbye (resolution)".10

## **TIC 843.306: Termination of Provider Participation**

This section protects providers (and by extension, the continuity of care for enrollees).41

* **Controlling Rule:** HMOs must give 90 days’ notice before terminating a provider's contract (unless for cause like fraud) and provide a reason. Providers have a right to an Advisory Review Panel.41  
* **Exam Significance:** Indirectly tested, usually in the context of "Continuity of Care."  
* **Takeaway:** An HMO cannot "fire" a doctor without warning or explanation unless the doctor is a danger to patients.41

## **TIC 843.362: Continuity of Care**

This section protects patients during a provider transition.16

* **Controlling Rule:** HMOs must continue to pay terminated providers for treatment of enrollees with special circumstances (terminal illness or pregnancy) for a limited time.16  
* **Exam Significance:** Highly testable. Specifically, the "24th week of pregnancy" and the "nine-month terminal illness" rules are standard exam questions.  
* **Takeaway:** If a pregnant woman's doctor leaves the network after her 24th week, she gets to keep that doctor through her six-week postpartum checkup.31

## **28 TAC 11.1607: Access and Availability Requirements**

This administrative rule puts the "meat" on the bones of the Insurance Code.4

* **Controlling Rule:** Sets the 30-mile (primary) and 75-mile (specialty) distance standards, and the 24-hour (urgent) and 3-week (routine) wait-time standards.4  
* **Exam Significance:** Extremely high yield. This is the most likely source of numerical data questions in the HMO domain.  
* **Takeaway:** Know the numbers 30, 75, 24, and 3\.4

## **SECTION 5: EXAM-FOCUSED TAKEAWAYS**

## **What an Agent Must Know Cold**

1. **The 30/75 Rule:** Primary Care and General Hospitals must be within 30 miles. Specialists and Specialty Hospitals must be within 75 miles.4  
2. **Complaint Timelines:** 5 business days for acknowledgment; 30 calendar days for a resolution.10  
3. **Emergency Standard:** The "Prudent Layperson" standard is the legal baseline for covering emergency care without prior authorization.2  
4. **Appeal Panel Makeup:** One-third HMO staff, one-third physicians, and one-third enrollees (who are not employees).37  
5. **OB/GYN Access:** Women have a non-negotiable right to see an OB/GYN without a PCP referral.12  
6. **EOC Filing:** Evidence of Coverage forms must be approved by the TDI before they can be used.22  
7. **Free Look:** 10 days to examine the EOC and return it for a full refund.21

## **Secondary but Still Testable**

1. **Continuity Periods:** Pregnancy (24+ weeks) lasts through postpartum checkup (six weeks after birth). Terminal illness lasts up to nine months.31  
2. **Credentialing:** The process of verifying a provider's qualifications, which must be updated at least every three years.44  
3. **Fiduciary Bond:** HMO officers must be bonded to protect against the mishandling of funds.8  
4. **Matrix Filings:** A technical filing method used by HMOs to manage multiple EOC versions efficiently.5  
5. **Access Plans:** What an HMO must file if they cannot meet the 30/75 mile distance standards.4

## **Most Likely Wrong-Answer Traps**

* **Trap: Business vs. Calendar Days.** A test question will offer "5 calendar days" for acknowledgment. The correct answer is **5 business days**.10  
* **Trap: The ER Referral.** A question might suggest that an enrollee must call their PCP or the HMO before going to an out-of-network ER. This is false; emergency care is the exception to the gatekeeper rule.3  
* **Trap: The Employee Enrollee.** A question might state that an HMO can put its own employees on the Complaint Appeal Panel as "enrollee members." This is prohibited; enrollee members must be independent.37  
* **Trap: Hindsight Denials.** A question might describe an HMO denying an ER claim because the final diagnosis wasn't an actual emergency (e.g., chest pain that was just heartburn). Under the **Prudent Layperson** standard, this is an illegal denial.2  
* **Trap: Indemnity Terminology.** A question might refer to an HMO "reimbursing" a patient for care. HMOs *provide or arrange for care*; they do not simply reimburse for it.2

## **SECTION 6: OFFICIAL RULES AND AUTHORITIES**

The following primary sources are the controlling authorities for the Texas HMO domain.

## **Texas Insurance Code Chapter 843 (The HMO Act)**

This is the foundational statute. It establishes the legal definition of an HMO, its powers, its licensing requirements (COA), and the fundamental structure of its complaint and grievance systems.2

* **Relevance:** Directly testable for definitions and operational rules.

## **28 Texas Administrative Code Chapter 11**

These are the administrative rules that clarify the HMO Act. They are particularly important for the "Access and Availability" standards (the 30/75 mile rule) and the "Evidence of Coverage" filing requirements.5

* **Relevance:** Directly testable for the specific numbers (miles and wait times).

## **Texas Insurance Code Chapter 1271**

Governs the specific benefits and coverage requirements that must be included in an Evidence of Coverage.7

* **Relevance:** Background support for EOC content.

## **Texas Insurance Code Section 843.362**

The "Continuity of Care" statute. It defines "Special Circumstances" and the HMO's obligation to maintain care during provider transitions.16

* **Relevance:** Directly testable for the pregnancy and terminal illness timelines.

## **Senate Bill 1264 (Texas 86th Legislature)**

While not a chapter in itself, this bill established the "Surprise Billing" and "Balance Billing" protections that are now integrated into the TIC.18

* **Relevance:** Background for the modern prohibitions on balance billing by out-of-network providers in an HMO setting.

## **SECTION 7: FINAL RESEARCH MEMO**

## **Top 15 Facts to Memorize**

1. Texas HMOs are defined by "prepaid" care and "delivery systems," not just financial risk.2  
2. The Evidence of Coverage (EOC) is the contract, not a "policy".2  
3. The Primary Care Physician (PCP) is the mandatory "gatekeeper".3  
4. PCPs and general hospitals must be within 30 miles of the enrollee.4  
5. Specialists and specialty hospitals must be within 75 miles.4  
6. Urgent care must be available within 24 hours.4  
7. Routine medical care must be available within three weeks.4  
8. HMOs must acknowledge a complaint within 5 business days.10  
9. HMOs must resolve a complaint within 30 calendar days.10  
10. The Complaint Appeal Panel must be 1/3 HMO staff, 1/3 physicians, and 1/3 enrollees.37  
11. Emergency care coverage is based on the "Prudent Layperson" standard.2  
12. No "prior authorization" is allowed for emergency care.3  
13. Pregnant women (past 24 weeks) can keep a terminated doctor through postpartum care.31  
14. Enrollees have a 10-day "Free Look" for their EOC.21  
15. HMOs are prohibited from retaliating against anyone who files a complaint.12

## **Top 10 Distinctions to Avoid Mixing Up**

1. **Complaint vs. Adverse Determination:** One is about service; the other is about medical necessity.2  
2. **Business Days vs. Calendar Days:** 5 business days for acknowledgments; 30 calendar days for resolutions.10  
3. **HMO vs. PPO:** HMOs generally have no out-of-network coverage; PPOs do (at a cost).12  
4. **Matrix Filing vs. Standard Filing:** Matrix allows blocks of text to be pre-approved and combined.5  
5. **30 Miles vs. 75 Miles:** Primary care distance vs. specialist distance.4  
6. **PCP Referral vs. OB/GYN Access:** Referrals are standard, but OB/GYN is an exception.12  
7. **Routine vs. Preventive vs. Urgent:** 3 weeks vs. 2-3 months vs. 24 hours.4  
8. **Internal Appeal vs. Independent Review:** Appeal Panel (service) vs. IRO (medical necessity).8  
9. **Prepaid vs. Indemnity:** Upfront "capitation" vs. reactive "reimbursement".2  
10. **Service Area vs. Network:** The geographic boundary vs. the list of providers inside it.11

## **Top 10 Likely Multiple-Choice Traps**

1. **"Prior Auth is needed for out-of-network ER visits."** (False \- it is never allowed).3  
2. **"HMOs have 30 business days to resolve a complaint."** (False \- it is 30 *calendar* days).10  
3. **"A PPO requires a PCP to act as a gatekeeper."** (False \- this is an HMO requirement).3  
4. **"The EOC must be approved by the agent before use."** (False \- must be approved by the *TDI*).22  
5. **"Continuity of care for terminal illness lasts for 90 days."** (False \- it can last for nine months).31  
6. **"HMOs can use the word 'Insurance' in their name freely."** (False \- they cannot mislead consumers about being an indemnity insurer).8  
7. **"Routine behavioral health must be available in 3 weeks."** (False \- it is 2 weeks; routine medical is 3 weeks).4  
8. **"The 10-day Free Look applies only if no premium was paid."** (False \- it applies regardless, and allows for a refund of paid premium).21  
9. **"The Commissioner sets the fees an HMO pays its doctors."** (False \- the TDI does not regulate fee levels, only access).7  
10. **"An HMO can fire a doctor for helping a patient file an appeal."** (False \- this is prohibited retaliation).16

## **"If You Only Had One Hour to Review" Summary**

Focus your final hour on the "Access and Availability" numbers and the "Complaint Timelines." If you know **30/75 miles**, **24 hours/3 weeks**, and **5 business days/30 calendar days**, you have captured the most likely quantitative questions. Review the **Prudent Layperson** standard for emergency care and the **OB/GYN direct-access rule**, as these are the two most common qualitative "exceptions" tested. Finally, ensure you understand that an **Adverse Determination** goes to an **IRO**, not a Complaint Appeal Panel.
